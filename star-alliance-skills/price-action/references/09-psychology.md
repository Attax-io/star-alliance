# The Trader's Mind & Becoming a Trader

## Why Trading Is Hard

The market is essentially designed to cause traders to do the wrong thing at the wrong time. It is the end result of interactions among thousands of traders across sizes, holding periods, and intents. That environment has evolved to turn our cognitive tools and psychological quirks against us. We are, in effect, set against ourselves.

- **Trading environment defined:** high-risk decisions, made under pressure, with insufficient information.
- **Self-directed disadvantage:** institutional traders have constraints, defined roles, and implicit mentoring that shape behavior. The individual self-directed trader has few limits and a nebulous job description of "making money."
- **Hard warning:** positive thinking, meditation, visualization, and correct psychology can take you only so far. You must have an edge in the market to make money. Psychological tools are not, by themselves, an edge.

## Evolutionary Adaptations

Our cognitive tools are relics of earlier development. They helped us survive prehistory but misfire in modern market life. The best we can do is be aware of these biases and attempt to counteract them — they cannot be fixed.

- **Pattern recognition in randomness:** brains are pattern machines so powerful they create patterns where none exist. The seriousness of this bias cannot be overstated; much of what looks convincing in market data may be random fluctuation.
- **Heuristics:** rule-of-thumb shortcuts that quickly relate new situations to old ones. Useful when built from valid information and carefully trained; destructive when reinforced by the random market environment (e.g., a trader's memory of breakout winners producing a heuristic that is actually incorrect).
- **Shifting blame:** the perception of a great unseen "them" manipulating the market says more about your cognition than the market. Accept the loss as natural, or modify your behavior if the stop was placed incorrectly.
- **Fight-or-flight:** hormones flood the bloodstream, hearing is impaired, vision narrows (tunnel vision is a perceptual reality). On tilt, you are incapable of making good decisions because the chemical balance of your body has been altered.
- **Aggression:** a legitimate trading skill, but must be disciplined and controlled. Hyperaggression is a state of mind that blinds you to everything except anger toward the market.

### On Tilt — Concrete Actions

When on tilt, do not try to push through. Break the destructive cycle before it damages the account.

- Stop trading.
- If short-term, immediately exit any position showing a loss; place breakeven-or-better stops on any other open trades — even if you do not normally use stops, you do now.
- Stand up and move; take a walk outside, perhaps without a coat. Discomfort jars you out of the state.
- Exercise. Go to the gym or do something physical.
- Have a non-trading conversation. Talk to the clerk at Starbucks. Call your mom. Break the cycle.
- Write something with pencil and paper. Even a grocery list works.
- Breathe deeply. If you can slow the body, the mind will follow.
- When cooled down, do one insignificant trade at very small size. Monitor your reactions to that one trade. If you are not ready, you will know.
- If you fail that test, take a longer break — possibly weeks or months. Focus on the damage you will not do, not the lost opportunity.

## Cognitive Biases

Most traders enter the marketplace with no awareness of the handicaps they bring into the arena and are doomed to failure before they begin. The first step in combating these biases is knowing they exist and that they will unavoidably color every interaction with the market.

- **Gambler's fallacy:** after observing deviations in a random process, most feel future deviations in the opposite direction are more likely. Runs and streaks are much more common in random data than most people expect; this single fact is responsible for many faulty intuitions about randomness. Note: markets are not always a flip of a fair coin — regimes (trends, ranges, high/low volatility) modify expectations.
- **Loss-aversion bias:** most people lock in certain gains and gamble on losses. Many traders take small wins and hold losers hoping to break even. With consistent position sizing, there is no such thing as a low-risk trade; every trade has a consistent impact on the bottom line regardless of stop distance, but there are low-probability trades. A tight stop with a high probability of being hit is, over a large sample, a very high-risk stop.
- **Overconfidence:** people consistently have an inflated view of their abilities. Trading attracts competitive, confident people, exacerbating the problem. Special skill in an asset class is irrelevant unless it shows in consistent money over a large sample. We are never as good as we think, and markets are far more random and far less knowable than we wish.
- **Confirmation bias:** overweighting information that reinforces beliefs and ignoring contradictions. Record keeping of trade results and research is critical because this bias distorts memory — you will simply not remember contradictory information, or it will be fuzzy.
- **Anchoring bias:** placing undue weight on one piece of information. A large outlier event can color your entire perspective. Do not spend undue time analyzing big winners or losers; spend time studying the entire set and understanding how those large outcomes fit within all possible trades.
- **Recency bias:** overweighting recent information, especially near the end of a series. Learn to see each event, regardless of where it falls on the timeline, as one of many possible outcomes; avoid attaching too much significance to large events near the end of the series.
- **Hindsight bias:** "coulda, woulda, shoulda." It is far easier to say this after the fact than to act on the information during the trade. Another reason to avoid putting too much emphasis on any one trade's outcome.
- **Illusion of control:** in stressful, competitive tasks with skill cues (choice, familiar outcomes), people cannot distinguish outcomes due to skill or chance. Pushing a button — even one that does nothing — inflates the perceived role of skill. This wraps together overconfidence, attribution, hindsight, and confirmation, suppressing the role of randomness. Test: can you lose deliberately? If you cannot, for an extended period, show results significantly different from those achieved trying to win, then your wins or losses are merely chance. It is better to know you do not have an edge than to waste time and money on a futile exercise.

## The Random Reinforcement Problem

The market is like a crazy teacher who randomly rewards and punishes without pattern. Correct actions can produce losses; bad decisions can produce profits. Statistical edges become valid only over a large sample size; on any one trial, anything can happen.

- Excellent decisions are about as likely to be met with good results as bad results, and poor decisions will also produce a number of winning trades.
- Over a large number of trades, results do tend to trend toward expected value, but the small-sample experience can seem random.
- Solution: evaluate results over a large sample size; use statistics to separate reality from emotional perception. Learn from 20 or 30 trades, not one. Make decisions about changing rules based on 50 trades, not five.

## Emotions: Ego, Hope, and Fear

Our decisions combine reason, intuition, and feeling, in degrees depending on the situation. Successful traders have all found a way to integrate emotions into their trading process; some deny and control them with iron discipline, some seek modes that remove emotion from the decision, some embrace emotionality and build their process around it. In all cases they understand their emotional balance and how to control it within their workflow.

- **Ego (self-image):** we all like to be right and hate to lose. Excessive ego leads traders to fight the market or hold positions at significant loss because they are convinced the market is wrong. It is not possible to make consistent money fighting the market. For most traders, the need to be right is at least as strong as the drive to make money; many find the pain of being wrong greater than the pain of losing money. Avoiding emotional attachment to trading decisions is a key skill; being able to immediately and unemotionally exit a losing trade is a hallmark of mastery. An appropriate view: normal trading losses are not losses at all, but a planned, recurring cost of doing business. Many good traders are wrong far more often than they are right; trading is not about being right or predicting the future. All you can do is identify places where you might have a small edge, put on the trade, and open yourself to the possible outcomes.
- **Hope and fear (Scylla and Charybdis):** we cannot completely conquer them, but we can become aware of our weaknesses and monitor ourselves for susceptibility to errors, intervening before emotional reaction produces a poor decision.
  - Fear: often rooted in misunderstanding; concern should focus on unexpected and uncontrollable loss from outliers, not on normal losses. Flash crashes have shown stable markets can have unprecedented sell-offs.
  - Hope: more dangerous than fear for many traders. It keeps us in winning trades long after the profit potential is gone and reluctant to exit losers, sometimes even at the predetermined stop. One of the distinguishing characteristics of successful traders is an ability to cut losers with minimal emotional attachment.
  - Working solution: for every trade, immediately assume you are wrong. This is your baseline assumption; if you find evidence to the contrary, be pleasantly surprised. This takes all pressure and all hope out of the trade. There is no struggle and no fear because you are expecting to be wrong.

## Intuition

A working definition: intuition is a way of knowing that falls outside rational thought. It works in leaps and bounds, and the intuitive person will often say he or she "just knows" or "feels it is right," and cannot explain much of the process. It is a completely different mode of thought, not a sign of sloppy reasoning.

- **Presence is not edge:** intuition is real but not special. Everyone who interacts with the market quickly develops some degree of intuition, so its presence is not a trading edge. There are far more people with intuitions about the market's movements than there are profitable traders.
- **Cultivation:** intuition is a normal problem-solving skill operating outside consciousness. It cannot be forced but can be cultivated. Two essential elements:
  1. Repeated exposure to consistently structured data.
  2. The right emotional context — an open, receptive, almost playful attitude. Learning should be fun, not only because motivation increases, but because the emotional charge of enjoyment encourages the physical changes needed for skill development. It is very difficult to excel at something unless you love it.
- **Brain rewiring:** high-level skill acquisition depends on physical changes in the brain; the brain rewires itself. Structural differences (e.g., a chess grandmaster versus a typical person) reflect intense training, and this is why skill acquisition does not happen overnight.
- **Theory of Mind (ToM) connection:** research found uninformed traders with no financial-market experience could quickly discern the intentions of informed insiders based on nothing more than information contained in price changes. Trader intuition in market simulations was strongly correlated with broader ToM-based abilities and specifically uncorrelated with mathematical or logical ability; brain scans confirmed activation of areas associated with ToM. Markets with insiders may be deliberately manipulated against the uninformed trader, making ToM a relevant skill.
- **Discretionary traders:** most discretionary trades are placed according to more or less clearly defined rules, not supernatural sense. Good discretionary traders have a passion for understanding how the market really works and are willing to let go of preconceptions immediately if disproven. All good discretionary traders are statistics junkies — some with hard-core statistical methods, others through charts, records, and pencil-and-paper. They are internalizing the patterns of the market.
- **When intuition speaks:** periods of knowing that go beyond statistics. A trade that is working well but that you keep thinking about when the market is closed. Considering trades in three markets and being drawn back to one that is objectively less attractive. These may be messages from beyond conscious grasp, but separating real messages from noise requires enormous emotional balance and experience. New traders have too much emotion and too many conflicts to respond to the still small voice of intuition.
- **Entries vs. exits:** many traders find it constructive to weight intuition more heavily when considering exits from existing trades rather than entries. It is easier to read a market when there is a position and fluctuating P&L involved, but guard against emotional distortions due to fear and greed.
- **Emotion channel conflict:** intuition communicates through bodily sensations ("gut feeling"), and emotions essentially communicate on the same channel, clouding and distorting the message. New traders should actively ignore any intuitive sense and focus on building a rational analytical framework based on the inherent statistical properties of the market. Once emotional charge is gone, intuition becomes more trustworthy — at the same time the trader has been exposed to enough patterns to develop valid intuition.
- **Critical reminders for developing traders:**
  - Intuition is real but not special. Its presence is not an edge.
  - Intuition relies heavily on heuristics, so it must be trained correctly.
  - It will often not be possible to walk through logical steps that fully explain intuitive conclusions.
  - Intuition will sometimes be at odds with logic and reason, creating dissonance the trader must resolve.
  - Intuition is fallible and will sometimes be wrong; all intuitive impressions are subject to rational review and evaluation.
- **Numbers to leave numbers:** true mastery does not come until many hours of work over many years, and until fundamentals have been assimilated on an unconscious level. At the end of this work, intuition grows as a natural result of everything the trader has done to understand the market. Statistical studies, trade experiences, and contemplation of market action are folded into a holistic understanding that is true market intuition.

## Flow

A state of complete absorption characterized by total focus, loss of self, loss of time, and a sense of ecstasy. Common in elite performance regardless of field.

- **Common features:**
  - Completely focused and absorbed in what we are doing.
  - Sense of great inner clarity.
  - Task has clearly defined goals; feedback is immediate and direct.
  - Skills are adequate; sense of easy self-confidence; no worries.
  - Loss of sense of self; we may perceive ourselves becoming one with the task.
  - Loss of sense of time; sometimes a feeling of standing outside of time.
  - Feels great — a dramatic understatement, often described as ecstasy.
  - Activities that produce flow become their own intrinsic motivation.
- **Three preconditions (plus a fourth):**
  1. The activity must have clearly defined objectives and goals.
  2. The task must offer clear and immediate feedback.
  3. The performer must have confidence that skills are matched to the task.
  4. The experience rests on a set of foundational skills assimilated below the level of conscious thought.
- **Challenges vs. skills quadrant:** skills and challenges both low → indifference; challenges increase without skill increase → anxiety; skills increase far beyond challenges → relaxation/boredom; extremely high challenges met by high skills → potential flow. Flow can act as a powerful guide for learning; its presence can be a clue the performer is in an optimal state to learn, grow, and develop new skills.
- **Foundation:** peak performance rests on a strong foundation of completely assimilated skills. The trader has seen enough market action and done enough statistical studies to know what usually happens when a market closes very strongly three days in a row, with a wider range the third day. There is nothing remarkable about any of these skills individually; their deep assimilation into a whole greater than the sum of the parts is one of the hallmarks of mastery.
- **Attention and focus:** we have only so much mental bandwidth; multitasking can cross into distraction. Flow arises when all available cognitive capacity is focused on one single point — a most unusual state of mind. Structure your environment to minimize distractions. Turn off the television, avoid Internet chats, do not check email, sequester yourself physically. Flow cannot be forced. It flourishes in an almost playful environment of open-minded wonder, which is also the environment most conducive to learning and skill development.

## Designing Your Approach

- **Personality fit:** if you don't know who you are, the markets are an expensive place to find out. Pick a trading style that plays to your strengths. Some decide intuitively and quickly; others prefer long debate. Some are naturally aggressive and active; others prefer sedate. Some have serious time constraints; others can sit at the screen every minute. Every piece of methodology and plan must be shaped by who the trader is.
- **Choosing a market:**
  - Many retail traders are drawn to foreign exchange because of the low account balances required — unfortunate, because forex markets tend to be among the most random and least predictable of major markets in many time frames; it is difficult to derive a quantifiable short-term edge in currencies.
  - Futures: even with mini contracts, considerable risk capital is needed; $50,000 is a realistic initial risk allocation for the learning period.
  - Stocks: can start trading using odd lots and limit total risk to small amounts. Takes a long time to lose the learning account in small increments, which is how a beginner should be thinking. Many stock traders come from investing backgrounds, but shorting stocks also needs to quickly become intuitive.
- **Choosing a time frame:** primary question — day trade, swing trade, or invest for the long haul?
  - Day trading advantages: a swing trader might see a few hundred patterns in a year; a day trader will see hundreds in a single week, accelerating pattern assimilation. The more times you apply a statistical edge, the more consistent and larger profits will be; no other time frame offers as many "at bats."
  - Day trading costs and challenges: severe. Transaction costs alone can present an insurmountable barrier (worked example: $250,000 account, 10 trades a day on 1,000 shares, $0.05 spreads, $0.001/share commission, paying spread on 50% of shares → ~$502/day → ~$126,000/year just to cover costs, a 50% annual return just to break even — versus the Medallion Fund averaging just under 40% annually). Psychological demands are extreme; day trading plays on every psychological weakness unless you have the emotional control of a Buddhist monk.
  - Long-term investing: technical patterns retain validity at long horizons, but you will not get to see them work very often; it is difficult to build intuition if making only a few decisions a year.
  - Swing trading: a style targeting one specific swing (usually the next one) while tolerating as little pain as possible. Holding periods vary (hours to months). For many traders, an intermediate time frame of several days to several weeks offers an excellent balance: trading is frequent enough for learning, and most analysis can be done outside of market hours, minimizing decisions made under pressure.
- **Choosing a style:** two key questions — trend follower or countertrend, scalper or holder of bigger moves.
  - Most traders are contrary to the extreme and naturally drawn to fading moves, regarding large sudden moves as mispriced. However, with-trend trades are usually easier and offer better expected value than countertrend setups. The most effective fade traders lie in wait until markets reach ridiculous emotional extremes on their time frame and then pounce — requiring extreme patience, discipline, and maturity. The crowd may be dumb, but trends can go much further than anyone would expect.
  - Trend following: with-trend trades tend to be less transactional; a single successful trend trade can make many multiples of the amount risked. Trend traders accumulate many small losses while they work to find the one market that will trend.
  - Scalping: a large number of very small trades, usually on very short time frames. Scalpers are exposed to very high transaction costs and rarely involved for the big swing. Edges have been significantly eroded in recent years by more efficient market microstructure; the game could well be completely over for the human scalper within the next decade. If you choose to scalp, do so because it is the right answer for your personality, not because you are undisciplined and impatient.
  - Best answer for most traders: middle ground. Trade a handful of patterns, some with-trend and some countertrend. If the only tool you have is a hammer, every problem looks like a nail.
- **Discretionary vs. systematic:**
  - Systematic may suit traders who are not good at handling decision stress and function better in an analytical context far removed from the heat of battle.
  - Hybrid: utilizing a system with intuitive interventions at critical points. Carefully monitor hybrid results against raw system results to ensure you are adding value; many traders intervene based on emotional reactions to risk, and their actions are rarely constructive. Your system must actually work and show profit after all costs in properly done backtests and forward tests.
  - Discretionary warning: many discretionary traders avoid the systematic route because of laziness or lack of quantitative skills — a mistake. Discretionary trading is probably the hardest trading there is; it takes more work, time, analysis, self-reflection, and self-control than any other type. As a discretionary trader, you are the most important element of your system. Any outside stress — illness, financial problems, relationship issues, sickness, or injury — that compromises emotional balance can seriously jeopardize results. Good discretionary traders develop a system to monitor themselves and their emotional state, and reduce risk when not at peak performance.

## The Stages of Trader Development

A rough road map. Individual experiences will vary due to environment, innate ability, mentoring, or pure luck, and depend on the specific market environment in which the trader begins.

- **Pretrader.** Everything is new and difficult. The trader is learning the basics of charting and market structure. No business actually placing trades — the emotional charge will hinder learning and losses are virtually assured. Most should spend about three months in this stage. Papertrading has limited utility, but can acclimate the trader to making decisions under uncertainty.
- **Novice trader.** Still guaranteed to lose, so maxim is "Stay small." Trading for experience and to begin dealing with emotional challenges. Introspection and constant journaling are critical. Stage may last six months to a full year. Mistakes are learning opportunities, so trade size must be kept small enough that they are not too destructive to capital or emotional balance. If journaling and trade review are neglected, mistakes simply become losses. Progress marker: starting to lose money more slowly. The novice is still losing, but losing less often and less consistently.
- **Early competent trader.** The first step toward making money is to stop losing money. A trader whose wins and losses balance out (before commissions) has taken the first steps to competence. Reaching this stage may take a year or more of very hard work. Still losing money every month due to transaction costs.
- **Competent trader.** First stage of real competence: able to cover transaction costs with trading profits. Most begin to develop emotional balance and control; not too attached to results, able to focus more on process. Established routine for market review, execution, and trade review. Journaling still important but many psychological hurdles have been conquered. May take a year and a half to two years. Two years in, a realistic expectation is to finally pay for your transaction costs — not much, but very few individual traders ever survive to this stage.
- **Proficient trader.** Starts making money. Errors and mistakes are far less frequent; when they happen, they are corrected and reviewed, and lessons are quickly assimilated. Stressors have lost most of their emotional charge; trader approaches markets in an open, receptive state. Returns still variable; significant losing periods possible, but able to match or beat appropriate benchmarks for most periods. May well take three to five years for the average trader.
- **Experienced trader.** Difficult to imagine becoming a true veteran without living through a complete bull/bear market cycle — about a decade. Has seen it all and become cognizant of the unknown and unknowable risks. No substitute for experience and seeing events unfold in real time. Even this trader is not immune to losing periods; very proficient traders may stumble in the presence of outside influences such as illness or family issues, but they have the control to know when to scale back operations to limit damage.

## Requirements

The requirements seem minimal — a few thousand dollars and an Internet connection — but this does not mean you can compete effectively, any more than buying a football qualifies you to be in the NFL.

- **Time.** Two meanings:
  - Sufficient, regular time on a consistent schedule. Many repeated regular work sessions beat one marathon session infrequently. Half hour a day, every day, is more rewarding than a single 12-hour weekend session. Part of the reason for the length of the learning curve is that you are physically restructuring your brain; this works most efficiently with repeated, constant stimuli.
  - Realistic duration: most traders are unlikely to find enduring success in less than three years — consistent with mastery in most other fields. Be prepared to endure three years of trading with very limited success, especially gauged by P&L. If you find quick and easy profits (e.g., during the dot-com bubble or the 2007–2008 crisis), this is probably the worst thing that can happen — very few traders who begin in an easy environment have long and successful careers. No trader should make a call on long-term ability in those first three years. If you find you do not love trading as much as you expected, consider it an important lesson and move on. If you find yourself captivated by the process but frustrated by lack of ability, persevere.
- **Capital.** Plan to lose money during the learning curve; this is a normal part of development. Most people do not have realistic expectations about professional trader returns; many believe they will support their lifestyle with $100,000 or less of risk capital. Institutional traders who consistently return 25% per year are very rare — these are the rock stars. Many competent traders beat their benchmarks by only a few percentage points. It is unrealistic to expect consistent returns of hundreds of percent annually. Do not go in expecting to dramatically outperform industry benchmarks.
  - Too many traders start with very small (less than $25,000) accounts and plan not only to be profitable but to pay living expenses very quickly — unrealistic and a formula that virtually assures failure. Plan additional funds for educational expenses, books, and possibly graduate-level coursework.
- **Working in a team.** Some of the best professional traders count teamwork as essential. Most effective when the team shares both trading decisions and P&L. Decisions will have impact on someone else's P&L and vice versa. Easier to avoid reckless or stupid behavior when you know you will be accountable and your mistake will cost someone else money. Teammates develop a sense of each other's rhythm, strengths, and weaknesses and can address behavioral problems before they impact P&L. Camaraderie counteracts the isolation of trading.
  - Caveats: not a relationship to enter into lightly. You will be giving the other person full access to your trading capital. Firm, written contracts are essential. Problematic for developing traders — two developing traders will grow at different rates and have different strengths and weaknesses, with great potential for reinforcement of weaknesses and additional confirmation bias. Possible to work at a distance, but many advantages to working in the same office. Expectations must be clearly defined. Be prepared to be called out on errors.
- **Working with a mentor.** Most traders who achieve success owe much of it to mentor guidance. Many forms: one-on-one instruction, course corrections. Mentors can be teachers (working from the beginning) or coaches (shaping the application of an existing system). Three important issues:
  1. Both parties must be committed; clear understanding of responsibilities.
  2. Question of fit is paramount — style and personality.
  3. Issue of compensation should be understood clearly. Anyone can claim to offer mentoring, but it is difficult for the student to truly evaluate the mentor's skill and ability.
- **Technology — software and hardware.**
  - Hardware: one or more good computers with data backup and fast, reliable Internet.
  - Execution software: easy to use, easy tracking of positions and open orders, excellent accounting support. Pay slightly higher commissions for better software and accounting if needed.
  - Charting software: most execution packages include some charting, but many traders pay for specialized programs. Fast access to data, all relevant data for markets traded, customizable displays. Higher-level programs allow custom indicators and data processing. For futures, understand how your vendor handles rolls and continuous contracts.
  - Record-keeping software: many ways to keep records. Develop your own system. Word processing or database.
  - Microsoft Excel: deserves its own bullet. Invest time learning formulas, references, and macros; learn to use it without touching the mouse. Keyboard shortcuts are literally five times faster. Excel is adequate for many basic analytical and record-keeping tasks.
  - Analytical software: Excel is not the right tool for deep analysis. Some versions have small but persistent calculation errors that can cause serious errors in trading applications, and Excel's management of large data sets is cumbersome. For deep-dive data analysis, learn SPSS, SAS, Stata, or R.

## The Trading and Business Plan

Always think of trading as a business and treat it as such. No one would start a business with insufficient capital, no expansion plan, and no vision.

- **Business plan:** crafts the overarching plan for the trading operation. In the beginning, focuses on development and provides a yardstick for commitment. As trading starts, modified to encompass risk parameters, markets traded, and new directions. Strike a balance between modifying often enough to remain relevant and having a good control document. If the plan is always changing, it is probably not very helpful.
- **Trading plan:** more dynamic and flexible; defines exactly what you will and will not do. Common to revise slightly every month. Should include:
  - Schedule for daily or weekly research and trade review.
  - List of patterns and ideas you will trade.
  - Target activity and risk levels — number of trades per day/week/month allowed; number of positions simultaneously; maximum percentage of capital at risk at any time; how and when to use margin; loss level at which you stop trading for the day/week/month.
  - Clear plan for trade management — all at once or scale in; partial profits; where and how.
  - How often you make trading decisions — mid-day, or create a plan the night before and execute as faithfully as possible.
- **Execution rule:** any evaluation of your performance over any time period is an evaluation of both your plan and how well you followed that plan. Follow the plan as precisely as possible to remove extraneous sources of variation in your returns.

## Executing the Process

The intellectual problems of trading are trivial. The essential elements of market structure and trade management could fit on a single piece of paper. Most traders rapidly assimilate the foundation; the gap is between knowledge and execution. The core skills of competent trading — and skill is not the same as knowledge — are primarily emotional. If traders have not mastered discipline and emotional poise in the face of volatile market movements, their trading will be forever plagued by errors that erode whatever edge they may have had. Only by living through the loop of analysis → decision → capital at risk → consequences, repeatedly, can the trader make progress; regular, repeated exposure is the key.

- **Stay small.** While going through the learning curve, expect to lose money. Keep risk as small as possible on each trade until you have achieved consistency. The gain or loss on any individual trade should be so small that it is insignificant. Granularity matters: equity and forex traders can usually risk as little as $50 per trade; a $25,000 to $50,000 account can lose $50 many times before making a dent. Futures and options traders may find it difficult to risk less than $500 per trade because of contract size. From a purely technical perspective, forex may be very difficult for shorter-term traders, but equities might offer a better learning environment than either futures or options for newer traders. Keep your risk small enough that the win or loss on any individual trade is insignificant until you have shown many months of consistent profitability.
- **Periodic evaluations.** Regularly scheduled time to evaluate trading performance, both recent and longer-term. Natural spot for most traders is end of month; for longer-term traders, end of quarter may suit. Find a balance — frequent enough to make useful corrections, long enough between periods that performance trends are meaningful (even for active traders, a week is too short). At minimum, review equity curve (perhaps without commissions for the developing trader), performance of various categories of trades, and each individual trade. May also make sense to review psychological journals and performance, and most importantly to note any recurring errors. The result should be a written list of things to work on in the next period — often just two or three biggest issues.
- **Dealing with setbacks.** Expect setbacks and failures, especially in the first two years. Triumphs may be promptly followed by dramatic failures; mistakes will appear in areas you thought you had mastered; positive P&L may be quickly erased. Progress is not a straight, upward-sloping line — it comes in jumps interspersed with long, flat plateaus. The sudden spurts of progress are the result of hard work done on the plateaus, but it can be difficult to keep up very hard work for seemingly no reward. Various estimates suggest around 95% of self-directed traders fail by some definitions; if we define success as substantial financial gains, perhaps fewer than one in a thousand really succeed. People who succeed in any field have one thing in common: they did not quit. All that matters is that you get it right once.

## Record Keeping

Good record keeping is an important part of skill development in many disciplines. Create a routine you can commit to on a regular schedule. There is a trade-off between complexity and ease of use; an elaborate system you cannot maintain is useless, but an overly simplistic record will not contain enough information. Decide between paper, electronic, or a combination. Electronic records are easily searchable, easier to read and store, and accessible from anywhere if backed up in the cloud. Even so, there may be some argument for paper: many people find that pencil and paper engages a type of thought different from typing.

- **Journal — about yourself.** A concrete record of successes and failures; careful reflection points out weaknesses and growing edges with immediate impact on the bottom line. Memory is mutable after the fact; highly emotional events color recollection. In the beginning, err on the side of too much detail — record trivial points such as breakfast, hours slept, anything unusual in the morning. Without recording, it is difficult to tease out patterns. Write down everything at the beginning; modify only after seeing how the document will be used.
- **Journal — about the market.** Two aspects:
  - Objective reality of price movements and relationships between markets; over time becomes a source and reference for research into patterns and ideas. A serious commitment to understanding how markets work maintains large backlogs of trading ideas, with many worked on at the same time. Old observations often become relevant years later.
  - Reactions to the market's movements. Adapted to your stage of development. Some reactions will be mistakes you can correct before they become costly. Others will reveal facets of your trading personality that you might never have seen — for instance, that you trade declining markets much better than advancing, or that you excel at positioning in consolidations before breakouts.
- **Daily game plan.** Written plan for each market session. Intraday traders need a detailed and specific plan for each day; long-term investors benefit from writing down important cues and planned reactions over days to weeks. Document serves as a control to build discipline, providing a connection between dispassionate analysis done outside market hours and the actual trading process. Should include at least three sections: observations on the overall market, points to watch on existing positions, and potential new entries. Bullet points are more efficient than narrative. In the beginning, include more detail — markets, specific setups, price levels, related markets, multiple time frames. Later, when setups are mastered and style is developed, the plan can be much shorter and more succinct. Retaining these plans and reviewing periodically gives insight into growth and highlights style drift you might not otherwise be aware of.
- **P&L records.** The most important set of records. Maintain clean, well-categorized, accurate records, and your own set to double-check your broker or clearing firm. Errors happen — positions missing or sized incorrectly or in the wrong account; corporate actions, buy-ins, dividends, rolls in futures, interest rates in forex, currency conversions, and options assignments can all cause issues. Develop an obsessive habit of checking statements after any of these events.
  - Most brokers produce records adequate for accounting and tax, but more resolution is usually needed. Categorize all trades and executions by the setup or pattern that generated the trade. For instance, separate all pullback trades or all failure test trades, then do statistical analysis on that set. Strike a balance between ease of use and detail — three to five main categories of trades corresponding to specific setups and patterns, with optional more information on entry/exit technique. Excel is adequate; many traders find a database program better.
  - Get it right from the beginning. If you change the way you categorize trades, you may have to go back through hundreds or thousands of trades and recategorize them, compromising integrity — you may be inclined to push certain trades into categories that skew metrics. Designate a trade as what you thought it was at the time, not as decisions made weeks or months afterward; beware revisions after the fact.

## Statistical Analysis of Trading Results

Your P&L is not subjective — you are either making or losing money. Correct analysis reveals hidden strengths and weaknesses; you may find you trade certain environments or asset classes better than others, or that a specific pattern is profitable but other trades give back the P&L. Tracking performance over time gives early warning of problems, highlights style drift, and ties performance to market environments.

- **Main questions at all stages:**
  - Are you making money? Does P&L show an edge better than coin-flip or dart-throwing?
  - Is performance dependent on one class of setup more than others? Could eliminating certain setups improve performance?
  - Is there a market, group of markets, or asset class traded better than others?
  - How is performance changing compared to other time periods?
- **Extended questions:** Are you repeating mistakes often enough that eliminating them would have a positive effect? Are you achieving superior risk-adjusted performance? Are there other things hurting your numbers?
- **Quantifying your edge.** Look at summary statistics for net P&L — number of trades, cumulative net P&L, mean of all trades, mean of winners and losers separately, win%, standard deviation, and a p value (one-tailed t-test) for the mean P&L being > 0.
  - The p value tells you the probability of seeing results at least this extreme due to random chance even if the trader did not have an edge. Visual inspection of a cumulative P&L time series is a good companion — ask how hard it is to believe the line ended above zero just due to chance.
  - Plot individual trade P&L (not cumulative) to expose runs of winners and losers. Question the runs: why did they happen? Does the trader's style favor certain market environments? Is there an element of performance psychology? Are the trader themselves responsible for the streaks?
  - Neither reward/risk ratio nor winning percentage is particularly important alone. A class of trades with 81.3% wins can be wiped out by average losses more than three times the size of average wins.
- **Standardizing for risk.** Because traders are often inconsistent in their risk, raw P&L conflates position sizing with actual edge. Standardize each trade's P&L as a percentage of the amount risked: %R = Actual dollar P&L ÷ Initial risk.
  - This can flip the conclusion — a trader whose nominal P&L was unpromising may show a clear statistical edge (high t-test significance on %R) when position sizing is removed. The standardized analysis isolates whether there is an actual edge beneath the sizing decisions.
  - This also enables a pro forma P&L, assuming the trader had risked a consistent percentage of equity on each trade. Compare the pro forma curve to the actual P&L — the gap shows what the edge would have produced under disciplined sizing.
- **Control charts.** Adapted from manufacturing quality control, used as a visual monitor of degradation or change. Caveat: trading results are far more variable than manufacturing applications.
  - **Standard Deviation Control Chart:** decide evaluation period (weekly for many traders; daily for day traders; trade-by-trade also possible). Choose a look-back period (10 to 50 probably most useful; 20 is approximately one trailing calendar month). For each data point, calculate a moving average of the look-back period and the standard deviation. Offset by one period so today's average uses yesterday's data — important because a large value today would influence the average and standard deviation and give an understated view of variation. Plot raw data as bars, the moving average, and bands ±N times the standard deviation (N = 1.5 to 3.0; 2.5 used in the example). Standard deviation bands are a visual representation of variation; usual rules of thumb do not apply because the data is unlikely to be normally distributed.
    - Signals to watch: variation steadily increasing; the average taking a turn down (especially crossing zero); the average recovering but with reduced consistency or fewer large winning days; performance returning to a controlled range.
  - **Range Control Chart:** for each evaluation period, plot the raw data as a bar; choose a look-back period and plot the high and low for the look-back period. Complements the standard deviation chart by clarifying whether expanding variation is positive (more large winning days) or balanced/symmetric.
  - **Win Ratio Control Chart:** plot a moving average of the win ratio over a look-back period. Too much focus on win ratio is counterproductive, but a shift in win ratio can be a harbinger of trouble and may identify problems before they become expensive.
  - Use charts in combination — a careful analysis of multiple charts together can provoke the right questions, even when the answers to most trading problems are not on the chart.

## Summary

Consistency is one of the most important keys to successful trading. It applies to actions and interactions with the market, but also to mundane points like daily schedule, record keeping, analysis, and journal review. Good trading is, to a large degree, boring and predictable. Many traders fail because they are drawn to the markets by what they perceive as a challenging, glamorous adventure and are unable to make the adjustment when confronted with the reality of competent trading. If you are seeking excitement in the market, you will find it — but creating excitement is often at odds with creating profits.

- Consistency matters from at least two perspectives:
  - It is not possible to evaluate the efficacy of any trading system or rule set if you are not applying it with perfect consistency and discipline. If a system is performing poorly and you are also trading it inconsistently, the bottom-line results combine both the system's actual performance and your inconsistent application. Adjusting the system rules based on this combination is illogical, but is an error many developing traders make. Your first task should be to remove degrees of freedom affecting your results. If self-directed, this should be the focus of your early development.
  - For both self-directed and institutional traders, consistency in planned study times, review, and even specific screen setups will foster the rapid assimilation of market patterns on a deep level, leading to the growth of real market sense and flow.

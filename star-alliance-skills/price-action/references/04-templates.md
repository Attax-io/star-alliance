# Practical Trading Templates

## Philosophy of the Templates

The eight patterns are expressions of the underlying balance of buying and selling pressure, not memorized shapes. They are precise, repeatable structures that define where to enter, where to exit, and how to manage risk, but they only have value when read through a complete understanding of market structure, price action, and the psychology that creates them. Three core concepts carry the entire framework: trends tend to continue, support and resistance sometimes hold, and support and resistance sometimes break. Every other setup is a combination of these ideas.

- **Primary trades:** pullback (trend continuation), failure test (support/resistance holding), breakout (support/resistance breaking).
- **Derived trades:** the Anti, buying support inside a pullback, paying a breakout of a pullback, entering in a pre-breakout base, entering on the first pullback after a breakout, and failed breakouts.
- **Trades deliberately excluded:** trades inside neutral ranges, simple buying or selling at support, fading overextended markets. If a setup feels like one of these, it can usually be expressed inside one of the eight structures.

## Failure Test

**Trade type:** trend termination or support/resistance holding.

**Concept.** Markets probe beyond significant levels to hunt stop orders. The probe often lacks real conviction and reverses quickly once stops are triggered. Entering after the failed probe offers an excellent reward-to-risk potential with a clearly defined risk point. This is the classic Wyckoff spring or upthrust, refined with a firm stop and a concrete plan; Sperandeo calls it the "2B."

- **Setup prerequisites:**
  - The market must be overextended or otherwise primed for reversal.
  - The cleanest examples occur in mature, extended trends, often with momentum divergence on the trading time frame.
  - A common variation: an extended consolidation just under resistance, where many participants are positioned for an upside breakout. If the breakout fails, trapped buyers add downside fuel.
- **Trigger:**
  - Short: price trades above a clearly defined resistance area, then immediately reverses on the same or following bar and closes back under the resistance.
  - Long: perfectly symmetrical below support.
  - There may be significant volatility, volume, and activity on the probe, but no real conviction beyond the level. Enter on the close of the bar that closes back inside.
- **Stop:** just outside the most extreme point of the probe beyond the level. On a daily or higher time frame, this is often the high (or low) of the bar preceding the entry bar. The stop is non-negotiable.
- **Failure patterns (warning signs after entry):**
  - Consolidation near the level, especially if the first profit target is not reached, is a bearish sign for the failure test thesis. If price spends two to three days hovering near the level on the wrong side, reduce exposure or exit.
  - The trade should be immediately profitable, ideally moving sharply away within one to three bars on the trading time frame. Hesitation contradicts the rejection reading.
- **Profit target:** take partial profits when profit equals the initial risk. A second portion can be held discretionarily, but consider that the trade may evolve into a longer, complex correction that simply leads to trend continuation. Home-run reversals happen but should not be the baseline expectation.
- **Reentry rule:** if stopped out and price fails a second time on the same or next bar, that second failure is also a valid entry. Plan for it in position sizing so the combined risk does not exceed normal limits.
- **Risk management note:** this is an aggressive countertrend trade with meaningful overnight gap risk. Trade smaller size than other setups, and never add to a losing position.

## Pullback — Buying Support or Shorting Resistance

**Trade type:** trend continuation.

**Concept.** Pullbacks are the core structural feature of trends and the quintessential with-trend trade. Flags, pennants, wedges, and continuation triangles are functionally the same thing; the exact shape is not important. The key is to use the countertrend pause to position in the larger trend at an advantageous price.

- **Setup prerequisites:**
  - The market must be trending. The preceding setup leg should show good momentum and not break the pattern of the trend (no momentum divergence, no abrupt loss of thrust versus prior legs).
  - The setup leg should suggest momentum that must be resolved through a third-leg continuation: impulse, retracement, impulse.
  - At potential trend changes or after range breakouts, there is no established trend, but the setup leg should show a distinct change of character versus the prior environment. This is the territory of the Anti pattern.
  - The pullback itself should show reduced activity (smaller bar ranges), absence of strong countertrend momentum, and clean price behavior. Volume will often be lighter, but reduced activity on the lower time frame and the lack of countertrend conviction are the actual signals.
- **Triggers — two ideal entries:**
  - **Trendline/channel failure then recovery:** draw a trendline (or parallel channel) on the pullback. Enter when the line is penetrated and price immediately recovers back above it within a few bars. The longs who bought the level have been washed out; the snap-back is the real entry. This is often near the low tick of the pullback but requires experience to read in real time.
  - **Buying against sloping support directly:** enter at the support level of the pullback itself. Trade location can be excellent, but stop placement is harder because support slopes. There is significant risk of being the weak trader who gets washed out on a drop below support.
- **Pattern to avoid:** buying against three or more tests of support in a pullback. Successive bounces finding fewer willing buyers (lower highs into support) is usually a precursor to failure, not a high-probability continuation.
- **Stop philosophy:**
  - Place stops at points where the market should not go if the trade is correct, not just below the visible level.
  - Adding a small "jitter" beyond the obvious level helps avoid the cluster of stops that markets tend to seek out.
  - Very tight stops have a higher probability of being hit. When the probability of loss is considered, a slightly wider stop often has a better expected value.
  - Trade size should make every loss equivalent. A "low risk" trade is a sizing problem, not a stop-tightening problem.
  - A good plan allows flexibility: scratch the trade for a small win or loss if developing structure suggests the trend is losing integrity. A time stop is also valid.
- **Profit targets — three useful frames:**
  - **Conservative:** the previous swing pivot (the high of the setup leg for longs, low for shorts). Expect volatility as some participants exit and others look to enter on breakouts beyond the level. Take partial profits here.
  - **Measured move objective (MMO):** project the length of the setup leg (AB) from the end of the pullback (C) to estimate the termination zone of the next leg (CD). This is a zone, not an exact level, and reflects the prevailing volatility rather than any mystical symmetry.
  - **Risk multiples:** 1× or 2× the initial risk. Cross-check against the conservative and MMO levels; if the conservative target is much closer than 1× risk, it usually makes sense to honor the structure.
- **Market-character note:** larger markets (in nominal value) tend to trend better. Some intraday index products rarely trend beyond three legs; some commodities (meats) trend poorly; petroleum, grains, currencies, and interest rates can trend for many clean legs. Adjust expectations accordingly.

## Pullback — Entering on a Lower Time Frame Breakout

**Trade type:** trend continuation blended with breakout.

**Concept.** Times the entry into the pullback by using the momentum of a breakout on a lower time frame so the trade is entered when price is already moving in the intended direction. The trade-off is worse location: larger initial stop, lower reward-to-risk ratio, but stronger momentum confirmation.

- **Setup:** identical to the support/resistance pullback. The same conditions for a trending market apply.
- **Trigger:** a breakout of some structure within the pullback, usually most clearly visible on a lower time frame. A breakout of the previous bar on the trading time frame is hit-or-miss over a large sample because that level may or may not be a meaningful structural point. A series of two to three bars sharing a common high inside a pullback is a more reliable trading-time-frame trigger.
- **Gap risk:** on higher time frames, markets frequently gap open beyond intended entry points. Decide in advance whether to skip, accept the gap, scale in, or wait for the opening range to define.
- **Stop philosophy:**
  - Many traders will want a very tight stop with multiple reentry attempts. The math: small losses add up, transaction costs (including the spread) compound, and the very tight stop does not respect the noise of the market.
  - Preferred: a larger stop true to the geometry of the trading time frame pattern. Tighter stops and wider stops give approximately the same expected payoff for consistent traders; the difference is mostly transaction costs and effort.
  - Position sizing becomes impossible with a tight stop and a reentry plan, because the trader does not know how many attempts the trade will require.
- **Profit target:** the same three frames as the support/resistance pullback apply.

## Complex Pullbacks

**Trade type:** trend continuation.

**Concept.** Complex pullbacks have two distinct countertrend legs and are very common, especially in mature trends. They are the most consistent source of losses for traders who only know how to trade simple pullbacks, but they are also the source of some of the strongest continuation moves, so a complete plan must embrace them.

- **Structure:** a complete ABCD pattern itself. The first leg is a lower time frame countertrend move, the second leg is a retracement in the direction of the higher time frame trend, the third leg is another countertrend move.
- **Two forms:**
  - **Visible:** three clearly separated countertrend legs on the trading time frame.
  - **Hidden:** one or two with-trend candles buried in what looks like a simple pullback on the trading time frame. These with-trend candles usually represent an aborted attempt to resume the primary trend and are a tell that a complex pullback is forming underneath. Candlestick charts highlight this most clearly: most pullback candles color against the prevailing trend, so a single opposing-color candle is the cue.
- **Setup rule for complex pullbacks after climaxes:** simple pullbacks should usually be avoided after buying or selling climaxes, but a complex consolidation in the same area can still offer a valid entry. The market often works off a serious overextension by transitioning into a longer consolidation, which frequently takes complex form. Evaluate the higher time frame for trend integrity rather than dismissing the trade on the trading time frame climax alone.
- **Triggers:** either of the two pullback entries (support play or breakout) work. The support play is often easier in complex pullbacks because the stop is more clearly defined by the termination of the second leg. Both countertrend legs tend to be similar in length, so an AB = CD projection often identifies the termination zone of the second leg.
- **Stop:** more clearly defined than in simple pullbacks. A stop just under the second leg's termination is often a clean risk point, though rare three-or-more-leg variants exist and should not be a focus of the plan.
- **Profit target:** the same frames as simple pullbacks apply, but a stronger move may develop from a complex consolidation. Consider taking less off at the first target and pressing more size for the next leg, balanced against the value of simple, consistent rules.
- **Risk management consideration:** complex pullbacks frequently follow losses on simple pullback attempts. Decide in advance how to handle the situation: a fixed dollar risk per trade, a reduced size after a loss, or a wait-and-see policy. These decisions must be made before they are needed.

## The Anti

**Trade type:** trend termination.

**Concept.** A specific pullback variation that enters the first pullback after a potential trend change. The structure of the trade is a pullback; what distinguishes it is its position in the evolving market structure. Borrowed from Linda Raschke.

- **Setup — two requirements, both required:**
  1. A market structure that justifies contemplating a countertrend trade: loss of momentum on successive thrusts, overextension on the higher time frame, double top or bottom, or a failure test.
  2. A distinct change of character: a countertrend leg (e.g., a downswing in an uptrend) that is much stronger than previous countertrend legs and prints a new momentum low on the trading time frame oscillator. This shows the dominant group has lost control, at least temporarily.
- **Trigger:** the entry is in the first pullback following the strong countertrend leg. The pullback itself is traded like any other pullback. The breakout entry on the lower time frame may have a slightly better edge here than in a normal pullback. A third, looser option exists: enter anywhere in the first pullback with a stop beyond the existing trend extreme. This is sloppy, but on certain time frames or for traders with a higher-level view, it can be appropriate.
- **Reading the pullback:** if the upward bounce after the strong down leg is itself weak, probabilities favor another leg down as a natural reaction. If the bounce shows strength, the prior leg may simply be erased and the original trend may explode to new highs. The character of the pullback is the real signal.
- **Stop:**
  - Conservative: beyond the existing trend extreme. Any price action inside the level is still consolidation consistent with trend change; violation of the level means the reversal attempt has failed and the original trend is favored.
  - Aggressive: closer to the market, using any standard pullback stop. These areas can be more volatile, but the moves, once they develop, are often very clean. Have a plan and execute it consistently.
- **Profit target:**
  - Countertrend trades demand closer targets and tighter management than with-trend trades because of the danger if the old trend reasserts itself.
  - A practical plan takes 33–66% of the position at the first target and holds the rest for a possible new trend.
  - The MMO is critical. The first thrust after the initial pullback often exhausts at the MMO, and price frequently turns back to violate the prior trend extreme, revealing a complex consolidation and trend continuation of the original trend. Watch the area near the MMO closely; if it fails, the trade may be catching a major trend reversal. If it holds with no continued countertrend momentum, be very protective of remaining profits.
- **Indicator note:** the original Raschke definition quantifies momentum on two time frames with a stochastic, where the fast and slow lines slope against each other following a shift in the longer-term direction. The market structure approach captures the same idea through price behavior.

## Breakouts — Entering in the Preceding Base

**Trade type:** support/resistance breaking.

**Concept.** Most support and resistance levels are mirages, but a small minority are extremely important, and breaking them generates a strong move. The best breakouts are preceded by formations that show energy building against the level: classic accumulation, inverted for breakdowns. Entering in the pre-breakout base sidesteps the slippage and volatility of the actual breakout.

- **Identifying a real level:**
  - The level should be visible to all market participants — tested cleanly multiple times, with price unable to penetrate.
  - Small, short-lived failure tests against the level often precede a successful breakout. Larger, more significant penetrations tend to expend the energy behind the eventual breakout.
  - Gaps through important levels are usually "gap and go" signals and should not be skipped. A gap that opens beyond the level often marks a powerful shift in dynamics.
- **Pre-breakout formations that hint at energy building:**
  - **Breakout base:** a tight range near the top of a larger range, showing pressure pinning price at a relatively high level.
  - **Higher lows into resistance:** successive higher lows against the level, showing buyers willing to pay up on each dip. Includes classic cup and handle variations.
  - Coiling of buying pressure against resistance is the operative concept; the chart pattern name is secondary.
- **Trigger — three options, each with trade-offs:**
  1. **Bid near the bottom of the base after the range establishes itself.** Best location. Filled only on declines, so buying against short-term momentum, and may not be filled at all if the market never returns.
  2. **Enter on springs below the base support.** Often coincides with entering on short-term momentum through the recovery. Not every range features springs, and it requires close attention to subtle price detail.
  3. **Enter anywhere in the range.** Loses precision but ensures participation. For a trade with 10 points of upside, a 5-point large range, and a 1.5-point base near the top, a 2-point stop is reasonable, and squeezing the last half-point out of the entry may not be worth the risk of missing the trade.
- **Adverse selection of limit orders:** a trader who bids at the bottom of the base will be filled on every losing trade that drops through the level, but will be priced out of winning trades that never return. Whether the improved location on filled winners compensates for the missed winners is a real, underappreciated question. The market order versus limit order choice is a serious one.
- **Stop — two phases:**
  1. **Before the breakout:** the stop is one level below the bottom of the base, or wider, with active management to exit before that level is reached if developing structure contradicts the trade. Quick drops below base support followed by rapid recoveries are normal noise and often good buying opportunities; consolidation below the support, on the other hand, shifts the probabilities toward a breakdown.
  2. **After the breakout:** the temptation is to move the stop up to just below the breakout level on the logic that a valid breakout should not return. This puts the trader in the position of being the weak hand who gets stopped out on the retest of the breakout level that often precedes the strongest continuations. The stop should remain at the level where the trade is genuinely wrong, not where it can be hit in normal volatility.
- **Profit target:** in the best cases, the trade has just entered a new trend at its inception and there is no reason to give up that location. Take partials at a level approximately equal to the initial risk, then hold the remainder as long as the new trend is intact. After one successful confirming pullback, the trade is simply a trend trade and is managed as such. Ratio-based targets (e.g., the width of the base) tend to be conservative because pre-breakout volatility is often abnormally contracted.
- **Turtle note:** simple channel breakout systems (e.g., 20-day or 55-day highs) often violate the rule that good levels must be visible to all participants. The Turtles' success was driven by systematic trend capture in markets that trended well, not by the breakout levels themselves. Short-term swing trading around real, visible levels is a fundamentally different exercise.

## Breakouts — Entering on the First Pullback Following

**Trade type:** support/resistance breaking.

**Concept.** The first pullback after a successful breakout is a clean way to position in the new trend while avoiding the volatility of the actual breakout and the uncertainty of pre-breakout positioning. It is, in essence, a hybrid that simplifies into a normal pullback trade.

- **Setup:** the market has broken an important level, there is good activity (volume, volatility, conviction) beyond the level proving the break was valid, the initial thrust exhausts itself, and a pullback begins.
- **Trigger:** any of the standard pullback triggers apply.
- **Stop:** the standard pullback stops apply. The breakout level itself is a poor stop, because some of the strongest continuations will dip below it to shake out weak hands before driving higher, with those trapped traders then forced to chase. A pre-breakout reference level (e.g., the high of the pre-breakout base) is more serviceable but rarely needs to be used as an actual stop in this trade. A pullback that drops below the pivot low of the pre-breakout base is much more indicative of a failed breakout than a normal pullback.
- **Profit target:** standard pullback targets apply, with one caveat. Trends from good breakout levels tend to be exceptional. Maintain the discipline of taking partials, but allow room to participate in potentially outsized runs. Most traders will find the best results restricting to first breakout attempts; later attempts after failed breakouts are messier.
- **Time frame note:** a clear breakout level on the trading time frame is usually even clearer on the higher time frame. In some cases, a trading time frame breakout nested near the bottom of a higher time frame pullback effectively becomes a breakout entry into the higher time frame pattern.

## Failed Breakouts

**Trade type:** support/resistance holding.

**Concept.** Most breakouts fail. This is not a fatal flaw of breakout trading, because exceptional reward-to-risk ratios can compensate for lower probability, and prequalification of setups raises the win rate. But the failure side of the pattern deserves serious study.

- **Why the obvious plan fails:** the plan of buying the breakout and flipping short on a violation of the level is a trap. In the common case of a volatile breakout whose pullback dips below the level before continuing, the trader books a loss on the long and is then positioned short against the new uptrend. The market does not respect the level mechanically.
- **Setup — two conditions to read:**
  1. The strength of the move beyond the level. Good breakouts show strong momentum, volume, and interest past the level. A weak probe may itself be a spring or upthrust outside the prior range.
  2. The character of the first reaction. The first reaction should be controlled and proportional to the breakout thrust; it should look like a good pullback in a new trend. If the first reaction is very strong (large counter-trend swing against the breakout), it greatly increases the probability of failure. Failure occurs through the failure of the first pullback, not through the level itself being violated.
- **Trigger:** the breakout failure is a failed pullback. The pullback pattern is the critical building block, and the patterns that suggest pullbacks failing and continuing are the operational vocabulary.
- **Stop:** above the extreme of the initial pullback thrust. The stop is non-negotiable. Do not average, do not trade around the stop. The volatility around breakouts and their failures contains real tail risk that is hard to comprehend and impossible to quantify; one trade wiped out by a stubborn position can erase months of profits.
- **Profit target:** two resolutions to plan for. In longer time frame commodities, failed breakouts are often absorbed into a larger consolidation that eventually breaks out. In shorter time frames, the more common resolution is a sharper collapse as trapped traders unwind. The first partial at a level equal to the initial risk is solid, possibly with more than half off at that target. If the target falls beyond a clear structural level, move the target slightly inside the level.
- **Skill note:** failed breakouts are the most complex and least well defined of the major setups. Newer traders are best advised to learn the other patterns first but should study failure patterns to develop intuition for managing losers. After enough exposure, the patterns of failure become legible; until then, the guidelines above help avoid the more serious mistakes.

## Applying the Templates to the Market Cycle

The eight patterns map directly onto the idealized cycle of market structure and, used together, form a complete analytical methodology.

- **Accumulation:** buy failure tests near the bottom of the range, especially with higher time frame support.
- **Transition to markup:** breakout trades, including entries in the pre-breakout base and on the first pullback after the break.
- **Markup and markdown:** pullback variations are the primary tool, with complex pullbacks taking over as trends mature and the strength of pullbacks providing a real-time read on trend integrity.
- **End of trend:** a final failure test at the trend extreme often marks the high, and the Anti is the ideal structure for catching the turn.
- **Distribution and breakdown:** sell failure tests near the previous trend extreme, and apply the pre-breakout and first-pullback framework in inverted form for breakdowns.

A trader can execute this entire cycle, or can use the trades as signposts to read the health of the market and its transitions between regimes. The skill set is modular: pullback skills appear inside the Anti, inside breakout trades, and inside complex pullbacks, which is why the chapter is organized from simple to complex. Mastery comes from internalizing the sequence rather than memorizing any single setup.

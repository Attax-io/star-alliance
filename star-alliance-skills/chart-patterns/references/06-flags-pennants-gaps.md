# Flags, Pennants, Gaps & Measured Moves

## Flags

**Type:** Short-term continuation; bullish (upward breakout) or bearish (downward breakout); appears within a steep, quick uptrend or downtrend.

**Identification:** A short, sloping rectangle bounded by two parallel trend lines. Formation is short — a few days up to 3 weeks. Must be preceded by a steep, quick price trend (the "flagpole"). Prices usually move against the prevailing trend inside the flag (rise in a downtrend, fall in an uptrend), but horizontal or trend-following slopes also occur. Volume trends downward over the course of the formation. Breakout is a close outside the flag trend line. Formations longer than 3 weeks are better classified as symmetrical triangles, rectangles, or wedges.

**Psychology:** A powerful, fast move leaves traders breathless. Price pauses as participants catch their breath and let the trend reset; volume recedes because the initial burst of energy has dissipated. The brief consolidation shakes out weak hands, and when a fresh wave of buying (or selling) hits on the breakout, the prior trend resumes.

**Measure rule:** Subtract the low at the start of the formation (point B) from the prior minor high (point A — the trend start) to get the price difference. Subtract that difference from the high at the formation end (point C) to get a minimum target. Expect the actual postflag move to fall somewhat short of the preflag move on a price basis (the move after the flag is slightly shorter in price but slightly longer in time).

**Key numbers:** Upward breakout, bull market — average rise 23%, break-even failure rate 4%, throwbacks 43%, meet price target 64%. Upward breakout, bear market — average rise 17%, break-even failure rate 3%, throwbacks 53%, meet price target 55%. Downward breakout, bull market — average decline 16%, break-even failure rate 2%, pullbacks 46%, meet price target 47%. Downward breakout, bear market — average decline 25%, break-even failure rate 0%, pullbacks 44%, meet price target 54%. Tall and wide flags outperform short and narrow ones. Flags with a falling volume trend or light breakout volume perform better. Throwbacks and pullbacks hurt performance. Formation end to breakout averages 2 days. Days to trend high or low averages 17 in all four environments. Over half of flags reach the trend high or low within 2 weeks.

**Trading tactics:** Wait for a close outside the flag boundary before taking a position; place the trade on the breakout, not before. Compute the measure rule target and weigh reward against nearby support/resistance. Place a stop just beyond the nearer resistance/support level. Close out the trade as price approaches the measure rule target, since hit rates are well below 80%. Trade with the prevailing market trend (up breakouts in bull markets, down breakouts in bear markets). Avoid flags longer than 3 weeks. If the price run leading to the flag is short or the formation is not proportional to that run, trade with caution or pass.

## Flags, High and Tight

**Type:** Short-term bullish continuation; appears in a strong uptrend after a stock nearly doubles in less than 2 months.

**Identification:** Requires a substantial, sharp prior price rise of at least ~90% (typically a doubling) in under 2 months. Followed by a consolidation region (the "flag") that is plainly visible near the doubled-price level. The formation is often irregular — not always a clean two-trend-line shape — and frequently slopes downward. Average formation length is 15–17 days. Volume trend should recede for best performance. Trade entry is a close above the flag's trend-line boundary or the highest high in the flag, whichever is lower.

**Psychology:** A stock that doubles in weeks reflects a momentum surge. After such a vertical move, the stock consolidates briefly to absorb gains and let weak holders exit. Once selling pressure fades (falling volume), the trend re-engages and the stock often rockets to new highs. HTFs are not true half-staff patterns — the preflag move is roughly double the postflag move.

**Measure rule:** Measure the price rise leading to the flag (trend start low to flag high). Divide it in half and add that value to the flag's low price. The result is the target.

**Key numbers:** Bull market — average rise 69%, break-even failure rate 0%, throwbacks 54%, meet price target 90%, days to ultimate high 39. Bear market — average rise 42%, break-even failure rate 0%, throwbacks 65%, meet price target 91%, days to ultimate high 25. The move leading to the flag averages 124% (bull) / 98% (bear) versus the postflag rise of 69% / 42%. Short or narrow patterns outperform tall or wide ones. Falling volume trend and U-shaped volume tend to perform well. Throwbacks hurt performance. Busted-pattern performance not available (no patterns failed by 5%). Half the patterns in a bear market top out within 2 weeks; bull-market patterns take about 3 weeks to top out.

**Trading tactics:** Use a buy stop just above the flag's trend-line boundary (or above the highest high in the flag if the shape is irregular). If price closes below the flag pattern, exit immediately. Once in a position, raise the stop to just below each prior minor low as price makes new highs. Close out quickly as the stock approaches the measure rule target, because the trend ends fast. Trade with the bull-market trend for the highest average rise. Select patterns with breakouts in the middle of the yearly price range. Look for overhead resistance before trading to avoid throwbacks. The HTF is a rocket — quick entry is essential.

## Gaps

**Type:** Continuation; bullish or bearish; appears in any trend environment. Four tracked types: area (common/pattern), breakaway, continuation (measuring/runaway), and exhaustion.

**Identification:** A gap is created when today's low (uptrend) is above the prior day's high, or today's high is below the prior day's low. Distinguish by context and volume:
- **Area gap:** Occurs in congestion/horizontal regions. Closes rapidly (often within a day or two). Volume may spike on gap day but returns to normal quickly. The gap "hooks" closed; no new trend is implied.
- **Breakaway gap:** Marks the start of a new trend, usually after a consolidation breakout. High volume accompanies and continues for several days. New highs (or lows) follow.
- **Continuation (measuring) gap:** Appears mid-trend in a straight-line run. Volume is high but not unusually so. The gap remains open and the trend continues making new extremes.
- **Exhaustion gap:** Appears near the end of a trend. Volume is high (the "last gasp"). The gap is often unusually wide. New highs/lows do not follow; the gap closes within about a week. Commonly follows a continuation gap.
- **Ex-dividend gap:** Caused by dividend distribution; rarely useful for trading.

**Psychology:** A gap reflects a sudden change in the supply/demand balance — overnight news, earnings surprises, or a momentum burst. The character of the gap reveals whether buyers (or sellers) are initiating a new trend, propelling an existing one, or exhausting themselves at the end of one.

**Measure rule (continuation gaps):** Measure from the trend start to the center of the continuation gap. Add (uptrend) or subtract (downtrend) that distance to/from the gap center to project the eventual high or low. Area gaps are too short-lived to be consistently traded. Breakaway and exhaustion gaps do not have a standard measure rule in the text.

**Key numbers:** Area gaps close on average in 3 days regardless of market condition; nearly 90–93% close within 1 week. Breakaway gaps take 61–168 days on average to close; only 1–9% close in 1 week. Continuation gaps take 43–98 days on average to close. Exhaustion gaps close in 7–14 days on average; 61–78% close in 1 week. Bear market gaps are larger than bull market gaps across all types. Bull-market gaps perform best near the yearly high; large breakaway gaps outperform small ones. Continuation gaps in upward breakouts appear near the middle of the time trend; in downward breakouts they appear roughly two-thirds of the way to trend end. Best-performing gaps show no consistent yearly-position trend. Area gaps are too short-lived to be consistently profitable to trade.

**Trading tactics:** Confirm gap type with identification guidelines before trading. For breakaway gaps, trade with the trend when high volume is present. For continuation gaps, use the center-of-gap measure rule and prepare to close the trade as the target nears. For exhaustion gaps, close the position when the trend reverses; consider reversing direction to trade the new trend, as violent reversals often follow. Place the stop 0.15 or so beyond the near rim of the gap (lower rim for uptrends, higher rim for downtrends) — gaps provide near-term support or resistance. Trade quickly; be ready to close out at a moment's notice. Select large breakaway gaps over small ones.

## Measured Move Down

**Type:** Intermediate-term bearish reversal; appears after a price peak, often near the end of a prior uptrend.

**Identification:** Three components:
- **First leg:** A straight-line decline from a new high. Avoid rounded declines (saucers/scallops).
- **Corrective phase:** An upward retrace, usually recovering 38%–62% of the first leg's decline. If the retrace nears or exceeds the first leg's high, avoid the formation.
- **Second leg:** Renewed straight-line decline, often with a slope similar to the first leg and usually fitting inside a down-sloping trend channel.

The two legs often run inside a parallel channel, and nested MMDs are common. Avoid formations springing from a flat (horizontal) base, saw-tooth consolidations, or cascades of consecutive MMDs (use the first or second; later ones get progressively weaker). Reject the pattern if the measure rule projects a target at or below zero.

**Psychology:** After a peak, sellers drive prices down sharply. Weak buyers step in and push prices back up — they believe the decline is over. They are wrong: the stock is merely resting, and the new owners are the supply for the second leg down. MMDs vividly tell how much further the decline will run.

**Measure rule:** Compute the height of the first leg (highest high minus lowest low at the start of the corrective phase). Subtract that height from the highest high reached in the corrective phase to get the price target. The second leg is on average 19%–20% shorter than the first leg on a dollar basis, so expect the actual move to fall short of the target. A more conservative target uses one-half of the first-leg height, which is reached 83% (bull) to 93% (bear) of the time.

**Key numbers:** Bull market — change after trend ends +46%, most frequent corrective retrace 40%–60%, average MMD length 153 days, first leg 27% in 61 days, corrective retrace 48% in 30 days, second leg 25% in 62 days, meet price target 35%, meet time target 53%. Bear market — change after trend ends +49%, average MMD length 113 days, first leg 36% in 45 days, corrective retrace 44% in 22 days, second leg 36% in 46 days, meet price target 39%, meet time target 49%. About 87% of bull-market and 81% of bear-market MMDs act as reversals. Larger corrective retraces (over 62%) make a second leg equal to or longer than the first more likely. U-shaped volume favors a longer second leg. After the pattern ends, price rises to or above the start of the corrective phase 80% (bear) to 84% (bull) of the time. Falling volume trend is most common (74%/73%).

**Trading tactics:** Short the stock as soon as the second leg clearly begins. Place a stop about 0.15 above the corrective phase high (corrective phase acts as support/resistance). Use the measure rule for the price target, but expect to fall short; for a more conservative target use half the first-leg height. Cover when price drops to a support zone or approaches the measure rule target. Trade in a bear market for steeper, farther declines. Select patterns with a falling volume trend and U-shaped volume. Use stops to protect shorts from adverse moves because price frequently returns to the corrective phase after the pattern ends.

## Measured Move Up

**Type:** Long-term bullish reversal; appears after a downtrend bottom.

**Identification:** Three components:
- **Downward trend:** A prior decline lasting weeks to over a year, ending near the yearly low.
- **First leg:** A straight-line rise, often fitting inside an up-sloping trend channel. Begins the trend change.
- **Corrective phase:** A downward retrace, usually 40%–60% of the first leg's rise. The retrace is usually proportional to the first leg (large retraces follow large rises). Saw-tooth corrective phases (a series of sharp rises and declines) are common when the prior climb was long — wait for price to rise above the first-leg high before buying in such cases.
- **Second leg:** Renewed rise, loosely following the slope of the first leg, often inside a trend channel. Common to see a falling wedge as the corrective phase.

Avoid formations where the corrective phase retraces more than ~80% of the first leg, where the first leg is choppy or doesn't fit a clear channel, and false breakouts where the retrace is shallow (e.g., 15%).

**Psychology:** After a long decline, buyers return and drive prices up sharply. Profit-taking creates a pause — old holders sell to lock in gains, while new buyers accumulate. Once the supply is absorbed, the rise resumes. The pattern tells the trader that the prior downtrend has truly reversed and how far the new uptrend is likely to run.

**Measure rule:** Compute the height of the first leg (highest high minus lowest low). Add that height to the lowest low in the corrective phase to get the target. This works only 45% (bull) to 56% (bear) of the time. A more reliable method is to add half the first-leg height, which is hit 85% (bull) to ~99% (bear) of the time. Be conservative when setting targets.

**Key numbers:** Bull market — change after trend ends −26%, average MMU length 180 days, first leg 46% in 87 days, corrective retrace 47% in 32 days, second leg 32% in 60 days, meet price target 45%, meet time target 38%. Bear market — change after trend ends −27%, average MMU length 85 days, first leg 39% in 30 days, corrective retrace 50% in 22 days, second leg 35% in 33 days, meet price target 56%, meet time target 56%. The second leg averages about two-thirds the length of the first leg on a percentage basis in a bull market. About 32% (bull) to 23% (bear) of MMUs act as continuations. After the pattern ends, price drops to or into the corrective phase 54% (bear) to 64% (bull) of the time. Rising volume trends favor a longer second leg. Dome-shaped volume is most common but performs worst in a bull market.

**Trading tactics:** Buy once prices leave the corrective phase; one trigger is a close above a down-sloping trend line drawn along the corrective-phase highs. Use the measure rule to set a target, but use the half-height version for a more reliable hit rate. Do not sell too early as price approaches the target — if momentum is strong, hold and let the trend run. If the move stalls below the target, sell. The corrective phase often acts as future support or resistance after the pattern ends — price commonly drops back to that zone. Use the bottom of the corrective phase as a stop-loss price. Consider selling at the top and buying back during the corrective-phase dip (swing-trade approach).

## Pennants

**Type:** Short-term continuation; bullish (upward breakout) or bearish (downward breakout); appears within a steep, quick uptrend or downtrend.

**Identification:** A short triangle bounded by two converging trend lines. Formation is short — a few days up to 3 weeks. Must be preceded by a steep, quick price trend (up or down). Prices typically move against the prevailing trend inside the pennant (rise in a downtrend, fall in an uptrend), but other slopes occur. Volume trends downward over the course of the formation. A breakout is a close outside the pennant trend line. Pennants longer than 3 weeks are better classified as symmetrical triangles or wedges.

**Psychology:** Like flags, pennants form when a fast-moving trend pauses to consolidate. The converging trend lines reflect equilibrium between buyers and sellers in the brief rest. When the balance tips in the direction of the prevailing trend on the breakout, the prior move resumes. Pennants act as half-staff patterns — the trend leading to the pattern is similar in length to the trend after it, though on a price basis the postbreakout move is shorter.

**Measure rule:** Subtract the trend-start low from the intraday high at the start of the pennant to get the trend height. Add that value to the intraday low at the pennant end (the day before breakout) to get the price target. Pennants meet the target only 50%–63% of the time, so be conservative. The postbreakout price move is shorter than the pre-pennant move in all four market/breakout combinations.

**Key numbers:** Upward breakout, bull market — average rise 25%, break-even failure rate 2%, throwbacks 47%, meet price target 60%. Upward breakout, bear market — average rise 21%, break-even failure rate 2%, throwbacks 54%, meet price target 63%. Downward breakout, bull market — average decline 19%, break-even failure rate 4%, pullbacks 31%, meet price target 51%. Downward breakout, bear market — average decline 25%, break-even failure rate 0%, pullbacks 54%, meet price target 50%. Bear-market down breakouts have the lowest failure rates. Tall pennants substantially outperform short ones. Wide pennants outperform narrow ones. Breakout day gaps power prices higher. Throwbacks and pullbacks hurt performance. Days to trend high or low averages 16–22. Formation end to breakout averages 2 days. Over half reach the trend end within 2 weeks. Rising volume trend favors upward breakouts; falling volume trend favors downward breakouts. U-shaped volume in a bull market outperforms dome-shaped.

**Trading tactics:** Pennants are for swing traders, not buy-and-hold. Wait for a close outside the pennant boundary before entry. Compute the measure rule target, but expect to fall short. Place an automatic sell order near the target so the market takes you out near a minor high, and sell on weakness (a lower close or lower high) rather than trying to time the top precisely. Trade with the prevailing market trend. Select pennants with breakouts near the yearly low. Search for overhead resistance or underlying support before trading to reduce the chance of throwback/pullback. Look for breakout-day gaps. Select tall pennants and U-shaped volume (bull market). Close out quickly — most pennants reach trend end within 2 weeks.

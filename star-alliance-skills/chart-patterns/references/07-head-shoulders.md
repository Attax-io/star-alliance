# Head-and-Shoulders Tops & Bottoms

## Head-and-Shoulders Bottoms

**Type:** Short-term bullish reversal; appears at the end of a downtrend (typically short-term, up to 3 months, or following an extended decline).

**Identification:** Three-trough formation with the center trough (head) distinctly below the other two. The left and right shoulders sit at roughly the same price level and are about the same width; sharp left shoulders usually produce sharp right shoulders. The three troughs and the two minor rises between them should be well defined and distinct (not a triple bottom). Volume is usually highest on the left shoulder or head and diminishes on the right shoulder. The neckline is an imaginary line connecting the two rises between the shoulders and head; it can slope up or down. The upward breakout occurs when price closes above a down-sloping neckline, or above the highest high in the pattern for up-sloping necklines. Breakout volume is often high but need not be.

**Psychology:** A struggle to find the bottom as the smart money accumulates the stock in anticipation of a fundamental change or eventual rise. Investors nibble on the way down, putting a crimp in the slide. Buying demand briefly lifts the stock (left shoulder), then sellers regain control and push it to a new low (head). Recovery is attempted again on receding volume (right shoulder), and only after the right shoulder forms does volume spike upward, confirming demand has overwhelmed supply.

**Measure rule:** Compute the formation height by subtracting the lowest low reached in the head from the neckline, measured vertically. Add the difference to the breakout price where prices pierce the neckline (or, for up-sloping necklines, substitute the highest high between the head and right shoulder for the breakout price). The result is the minimum target price.

**Key numbers:** 672 formations (554 bull / 118 bear). Average rise 38% bull, 30% bear. Break-even failure rate 3% bull, 4% bear. Change after trend ends −31% bull, −33% bear. Throwbacks 45% bull, 51% bear. Percentage meeting price target 74% bull, 58% bear. Busted pattern performance −27% bull, −31% bear. Rises over 45% in 34% of bull-market and 24% of bear-market patterns. Formation end to breakout ~16–17 days. Days to ultimate high 176 bull, 107 bear. Breakout-day gap helps (43% with gap vs. 37% without in bull market). Tall patterns outperform short (41% vs. 36% bull); narrow outperform wide (39% vs. 37% bull). Best: tall and narrow (43% bull). Falling volume trend outperforms rising (39% vs. 36% bull). U-shaped volume (44% bull) outperforms. Light breakout volume wins in bull markets (41%), heavy in bear markets (31%). Down-sloping necklines outperform (42% bull). Higher left shoulder lows outperform (39% bull).

**Trading tactics:** A confident identification allows buying before the neckline pierce for a better fill, but a wrong guess turns the trade bad fast — waiting for a close above the neckline (or right neckline high) is the safe play. Set a stop 0.15 below the lower of the two shoulders (or at the nearest support zone), and raise it as price climbs. Because a throwback occurs roughly half the time, a missed breakout often gives a second chance at the neckline.

## Head-and-Shoulders Bottoms, Complex

**Type:** Short-term bullish reversal; appears at the end of a downtrend, occasionally as the corrective phase of a larger measured move up.

**Identification:** A head-and-shoulders bottom with multiple shoulders, multiple heads, or (rarely) both. The head is lower than the shoulders but generally not by very much. Shoulders mirror each other in price and time-distance about the head, and the two outer shoulders are near the same price level. Volume is higher on the left side than on the corresponding right shoulders, and the overall volume trend recedes; volume is often highest on the head. The neckline is often near horizontal (74% of formations), connecting the highest peak on the left with the highest peak on the right. The upward breakout is a close above a down-sloping neckline, or a close above the highest high in the formation for up-sloping necklines. Throwbacks are common.

**Psychology:** A more drawn-out search for a bottom than the simple HSB. Accumulation is spread across multiple shoulders and heads as supply and demand battle in waves before demand finally wins. The pattern often appears within or alongside a rounding bottom, reinforcing the sense of a slow bullish takeover.

**Measure rule:** Compute the formation height by subtracting the lowest low reached in the head(s) from the neckline, measured vertically. Add the result to the breakout price where prices pierce the neckline. The value is the minimum target price.

**Key numbers:** 366 formations (268 bull / 98 bear). Average rise 39% bull, 31% bear (the bear-market rise is steeper than the bull-market rise). Break-even failure rate 4% bull, 3% bear. Change after trend ends −29% bull, −33% bear. Throwbacks 63% bull, 66% bear; average throwback length ~10–11 days. Percentage meeting price target 74% bull, 49% bear. Busted pattern performance −23% bull, −30% bear. Rises over 45% in 34% bull, 27% bear. Formation end to breakout 20 days bull, 14 days bear. Days to ultimate high 257 bull, 107 bear. Breakout-day gap helps (46% with gap vs. 38% without in bull). Bull market: short and wide patterns perform best (47%); bear market: tall and narrow do best (41%). Rising volume trend outperforms (40% bull, 35% bear). Random volume shape (neither U nor dome) does well (41% bull, 35% bear). Heavy breakout volume outperforms (40% bull, 34% bear). Down-sloping necklines outperform (43% bull, 34% bear). Higher right shoulder lows outperform (40% bull, 32% bear). Volume highest on far left shoulder gives best bull-market rise (44%). Median length 90 days bull, 69 days bear; average formation length 101 days bull, 86 days bear.

**Trading tactics:** Trade the inner head-and-shoulders to get a better entry — buy when price pierces a down-sloping neckline or use the inner right shoulder high as the breakout price. Place a stop 0.15 below the lowest shoulder or head. Because a throwback occurs over 60% of the time, consider adding on the pullback, but wait for prices to finish falling and rebound first, or the pullback can extend.

## Head-and-Shoulders Tops

**Type:** Short-term bearish reversal; appears at the end of an uptrend (short, intermediate, or primary).

**Identification:** Three-peak formation with the center peak (head) taller than the other two, resembling a bust. Shoulders appear at about the same price level and are equidistant in time from the head; symmetry is the key giveaway separating the pattern from any three bumps. Volume is highest on the left shoulder, then the head, with the right shoulder showing the lowest volume of the three peaks. The neckline connects the lows of the two troughs between the peaks; it can slope up (≈52% of formations), down (≈45%), or be horizontal. The downward breakout occurs when price pierces the neckline; pullbacks to the neckline after the breakout are common.

**Psychology:** Smart money accumulates during the prior decline, then sells into the rise as the stock advances — its selling creates the left shoulder top. Momentum buyers push the price back up, often toward the prior peak; smart money sells into the rally, forming the head. A last gasp on weak volume produces the right shoulder, and lack of support causes price to turn down. Traders who recognize the pattern sell long positions or initiate shorts; underlying support near the neckline offers brief respite before the trend resumes.

**Measure rule:** Conventional method: subtract the neckline value from the highest high reached in the head, measured vertically, to get the formation height. Subtract the height from the breakout price (close below the right shoulder low for a down-sloping neckline; close below the neckline for up-sloping). Alternative for steep necklines: compute the height as the difference between the highest high and the lowest low in the higher of the two troughs, then subtract the height from that trough's low to get the target. The alternative boosts the success rate and does not depend on the neckline.

**Key numbers:** 814 formations (640 bull / 174 bear). Average decline 22% bull, 29% bear. Break-even failure rate 4% bull, 1% bear. Change after trend ends +51% bull, +45% bear. Pullbacks 50% bull, 64% bear; average pullback length ~11–12 days. Percentage meeting price target 55% bull, 56% bear. Busted pattern performance 40% bull, 21% bear. Declines over 45% in 5% bull, 13% bear. Formation end to breakout 15 days bull, 11 days bear. Days to ultimate low 62 bull, 41 bear (decline is steeper in bear markets). Tall patterns outperform short (−24% vs. −20% bull); narrow outperform wide (−23% vs. −21% bull); best is tall and narrow (−26% bull). Rising volume trend outperforms (−24% bull, −30% bear). U-shaped volume outperforms (−24% bull, −30% bear). Heavy breakout volume helps marginally. Volume highest on the head outperforms (−23% bull, −30% bear). Up-sloping and horizontal necklines outperform down-sloping (−23% / −24% bull vs. −21% bull). Higher left shoulder highs outperform (−25% bull, −30% bear). Average formation length 62 days bull, 52 days bear.

**Trading tactics:** Wait for confirmation — close below the neckline (or right shoulder low for down-sloping necklines) — before shorting. Place a stop just above the higher of (a) the lower of the two troughs and (b) the neckline. Use a pullback to the neckline as a chance to add to the short or initiate one, but wait for prices to begin falling again, because pullbacks occasionally turn into new highs.

## Head-and-Shoulders Tops, Complex

**Type:** Short-term bearish reversal; appears at the top of an uptrend, occasionally as part of a measured move.

**Identification:** A head-and-shoulders top with multiple shoulders or (more rarely) two heads. The head is higher than the shoulders but generally not by very much. The pattern is strongly symmetrical — shoulders mirror each other in price and time about the head, and outer shoulders mirror each other across the head as well. Volume is higher on the left side than on the right (left shoulders heavier than the right pair) and the overall volume trend recedes. The neckline connects the lowest left shoulder trough with the lowest right shoulder trough. A downward breakout occurs on a close below the neckline; for steep, down-sloping necklines, use the lowest trough price as the breakout point.

**Psychology:** A more elaborate distribution than the simple HST. The pattern frequently appears as a smooth rollover — even a rounding-top look in the inner price action — as smart money distributes across multiple peaks. The symmetry reflects patient, repeated selling into rallies, with each successive rally failing to reach the prior high. Once support gives way, the multi-month distribution resolves into a sharp decline.

**Measure rule:** Compute the formation height by subtracting the neckline value from the highest high reached in the head, measured vertically. Subtract the result from the breakout price where price pierces the neckline. If the formation looks like a mountain rising from a flat base, prices may return all the way to the base after the breakout. A slightly more conservative method: compute the height from the highest high to the higher of the two neckline troughs, then subtract the height from the higher trough to get the target.

**Key numbers:** 288 formations (210 bull / 78 bear). Average decline 23% bull, 27% bear. Break-even failure rate 4% bull, 1% bear. Change after trend ends +48% bull, +42% bear. Pullbacks 67% bull, 60% bear; average pullback length ~9–11 days. Percentage meeting price target 53% bull, 45% bear. Busted pattern performance 60% bull, 35% bear. Declines over 45% in 8% bull, 10% bear. Formation end to breakout 15 days bull, 14 days bear. Days to ultimate low 60 bull, 39 bear (a month after the breakout, ~61% bear and ~46% bull patterns have bottomed). Tall patterns outperform short (−25% vs. −21% bull); in bull markets narrow edges wide (−23% vs. −22%); in bear markets wide edges narrow (−28% vs. −26%). Bull-market best: tall and narrow (−26%); bear-market best: tall and wide (−29%). Rising volume trend outperforms (−23% bull, −29% bear). U-shaped volume wins in bull markets (−26%). Heavy breakout volume outperforms (−23% bull, −28% bear). Down-sloping necklines outperform (−23% bull, −30% bear). Higher right shoulder highs do best in bear markets (−30%). Average formation length 84 days bull, 71 days bear.

**Trading tactics:** Since a simple H&S sits inside a CHST, trade the inner H&S to get in sooner — sell any long position or initiate a short on inner confirmation. Conservative traders can wait for the outer pattern to confirm. For stops, look for resistance near the shoulder troughs, shoulder tops, and head top; place a stop just above the higher shoulder trough, and cover short if price rises above the highest high in the CHST. A pullback to the neckline is common; add to shorts on the pullback, but only after prices resume falling, because pullbacks can extend into new highs.

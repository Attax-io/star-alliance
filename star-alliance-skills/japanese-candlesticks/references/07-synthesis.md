# Putting It Together & Confluence of Candlesticks

## The Visual-Summary Method (Chapter 9)

### Philosophy of Interpretation
- The chapter's charts are numbered visual summaries of previously discussed candlestick lines and formations.
- Interpretations are **subjective**; different analysts may see different indicators in the same chart.
- There are **no concrete rules, only general guidelines**.
- A "purist" may reject an indicator that is not in its textbook-ideal form; others may act on the indicator; still others may wait for the next session to confirm.

### The Flexibility Rule
- A hammer-like line with a lower shadow only **1.5 times** the height of the real body (rather than the ideal 2× or 3×) may still be acted upon by some traders.
- Three acceptable responses to an ambiguous pattern:
  - **Reject it** (purist).
  - **Act on it** (e.g., cover shorts).
  - **Wait for the next session** to see what unfolds.

## Patterns Referenced in the Visual Summaries

## Bullish Inverted Hammer
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the lower end of the trading range with a long upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Identified by its position at a downtrend bottom and confirmed when the next session opens higher and prints a white candlestick.
- **Psychology:** The market probed higher but could not hold the gains by the close; however, the next session's higher open and white body show buyers have absorbed the supply.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Signals a potential bottom. **Confirmation =** next session's higher opening combined with a white candlestick.
- **Nison's notes:** In the cited chart, the bullish inverted hammer was also part of a **morning star** formation.

## Hanging Man
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the upper end of the range with a long lower shadow; little or no upper shadow. The "almost ideal" version has a very long lower shadow, a small real body, and almost no upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Found at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** Although the close was near the high (showing apparent strength), the long lower shadow reveals that sellers drove prices sharply lower during the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal warning. Confirmation comes from the next session:
  - A **black candlestick** the next day confirms the bearish hanging man.
  - Conversely, when the next session **gaps higher on the open**, the hanging man is **not confirmed**.
- **Nison's notes:** The hanging man **adds a bearish hue** to a preceding stalled pattern; combining a hanging man with a following black candlestick can simultaneously form a tweezer top and a bearish engulfing pattern.

## Stalled Pattern
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A tall white candlestick followed by a small white candlestick.
- **Recognition:** Look for a long white real body followed immediately by a small white real body at/near the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** The tall white body shows strong bulls; the small white body that follows shows the upward drive has stalled.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Implies the market's upward drive has stalled. Its bearishness is amplified when the last line of the pattern is itself a hanging man.
- **Nison's notes:** Stated as a pattern whose bearish implication is enhanced by the addition of a hanging-man last line.

## Tweezer Top
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Two consecutive candles with nearly identical highs.
- **Recognition:** Two candles at the top of an uptrend share approximately the same high.
- **Psychology:** The market tested the same price level twice and failed both times; buyers could not push prices above that level.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal at the top.
- **Nison's notes:** In the cited chart, the tweezer top and a bearish engulfing pattern were **simultaneously** formed by the same two candles (a hanging man plus the confirming black candlestick).

## Bearish Engulfing Pattern
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A white real body followed by a black real body that **completely engulfs** the prior white real body (open higher, close lower than prior body).
- **Recognition:** Found at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** Buyers were overwhelmed; the second session's selling fully engulfed the prior session's gains.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Strong bearish reversal. Per Nison, the pattern is meaningful **even when the market does not make a new high** on the black candlestick day — in which case Western reversal-day methods may fail to flag the turn while candlesticks still send a "loud reversal signal."
- **Nison's notes:** In one cited chart the engulfing pattern directly followed a harami; this combination (bearish engulfing after a harami) can be especially powerful.

## Bullish Engulfing Pattern
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A black real body followed by a white real body that completely engulfs the prior black real body.
- **Recognition:** Found at the bottom of a downtrend.
- **Psychology:** Sellers lost control; buyers engulfed the prior session's losses.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. Nison pairs it with a bullish white belt-hold line as a sign of a rally ahead.
- **Nison's notes:** A few days after the engulfing pattern, a successful test of the engulfing-pattern day's lows created a **tweezers bottom**, providing additional confirmation of support.

## Bullish White Belt-Hold Line (Bullish Belt Hold)
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A long white real body with **no lower shadow** (open at the low, close on or near the high).
- **Recognition:** Appears at the bottom of a downtrend; opens at the period's low and rallies to close on the high.
- **Psychology:** Buyers controlled the entire session from the open.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. In one cited example the line closed on its high, strengthening the signal.
- **Nison's notes:** A line with a small lower shadow can still be **viewed as a bullish belt hold** if the shadow is small enough. A bullish belt hold intersecting a long-term support line is an example of the Rule of Multiple Techniques at work.

## Piercing Pattern
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A black candlestick followed by a white candlestick that opens below the prior low (classic) or below the prior close (variation) and closes **well into** the black candlestick's real body.
- **Recognition:**
  - **Classic piercing pattern:** second white session opens **under the first day's low** and rallies into the black body.
  - **Variation on bullish piercing pattern:** second white session opens **under the first day's close** (not its low) and rallies to close well into the black real body.
- **Psychology:** After an initial black session showing bearish control, the next session opens weak but buyers drive prices deep into the prior loss.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. The pattern in the cited example **successfully tested the lows** of a prior support area.
- **Nison's notes:** When the second leg is itself a bullish belt-hold line that closes on its high, the piercing pattern is reinforced.

## Morning Star
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long black real body, a small real body (the "star") that gaps away from the first body, and a long white real body that closes well into the first session's body.
- **Recognition:** Found at the bottom of a downtrend.
- **Psychology:** Selling momentum paused with the small middle line; buyers then reasserted control.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. Nison notes the third line of a morning star need not be a "long" white real body — it is enough that it pushes well into the first session's white candlestick.
- **Nison's notes:** A morning star that also retests prior week lows is a more reliable reversal.

## Evening Star
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long white real body, a small real body (the "star"), and a long black real body that closes well into the first session's body.
- **Recognition:** Found at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** The middle session's small body signals indecision after the rally; the third session's black body shows sellers reasserting.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal. In one cited example, the **hanging-man session was the star** of an evening star formation.
- **Nison's notes:** This is an example of a bearish candlestick signal within another bearish candlestick signal.

## Harami (Harami Pattern)
- **Type:** Bullish or bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long real body followed by a **small real body contained within** the prior real body (no gap requirement in the cited examples).
- **Recognition:**
  - **Bullish harami:** long black body, then a small body (white or black) within it, at the bottom of a downtrend.
  - **Bearish harami:** long white body, then a small body within it, at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** The second session's small body shows the prior trend's momentum has stalled; the market has shifted to **indecision**.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - Signals a **warning that an uptrend has become a point of indecision** (bearish harami).
  - Signals the prior **downtrend has run out of steam** (bullish harami).
  - In a rally, a harami can **put the rally on hold**.
  - In a short-term uptrend, a harami can **short-circuit** the uptrend.
- **Nison's notes:** A shooting star that is also within the previous day's real body becomes a **harami and a shooting star simultaneously**. The bearish implications of a large black candlestick can be **mitigated** by the next day's small real body, forming a harami.

## Doji Star
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines (a doji following another line).
- **Structure:** A doji (open ≈ close) following a prior candlestick, often after a long white real body.
- **Recognition:** A doji appearing at the top of an uptrend, especially after a long white body.
- **Psychology:** After a strong rally, the doji shows the buyers could not push the close above the open — a warning of exhaustion.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - **Top reversal warning** when at the end of a rally.
  - After a long white candlestick, a doji star is a **top-reversal signal** meaning the prior uptrend should end.
- **Nison's notes:** A doji that **precedes a hanging man** is "not a healthy combination" — both signals together reinforce a bearish top.

## Hammer
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the top of the range, long lower shadow, little or no upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Found at the bottom of a downtrend.
- **Psychology:** Sellers drove prices sharply lower, but buyers absorbed the pressure and pushed the close back near the open.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Signals a possible bottom. A hammer that appears after a bullish harami provides **added proof** that the prior downtrend is done.
- **Nison's notes:** Nison uses the same line (hanging man / hammer) to denote either reversal depending on the prior trend context. The hammer is "almost ideal" when it has a very long lower shadow, a small real body, and almost no upper shadow.

## Doji
- **Type:** Neutral / warning · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Open and close at (or extremely near) the same price, producing no (or an almost invisible) real body.
- **Recognition:** The candle is essentially a horizontal line.
- **Psychology:** Perfect indecision — neither buyers nor sellers won the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A doji following a long white candlestick is a **top-reversal warning**. A doji that immediately follows a hanging man is a bearish reinforcement.
- **Nison's notes:** Used in confluence with hanging men and shooting stars to form bearish top reversals.

## Shooting Star
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the lower end of the range with a long upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Found at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** Buyers pushed prices higher, but sellers drove the close back near the open — a failed rally.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal. Nison treats a shooting star as the **"coup de grâce"** when it appears as the third in a series of top-reversal signals (hanging man, doji, shooting star).
- **Nison's notes:** A shooting star that is also within the previous day's real body forms a **harami and a shooting star** simultaneously.

## Three Buddha Top (Sanbutsujyou Sen)
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 3 candle lines (a sequence marked X, Y, Z across time).
- **Structure:** Three successive peaks separated in time, where each new test of resistance fails and the highs are roughly equivalent (a multi-session triple-top analogue expressed through candlestick labeling).
- **Recognition:** Three lettered points (X, Y, Z in the cited example from early, mid, and late February) form the three "Buddha" tops.
- **Psychology:** Repeated failure at the same resistance zone — three attempts to push higher all rejected.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Strong bearish reversal at the top.
- **Nison's notes:** Cited as a Japanese-named top-reversal formation in combination with a dark-cloud cover.

## Tweezer Bottom
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Two consecutive candles with nearly identical lows.
- **Recognition:** Two candles at the bottom of a downtrend share approximately the same low.
- **Psychology:** The market tested the same support twice and held both times — buyers defended that level.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. A tweezer bottom can be created by a **successful second test of a prior swing low**, confirming support.
- **Nison's notes:** In one chart, a tweezer bottom and a bullish belt-hold line (with a small lower shadow) ended a selloff. An inverted hammer that also made a tweezer bottom is doubly significant.

## Minor Tweezer Top
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A small version of the tweezer top, with two candles sharing similar highs but otherwise less extreme than a full tweezer top.
- **Recognition:** Two candles near the top of a rally with matching highs.
- **Psychology:** Minor failure at the same level.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A minor top that can stop a rally at a window's resistance.
- **Nison's notes:** Cited as the form that stopped a rally at a window's resistance from below.

## Dark-Cloud Cover
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A white candlestick followed by a black candlestick that opens above the prior high (or near it) and closes **well into** the prior white real body.
- **Recognition:** Found at the top of an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** The market opened strongly higher (above the prior high), implying continuation, but sellers took over and pushed the close deep into the prior gains.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal.
- **Nison's notes:** Appears in the cited chart immediately after a tweezer-bottom/bullish belt-hold setup, and as a key reversal following a three Buddha top.

## Window (Candlestick Gap)
- **Type:** Neutral · support/resistance · 1+ candle gap.
- **Structure:** A gap between the high of one candlestick and the low of the next (or vice versa) that is **not** filled by overlapping trades.
- **Recognition:** A clean price gap on the chart.
- **Psychology:** A sudden shift in sentiment that leaves unfilled price behind; the unfilled area now represents supply or demand.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - A window should act as **resistance** when the market later approaches it from below.
  - A window should act as **support** when the market approaches it from above.
- **Nison's notes:** In one example, a rally that started from an inverted hammer **failed at the resistance** of an open window. A window is one of the strongest standalone Japanese technical signals in this excerpt.

## Confluence Concepts (Chapter 10)

## The Rule of Multiple Technical Techniques
- **Definition:** A method, named by Arthur Sklarew in *Techniques of a Professional Chart Analyst*, stating that **the more technical indicators that assemble at the same price area, the greater the chance of an accurate forecast**.
- **Application:** Candlestick techniques become more powerfully significant if they **confirm a Western technical signal**, and vice versa.
- **Example — Gold (late 1989–early 1990):** Four separate Western indicators pointed to major resistance at the **$425–$433** zone:
  1. A **50% retracement** of the 1987 high ($502) and 1989 lows ($357) implied resistance at $430.
  2. A **double-bottom measured move** (height of move between the two 1989 lows added to the intervening high) implied a target of $425.
  3. The **late-1988 high** was at $433.
  4. **Elliott Wave theory** (per John Gambino): gold was in a fourth-wave count, implying rallies should not pierce the first-wave's low at $425.
- **Failure rule:** If gold closed **above $433**, the long-term bearish prognosis would have to be changed. The market defines when an analyst's view is wrong.
- **Example — Sugar (May 1990):** Four technical indicators implied strong resistance in the **$.1515–$.1520** zone:
  1. A **multi-tested old support level** (early March through April) — once broken, strong support should become equally strong resistance.
  2. The **65-day moving average** intersected near the $.1515 level.
  3. **Psychological importance** of the $.15 level based on the January highs and the March gap.
  4. A **Fibonacci 32% retracement** of the move from the $.1627 peak (H) to the $.1444 low (L) was $.1514.
- **Practical implication:** Sugar peaked at **$.1505** on May 14, just below the resistance zone, confirming the multiple-technique forecast.

## Confluence of Candlesticks
- **Definition:** When a **cluster of candlestick patterns or lines coincide at the same price area**, that level becomes an important market juncture.
- **Example 1 (Crude Oil, Oct 1989):** Top — a hanging man was immediately followed by a doji; prices then fell. Bottom — a hammer, followed by a bullish engulfing line, then a successful retest of the hammer day's lows that created a **tweezers bottom**.
- **Example 2 (Bonds, Sept 1990 chart caption):** A **September hammer** presaged a rally. The rally was then ended in late November by **three top-reversal indicators** at the same level:
  1. A **hanging man**.
  2. A **doji**.
  3. A **shooting star** (the "coup de grâce").
- **Example 3 (Fujitsu, 1990):** One candlestick line gave **multiple simultaneous signals**:
  - A long white real body was followed by a small real body with a long upper shadow — the **shape of a shooting star**.
  - The small real body (within the previous day's real body) made it a **harami**.
  - The top of the upper shadow failed at the **February 1600 highs** (a Western resistance level).
- **Example 4 (Exxon, weekly):** Within a few weeks the market formed a **tweezers bottom, a bullish engulfing pattern, and a hammer** at the same support area.
- **Example 5 (Sugar, Oct 1989):** From mid- to late-July, a series of bearish indications occurred: a **doji star** followed by three **hanging-man lines** (labeled 1, 2, 3); in between hanging-man 1 and 2, a **shooting star** formed.
- **Example 6 (Wheat, March 1990):** A bearish-candlestick-within-a-bearish-candlestick — the rally's peak was touched by a **hanging-man session** that was simultaneously the **star portion of an evening star** formation.
- **Example 7 (Copper, Sept 1990):** May 9–11 delivered four bearish indications at the **$1.12 area** within three sessions:
  1. A **hanging man** (May 9's tall white candle, then May 10's small body).
  2. A **harami** (May 9 + May 10).
  3. A **shooting star** (May 11's failed assault at $1.12).
  4. A **tweezers top** (the three sessions' nearly identical highs).
  - The **$1.12 level then became pivotal resistance** in mid-June. Once **broken upside on June 28**, it **converted to pivotal support**.
  - A **doji star** appeared after the June 28 long white candle, signaling a top reversal of the short-term bull market that began June 26.
  - Two black candlesticks (July 2 and 3) held the old $1.12 resistance as support — the bears could not break it. Until that support broke, the back of the short-term bull market could not be broken.
- **Example 8 (British Pound, weekly 1987):** Within a month, three candlestick signals intimated a top: a **hanging man, a doji, and a dark-cloud cover**. After the dark-cloud cover, the market sold off and **opened a window**, which then became **resistance** on the brief rally before the next leg lower. The selloff ended with a **tweezers bottom and a bullish belt-hold line** (small enough lower shadow to view the line as a bullish belt hold).

## Support/Resistance Flip ("Polarity")
- **Rule:** **Old support becomes new resistance and vice versa.**
- **Application in the text:** The $1.12 area that was a strong **top-reversal confluence** in May became important **resistance** in mid-June, and then **pivotal support** once broken upside on June 28.
- **Sugar example:** A multi-tested old support level, once broken, should become equally strong resistance.

## The "DISCIPLINE" Anagram (Nison's Trading Rules)

Nison runs a non-candlestick seminar titled *"Techniques of Disciplined Trading Using Technical Analysis"* in which the word **DISCIPLINE** is used as an anagram — each letter introduces a trading rule:

- **D** — Don't forget old support and resistance levels (old support becomes new resistance and vice versa).
- **I** — **If…then system**: if the market behaves as anticipated, then stay with the trade — otherwise exit.
- **S** — **Stops** — always use them.
- **C** — **Consider options**.
- **I** — **Intra-day technicals are important**.
- **P** — **Pace trades to market environment** (change trading style according to market conditions).
- **L** — **Locals** — never forget them.
- **I** — **Indicators — the more the better** (the rule of multiple technical techniques).
- **N** — **Never trade in the belief the market is wrong**.
- **E** — **Examine the market's reaction to the fundamentals**.

### "The Market Is Never Wrong"
- **Rule:** Do not try to impose your beliefs on the market. **Do not buck the trend.**
- **Logic:** The market communicates by price activity. If price action says the analyst is mistaken, the analyst must adjust — the market will not adjust to the analyst.
- **Steamroller analogy:** A driver going the wrong way on a one-way street will not turn around in time to avoid the analyst standing in its path, no matter how right the analyst was about the direction.
- **Practical advice:** Be a **trend follower, not a trend predictor**. If bullish, jump onto uptrends; if bearish, hop onto downtrends.
- **Margin risk:** Bucking the trend may prove correct eventually, but margin calls may force exit before the expected move.
- **Japanese source quote (Sakata Goho Wa Furinkazan, 1969, p. 46, translated by Richard Solberg):** *"Buying or selling from the beginning without knowing the character of the market is the same nonsense as a literary man talking about weapons. When faced with a large bull or bear market they are sure to lose the castle; what seems safe is infinitely dangerous. … Waiting for just the right moment is virtuous and essential."*

## Key Conceptual Takeaways From the Excerpt

### Pattern Combinations Cited as Especially Powerful
- **Hanging man + following black candle** = tweezer top **and** bearish engulfing pattern simultaneously.
- **Piercing pattern + bullish belt-hold line** that closes on its high and successfully retests prior lows.
- **Inverted hammer + tweezer bottom** (two bullish signals in one line).
- **Shooting star whose body is within the prior body** = shooting star **and** harami simultaneously.
- **Hanging man whose session is also the star of an evening star** = a bearish signal inside a bearish signal.
- **Hanging man + harami + shooting star + tweezer top** at the same level (Copper example) = four bearish signals within three sessions.

### How Nison Defines "Confirmation" in Practice
- Higher open + white candle following an inverted hammer.
- Black candle following a hanging man.
- A successful second test of a swing low (creating a tweezers bottom) confirming a prior bullish engulfing pattern.
- Old resistance holding as new support after being broken (support/resistance flip).

### What Invalidates a Pattern
- A hanging man is **not confirmed** if the next session **gaps higher on the open**.
- A "purist" framework may reject near-ideal versions of a pattern (e.g., a hammer with a 1.5× lower shadow).

### Discretionary vs. Mechanical Posture
- Interpretations are **subjective**; different experiences yield different perspectives.
- There are **no concrete rules, only general guidelines**.
- Nison's book pairs candlesticks with Western futures tools (trendlines, moving averages, oscillators); equity-specific tools such as advance/decline lines, the ARMS/TRIN index, and specialist short sales are explicitly out of scope.

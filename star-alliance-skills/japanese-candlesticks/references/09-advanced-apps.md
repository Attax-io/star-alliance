# Advanced Applications — Elliott, Market Profile, Options, Hedging, Practice

## Elliott Wave — Foundational Concepts

### Elliott Wave Principle
- **Type:** analytical framework (not a single candlestick pattern).
- **Structure:** a five-wave move in the direction of the main trend (waves 1, 3, 5 are impulse waves; waves 2 and 4 are corrective waves) followed by a three-wave corrective move. These eight waves form a complete cycle. The same concepts hold in a falling market (impulse waves slope down, corrective waves are upward bounces against the main trend).
- **Recognition:** applicable from intra-day to yearly charts; wave counts are subjective until the wave is over, so different analysts may derive different counts.
- **Psychology:** price moves unfold in repeating, structured sequences of impulse and correction rather than as random fluctuations.
- **Signal & confirmation:** wave 3 usually has the most powerful move and is the most important wave to trade; the top of wave 5 calls for a reversal of positions. Wave counts are confirmed by candlestick signals at termination points.
- **Nison's notes:** Elliott Wave is described as introductory here ("scratch the surface"). Most important trading waves are 3 and 5.

### Fibonacci Ratios with Elliott Wave
- **Type:** projection tool.
- **Structure:** Fibonacci ratios are used to project price targets for the next wave based on the height of prior waves. Examples: wave 3 could be projected to move 1.618 times the height of wave 1; wave 4 could correct 38.2% or 50% of the wave 3 move.
- **Recognition:** measure the height of a prior wave, then apply a Fibonacci multiplier to estimate the next wave's target.
- **Psychology:** markets self-organize around these natural ratios.
- **Signal & confirmation:** provides price targets where candlestick confirmation should then be sought.
- **Nison's notes:** "Wave counts and Fibonacci ratios go hand in hand."

### Wave 4 Constraint
- **Type:** Elliott structural rule.
- **Structure:** the top of corrective wave 4 should rally but should not move above the bottom of the prior wave 1.
- **Recognition:** measure the low of wave 1; the high of wave 4 should stay below it.
- **Psychology:** a corrective wave cannot retrace more than 100% of the prior impulse wave.
- **Signal & confirmation:** approaching the wave 1 low becomes a zone to watch for a bearish candlestick cue.
- **Nison's notes:** when wave 4 is broken into (a), (b), (c) subcomponents, candlestick indicators can corroborate each subcomponent.

## Elliott Wave — Candlestick Patterns Confirmed at Wave Points

### Dark-Cloud Cover (at Elliott Wave termination)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a dark-cloud cover formed at the end of a wave 1 (late February) and again ended corrective wave 2.
- **Recognition:** appears as a bearish reversal pattern at the close of an impulse wave.
- **Psychology:** prior upward pressure is overcome by sellers.
- **Signal & confirmation:** in the crude oil example, it marked the end of wave 1 and the end of corrective wave 2.
- **Nison's notes:** dark-cloud covers are used to call the termination of an upward impulse or corrective wave.

### Harami (at Elliott Wave termination)
- **Type:** bullish or bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a harami pattern. When at the end of a down wave, the prior downward pressure was abating and a minor bounce unfolded. Also appeared as the wave 4 top (harami cross with tweezers top). Also constituted a wave 4 in the bond example.
- **Recognition:** small real body inside the prior real body at the termination of an Elliott wave.
- **Psychology:** the prior momentum is exhausted.
- **Signal & confirmation:** at the end of wave 1 it implied that the prior downward pressure was abating; at the wave 4 top in crude it was accompanied by a tweezers top under $20.
- **Nison's notes:** the harami was also used as a signal that the major downtrend had been neutralized in the coffee example (small real body after a long black line).

### Hammer (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** a classic hammer appeared on the hourly chart at the first hour of trading during a selloff on April 11 (crude oil). The selloff stopped near the Elliott wave 3 target of $17.60.
- **Recognition:** long lower shadow; small body at the top of the range.
- **Psychology:** the selloff abruptly stopped at the projected Elliott target; the bulls defended the level.
- **Signal & confirmation:** immediately followed by a series of strong long white bodies on the hourly chart, which together confirmed a bottom for the wave 3 count.
- **Nison's notes:** "some interesting price events occur" when the hammer appears exactly at the Fibonacci-derived Elliott target.

### Piercing Pattern / Piercing Line (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a piercing pattern based on the price action of April 10 and 11 confirmed the daily chart bottom reversal for the wave 3 count in crude. Also the bottom of wave 4 in cocoa was marked by a bullish piercing line.
- **Recognition:** opens below the prior low and closes above the midpoint of the prior black real body.
- **Psychology:** buyers overwhelm the prior session's sellers.
- **Signal & confirmation:** combined with the Elliott wave 3 count target of $17.60.
- **Nison's notes:** it was the daily chart that provided the bottom reversal signal even though the hammer was hourly.

### Harami Cross (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bullish or bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a harami cross where the small real body is a doji.
- **Recognition:** a doji (cross) inside the prior real body.
- **Psychology:** indecision at the termination of a wave.
- **Signal & confirmation:** in crude, the harami cross plus a tweezers top stalled the market under $20 at the wave 4 top. In silver (options example), the harami cross marked the end of a selloff, after which the trend changed from lower to lateral and volatility contracted.
- **Nison's notes:** when the harami cross is formed by a long white candlestick and a doji, the market is "in trouble" — a doji after a long white real body is often a sign of a top.

### Tweezers Top (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** two or more candlesticks with equal highs; in crude, it appeared with a harami cross at the wave 4 top, stalling the market under $20.
- **Recognition:** matching highs across consecutive sessions at a resistance level.
- **Psychology:** buyers repeatedly fail to push above a key price.
- **Signal & confirmation:** combined with the harami cross and the wave 1 low constraint.
- **Nison's notes:** the combination of harami cross and tweezers top at the same area gave high confidence in the wave 4 top.

### Shooting Star (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** appeared at subcomponent (a) of wave 4, with highs stopped at a window from early April. Also marked the top of wave 3 in cocoa.
- **Recognition:** small body near the low of the session, long upper shadow, occurring after an upmove.
- **Psychology:** higher prices attracted strong selling.
- **Signal & confirmation:** at the cocoa wave 3 top, it joined a Fibonacci-derived wave 5 target near $1,520 (the actual high was $1,541).
- **Nison's notes:** the highs stopping at a prior window adds extra significance.

### Morning Star (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a morning star at subcomponent (b) called the bottom of the wave 4 retracement in crude.
- **Recognition:** small-body session after a black real body, followed by a strong white real body.
- **Psychology:** the prior downtrend stalls and reverses.
- **Signal & confirmation:** marked the (b) subcomponent bottom.
- **Nison's notes:** morning stars are useful for identifying (b) subcomponent bottoms inside corrective waves.

### Tweezers Bottom (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** at the end of April in bonds, the low of the move (88.07) was accompanied by a tweezers bottom and two doji at this tweezers bottom. Wave 5 target was 88.08 (height of wave 3 with a Fibonacci ratio), so the low came within 0.01 of the target. In sugar (options), the March 2 low joined with a February 26 hammer to complete a tweezers bottom.
- **Recognition:** two or more candlesticks with matching lows.
- **Psychology:** sellers repeatedly fail to push below a key support.
- **Signal & confirmation:** in bonds, volume also confirmed this bottom.
- **Nison's notes:** the precision of the Elliott target (88.08 vs. actual 88.07) was striking, and the candlestick signal at that exact level added high confidence.

### Doji (in Elliott Wave context)
- **Type:** neutral · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** two doji at the tweezers bottom in bonds (late April 1990). Also the harami cross is a doji.
- **Recognition:** open and close at the same level.
- **Psychology:** indecision.
- **Signal & confirmation:** in the bond example, two doji at the tweezers bottom sent a strong bullish signal; volume also confirmed the bottom.
- **Nison's notes:** a doji after a long white real body is often a sign of a top (when it is part of a harami cross with a long white line, the market is "in trouble").

## Market Profile — Foundational Concepts

### Market Profile®
- **Type:** analytical framework, registered trademark of the Chicago Board of Trade.
- **Structure:** presents information about markets that was previously only available to those in the trading pits. Offers a logical, statistically based analysis of price, time, and volume. Helps technicians understand the internal structure of markets.
- **Recognition:** organizes daily action into half-hour periods and assigns a letter to each half-hour period. Each letter is a TPO (Time-Price Opportunity). The half-hour segment represents the price range that developed over that time period.
- **Psychology:** the purpose of all markets is to facilitate trade. Markets are self-regulating via price, time, and volume. The market uses price probes to "advertise" for sellers or buyers; the reaction to these probes provides valuable clues.
- **Signal & confirmation:** combines with candlestick signals to corroborate market turns.
- **Nison's notes:** this section examines only a few of the many Market Profile® tools. Topics such as different types of profile days (normal, trend, neutral, long-term market activity charts) or the Liquidity Data Bank® are not discussed.

### TPO (Time-Price Opportunity) Lettering
- **Type:** organizational convention.
- **Structure:** "A" period is 8:00 to 8:30 A.M. Chicago time, "B" is 8:30 to 9:00 A.M., and so forth. For markets that open before 8:00 A.M. (such as bonds, currencies, and metals), the first half-hours are usually designated "y" and "z."
- **Recognition:** each letter represents a 30-minute period.
- **Signal & confirmation:** the total number of TPO counts in a session can be used as a gauge of volume (since actual volume is not released until the following day).
- **Nison's notes:** a session's TPO count serves as a proxy for volume in real time.

### Initial Balance
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** the first hour of trading; the time period when the market is exploring the range of trading. The market's early attempt to find value.
- **Recognition:** the first two 30-minute periods combined.
- **Psychology:** the market probes for boundaries of value.
- **Signal & confirmation:** reference point for later range extensions.
- **Nison's notes:** also called "the market's early attempt to find value."

### Range Extension
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** any new high or low made after the first hour's initial balance. Can be to the upside (buying range extension) or downside (selling range extension). Exhibit 17.1 shows a selling range extension but no buying range extension.
- **Recognition:** new high or low after the first hour.
- **Psychology:** the market advertises for buyers or sellers at new prices.
- **Signal & confirmation:** reaction to the probe reveals the strength or weakness of the market.
- **Nison's notes:** a range extension to both sides indicates a "tug of war" between the bulls and bears (as on July 2 in the bonds example).

### Value and Value Area
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** value is defined as the market's acceptance of price over time and is reflected in the amount of volume traded at that price. Time and volume are the key ingredients. If the market trades briefly at a price, the market is indicating rejection. The market's acceptance of price is where 70% of the day's volume occurred (one standard deviation, rounded up to 70%). This is the day's value area. Example: if 70% of wheat volume took place in a $3.30 to $3.33 range, that range would be its value area.
- **Recognition:** prices accepted for an extended time with good volume = value found. Brief trade at a price = rejection.
- **Psychology:** acceptance of price requires both time and volume.
- **Signal & confirmation:** wider value areas usually represent facilitation of trade and increase probability for price trend continuation. Shrinking value areas reflect less facilitation and less certainty of continuation.
- **Nison's notes:** "the market has not found value" when prices are quickly rejected. Wider value areas in an uptrend (parallel to longer and longer white real bodies in candlesticks) confirm the power behind the move. Shrinking value areas parallel advance block or stalled patterns.

### Price Probe
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** the market's search for the boundaries of value. After a price probe, one of two actions occurs: prices backtrack to the value area (rejection) or value relocates to the new price (acceptance). Acceptance of a new price as value is confirmed by increased volume and the time spent at that level.
- **Recognition:** a new high or low after the initial balance.
- **Psychology:** markets use probes to discover where buyers and sellers will act.
- **Signal & confirmation:** quick rejection of a price can result in an extreme.
- **Nison's notes:** the trading community's response to probes sends important information.

### Extreme (Buying or Selling)
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** defined as two or more single TPOs at the top or bottom of the profile (except for the last half hour). A top extreme is normally caused by competition among sellers attracted by higher prices and a lack of buyers. A bottom extreme is caused by an influx of buyers attracted by lower prices and a dearth of sellers.
- **Recognition:** single TPOs isolated at the top or bottom of the profile.
- **Psychology:** strong rejection of extreme prices.
- **Signal & confirmation:** extremes are clues about the strength or weakness of the market. A hammer's lower shadow might be formed due to a buying extreme; a shooting star's long upper shadow could be the result of a selling extreme.
- **Nison's notes:** buying and selling extremes are direct analogues to hammer (buying extreme) and shooting star (selling extreme) candlesticks.

### Initiating vs. Responsive Buying/Selling
- **Type:** Market Profile concept.
- **Structure:** identified by determining where the current day's extreme and range extensions are occurring with respect to the prior day's value area.
  - **Buying below the prior day's value area:** responsive buying (buyers expect prices will return to value).
  - **Selling below the prior day's value area:** initiating selling (aggressive sellers; they believe value will move down).
  - **Buying above the prior session's value area:** initiating buying (aggressive buyers; they believe value will move up).
  - **Selling above the prior day's value area:** responsive sellers (expect prices to return to value).
- **Recognition:** relative to the prior day's value area.
- **Psychology:** initiating participants expect value to migrate; responsive participants expect prices to return to value.
- **Signal & confirmation:** trading should go in the direction of the initiating group unless price is quickly rejected. Initiating selling activity (extremes and/or range extension) under the previous day's value area on expanding volume should have bearish implications.
- **Nison's notes:** in the July 6 bond example, the open under the prior day's value area showed initiating selling on the opening.

## Market Profile — Candlestick Pattern Parallels

### Doji (Market Profile context)
- **Type:** neutral · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** a doji on July 2 in bonds was a candlestick alert of a top.
- **Recognition:** open and close at the same level.
- **Psychology:** indecision.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Market Profile® on July 2 showed a relatively small value area (versus the prior session) and light volume (132,000 contracts versus 303,000 prior). This hinted that price was having trouble being accepted at higher rates — lack of trade facilitation. The profile had range extension to both sides, indicating a "tug of war."
- **Nison's notes:** the candlestick doji and the Market Profile® data (small value area, light volume, two-sided range extension) corroborated the same top.

### Hanging Man (Market Profile context)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** a hanging man appeared during the next session (July 3) after the July 2 doji.
- **Recognition:** small body at the top with a long lower shadow after an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** the prior uptrend is exhausted.
- **Signal & confirmation:** it is only what happens after the hanging man that makes it a bearish indicator. July 3 was the hanging-man day and had some positive indications: upside range extension and a close at the highs of the day. The top was only verified on July 5 and the morning of July 6 via Market Profile®. The July 3 session was light volume (109,000 contracts), which discounted the bullish developments.
- **Nison's notes:** Market Profile® revealed that the immediate context of the hanging man was actually positive — emphasizing that the hanging man requires confirmation by subsequent price action.

### Shooting Star (Market Profile context)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** a very evident shooting star on July 5 in cotton. After this line, cotton plunged for three sessions.
- **Recognition:** small body at the bottom, long upper shadow, after an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** higher prices attracted strong selling.
- **Signal & confirmation:** from June 29 to July 3, prices advanced but by way of a shrinking value area (less trade facilitation at higher prices). Volume, as gauged by TPO counts, was decreasing. The July 5 Market Profile® showed a range toward the upside failed to get buying follow-through; entry of sellers drove the market down (range extension down, weak close). On July 6 open, an initiating selling extreme developed under the previous day's value area.
- **Nison's notes:** prior to the shooting star, the shrinking value area and declining TPO count already hinted at trouble. The shooting star provided the final candlestick alert.

### Advance Block / Stalled Patterns (Market Profile parallel)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 3 or more candle lines.
- **Structure:** the trend is still up but it takes place by means of shrinking white real bodies. These formations indicate that the prior momentum is running out of steam.
- **Recognition:** series of white real bodies, each smaller than the prior one, in an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** buyers are losing conviction even though the trend persists.
- **Signal & confirmation:** parallels the shrinking value area concept in Market Profile® (less facilitation of trade, less certainty of continuation).
- **Nison's notes:** "Shrinking value areas in Market Profile® reflect less facilitation of trade and thus less certainty of a continuation of the price move. So it is with the candlestick's advance block or stalled patterns."

### Star in Candlesticks (Market Profile parallel)
- **Type:** neutral · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** a short real body in an uptrend (star in uptrend — decreasing bull vigor) or in a downtrend (decreasing bear vigor).
- **Recognition:** small real body in the context of a prevailing trend.
- **Psychology:** the prevailing side is losing strength.
- **Signal & confirmation:** parallels a small value area after a strong advance or decline (lack of trade facilitation), a possible harbinger of trend change.
- **Nison's notes:** "A short real body in an uptrend or downtrend would be a sign of decreasing vigor by the bulls (a star in an uptrend) or the bears (a star in a downtrend)."

## Options — Foundational Concepts

### Options Advantages
- **Type:** strategic considerations.
- **Structure:** four advantages: (1) Staying power — buying an option limits risk to the premium paid; useful when picking tops and bottoms. (2) The ability to benefit in sideways markets via selling straddles or combinations. (3) Strategy flexibility — risk/reward parameters tailored to price, volatility, and timing projections. (4) Occasionally superior leverage — an out-of-the-money call may provide a greater percentage return than an outright position if the market swiftly rallies under certain circumstances.
- **Recognition:** apply these principles to a given market context.
- **Psychology:** options confer advantages over underlying positions in specific scenarios.
- **Signal & confirmation:** limited risk makes options useful for risky propositions like top and bottom picking.
- **Nison's notes:** all four advantages are stated as "occasionally" or "at times" — they are situation-dependent.

### Option Pricing — Five Factors
- **Type:** theoretical framework.
- **Structure:** the five factors needed to figure the theoretical value of a futures options price are: (1) the exercise price, (2) the time to expiration, (3) the price of the underlying instrument, (4) volatility, and (5) short-term interest rates (to a minor extent). Three of these are known (time until expiration, exercise price, short-term interest rates). Two must be estimated: forecasted price of the underlying and its volatility.
- **Recognition:** input these variables to derive the theoretical option price.
- **Psychology:** option pricing is probabilistic; volatility is often the most underestimated input.
- **Signal & confirmation:** "one should not underrate the importance that volatility plays in option pricing. In fact, at times, a change in volatility can have a stronger impact on option premiums than a change in price of the underlying contract."
- **Nison's notes:** the example given: an at-the-money $390 gold call with 60 days to expiration is theoretically $1,300 at 20% volatility and $1,000 at 15% volatility.

### Volatility Interpretation
- **Type:** probabilistic concept.
- **Structure:** volatility levels provide the expected range of prices over the next year (annual basis). A volatility of 20% suggests 68% probability that a year from now price will remain within ±20% of current price, and 95% chance within ±40% (two times the volatility). Example: gold at $400, volatility 20% → 68% chance price is between $320 and $480 after one year, 95% chance between $240 and $560.
- **Recognition:** compute the ±1× and ±2× ranges from current price using the volatility percentage.
- **Psychology:** markets trade within a probabilistic range.
- **Signal & confirmation:** these are probability-based and the levels can be exceeded.
- **Nison's notes:** "Keep in mind that these levels are based on probabilities and that at times these levels are exceeded."

### Why Greater Volatility Means More Expensive Options
- **Type:** pricing relationship.
- **Structure:** three factors: (1) from speculators' view, greater volatility = greater chance to move into the money; (2) from hedgers' view, higher price volatility = more price risk, so more reason to buy options as hedging vehicle; (3) option sellers require more compensation for higher perceived risk. All buoy option premiums.
- **Recognition:** compare option prices at different volatility levels.
- **Psychology:** multiple market participants bid up premium when volatility is high.
- **Signal & confirmation:** confirm by monitoring the option's bid/ask.
- **Nison's notes:** the three factors are all reinforcing.

### Historic vs. Implied Volatility
- **Type:** measurement distinction.
- **Structure:** **Historic volatility** is based on past volatility levels of the underlying contract, usually calculated from daily price changes over a specified number of business days, annualized. In futures markets, 20 or 30 days are most common. A 20-day historic volatility of 15% does not mean it will remain at that level during the option's life — so forecasting is required. **Implied volatility** is the market's estimate of what volatility will be from now until the option expires. To obtain it, input the current futures price, option strike price, short-term interest rate, expiration date, and current option price into a computer using an options pricing formula; the computer returns the implied volatility.
- **Recognition:** compare historic to implied; some traders focus on historic, others on implied, still others compare.
- **Psychology:** the market's own pricing of options reflects its collective forecast of future volatility.
- **Signal & confirmation:** if a candlestick signal implies a trend change to lateral, implied volatility may decline; if it implies a new trend, implied volatility may rise.
- **Nison's notes:** "Based on experience, and for various other reasons, candlestick signals seem to work better with historic volatility than with implied volatility."

### Hammer (Options context — Sugar)

Volatility at areas A and B was at relatively low levels; with a possible strong price rally and low volatility, expanding volatility was expected — and unfolded.
- **Nison's notes:** "candlesticks, at times, can be useful instruments to assist in forecasting short-term moves in implied volatility." Also: candlestick signals should be viewed in the context that volatility shifts will most likely be shorter term.

## Hedging — Foundational Concepts

### Short Hedger
- **Type:** hedging concept.
- **Structure:** an underlying cash position at risk of declining prices. Examples: a copper producer or a farmer. To manage risk of lower prices, initiate a short position by selling futures or by buying puts. If prices fall, decreasing cash value is at least partially counter-balanced by futures/options profits.
- **Recognition:** identify a long cash position in a market where prices may fall.
- **Psychology:** the hedge offsets the price risk of the cash position.
- **Signal & confirmation:** candlestick reversal signals can time placement, lifting, or adjustment of the hedge.
- **Nison's notes:** a short hedger may need to decide what percent of exposure to hedge; candlesticks can help answer this.

### Long Hedger
- **Type:** hedging concept.
- **Structure:** an ultimate user of the cash commodity whose underlying cash position is at risk of rising prices. Example: a shirt manufacturer who must buy cotton. To manage risk of higher prices, place a long hedge by buying futures or calls. If prices rise, higher cash cost is at least partially offset by futures/options gains.
- **Recognition:** identify a cash need to buy a commodity in a market where prices may rise.
- **Psychology:** the hedge offsets the price risk of the cash position.
- **Signal & confirmation:** candlestick reversal signals can time placement or lifting of the hedge.
- **Nison's notes:** candlesticks can help those who are 100% hedged as well — for example, easing back on a profitable hedge if a strongly bullish candlestick reversal appears.

## Hedging — Candlestick Patterns Applied to Hedge Timing

### Bearish Engulfing Pattern (Hedging context — Soybeans)
- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** a bearish engulfing pattern at the peak of the rally in mid-1988 (soybeans, $6 to $11). The black candlestick session did not create a new high for the move — so while not a Western top reversal pattern (which would have required a new high followed by a close under the prior week's close), it was a reversal formation with candlesticks. In 1989, another bearish engulfing pattern warned of a top — also not a Western top reversal pattern.
- **Recognition:** a black real body that engulfs the prior white real body.
- **Psychology:** sellers overwhelmed buyers.
- **Signal & confirmation:** could have been used by short hedgers to initiate or raise the percentage hedged. The 1989 pattern warned of a top; the window a few weeks later was another bearish candlestick signal telling short hedgers to add to their short hedge position.
- **Nison's notes:** "This would have not been seen as a Western top reversal formation using traditional Western techniques. A top reversal formation would have required a new high for the move and then a close under the prior week's close. The black candlestick session of the engulfing pattern did not create a new high. Thus, while not a Western top reversal pattern, it was a reversal formation with candlesticks."

### Window (Hedging context — Soybeans)
- **Type:** bearish or bullish · continuation/reversal · 2 candle lines (gap).
- **Structure:** a window a few weeks after the 1989 bearish engulfing pattern in soybeans.
- **Recognition:** a gap between the high of one session and the low of the next (or vice versa).
- **Psychology:** the move is so strong it leaves unfilled price behind.
- **Signal & confirmation:** another bearish candlestick signal telling short hedgers to add to their short hedge position (if not already 100% hedged).
- **Nison's notes:** used in conjunction with bearish engulfing patterns to time incremental hedging.

### Bullish Piercing Pattern (Hedging context — Soybeans)
- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** October's bullish piercing pattern in soybeans.
- **Recognition:** opens below the prior low and closes above the midpoint of the prior black real body.
- **Psychology:** buyers overwhelm the prior session's sellers.
- **Signal & confirmation:** for short hedgers, a signal to ease back on short-hedge positions. For those looking at a long hedge, a signal to initiate such a hedge.
- **Nison's notes:** piercing patterns serve opposite signals for short versus long hedgers at the same time.

### Bullish Engulfing Pattern (Hedging context — Cocoa)

### Tweezers Bottom (Hedging context — Cocoa)

### Bullish Belt Hold (Hedging context — Cocoa)

- **Signal & confirmation:** for long hedgers, a sign to start or add to hedges. The fact that it engulfed five prior candlesticks added significance.

### Harami Cross (Hedging context — Cocoa)

## General Practice Principles

### Trade Location Through Pattern Confluence
- **Type:** methodology.
- **Structure:** "candlesticks can help to provide good trade location." The confluence of multiple patterns (hammer + harami + spring; or engulfing + belt hold + spring + positive divergence) creates high-confidence signals.
- **Recognition:** multiple bullish or bearish signals emerging in the same price area.
- **Psychology:** multiple signals from different methodologies reduce uncertainty.
- **Signal & confirmation:** the more technical signals confirming each other, the greater the likelihood of an accurate call for a trend change.
- **Nison's notes:** "candlestick techniques, in conjunction with Western technical methods, can create a powerful synergy. The more technical signals confirming each other, the greater the likelihood of an accurate call for a trend change."

### Bias Toward the Major Trend
- **Type:** trading principle.
- **Structure:** "a bullish candlestick signal in a major bear market should not be used as a buy signal. A bullish candlestick formation, especially when confirmed by other technical signals in a bull market, would be a buying point." In crude oil, a bullish hammer was not used as a buy signal because the major trend was down — instead, it was used to anticipate resistance for a short sale.
- **Recognition:** check the prevailing trend before acting on a candlestick signal.
- **Psychology:** counter-trend signals have lower reliability.
- **Signal & confirmation:** confluence with other signals in a bull market upgrades a bullish signal.
- **Nison's notes:** "Where you stand in relation to the overall technical evidence may be more important than an individual candlestick pattern."

### Subjectivity in Candlestick Analysis
- **Type:** methodology principle.
- **Structure:** "candlesticks, as with all other charting methods, require subjectivity." Two candlestick analysts are like two doctors with the same training but different experiences, philosophies, and preferences — they may give the same patient a different diagnosis.
- **Recognition:** different analysts may derive different wave counts or pattern identifications.
- **Psychology:** subjectivity is inherent to technical analysis.
- **Signal & confirmation:** individual interpretation matters.
- **Nison's notes:** "How you trade with candlesticks will depend on your trading philosophy, your risk adversity, and your temperament. There are very individual aspects."

### Market Personality
- **Type:** methodology principle.
- **Structure:** "each market has its own unique personality." Dark-cloud covers seem to work well in daily crude oil charts; hammers appear with relative frequency in weekly copper charts. By studying the market's personality, one can uncover the candlestick formations (and variations) that appear most and work best.
- **Recognition:** track which patterns appear most often in a given market and timeframe.
- **Psychology:** each market has its own character.
- **Signal & confirmation:** frequent appearance of a pattern in a market can validate it as a reliable signal for that market.
- **Nison's notes:** "The pattern of the market is like a person's face—no two are exactly alike."

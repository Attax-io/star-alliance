# Foundations — History & Construction

## Author's Path to Candlesticks
- Steve Nison wrote an introductory article on candlesticks in December 1989 that sparked immediate U.S. interest.
- He was one of the few Americans familiar with the centuries-old Japanese technique at that time.
- In early 1990 he wrote a short reference piece for his Chartered Market Technician (CMT) thesis on candlestick charts; Merrill Lynch, the publisher, received over 10,000 requests within a few months.
- **How he first encountered the method:** In 1987, while with a Japanese broker in her office, she pointed to a Japanese stock chart book and exclaimed "look, a window." She explained a "window" = a "gap" in Western technicals; "closing the window" = "filling in the gap." She also used terms like *doji* and *dark-cloud cover*.
- He then spent the next few years exploring, researching, and analyzing anything he could about candlestick charts.
- **Key sources in chronological order:**
  - A Japanese broker and his own hand-drawn candlestick charts.
  - The Market Technicians Association (MTA) library — a booklet from the Nippon Technical Analysts Association titled *Analysis of Stock Price in Japan*, containing ten pages on interpreting candlestick charts.
  - *The Japanese Chart of Charts* by Seiki Shimizu, translated by Greg Nicholson (Tokyo Futures Trading Publishing Co.) — ~70 pages on candlesticks in English. Nison calls it his "Rosetta Stone."
  - Direct correspondence with author Seiki Shimizu via fax through translator Greg Nicholson.
  - Japanese trader Morihiko Goto, whose family had used candlestick charts for generations.
  - Translations done by Richard Solberg of Language Services Unlimited, New York.
- Based on one estimate referenced in the text, there are probably fewer than 400 full-time Japanese-to-English translators in America (including part-time).

## Five Reasons Candlesticks Captured Global Attention
1. **Flexibility.** Candlestick charts are used by first-time chartists through seasoned professionals. They can be used alone or in combination with other technical tools — they are an addition to, not a replacement for, other techniques.
2. **Unused in the U.S. but centuries-old in the Far East.** The approach evolved from centuries of trial and error.
3. **Picturesque terminology.** Names such as "hanging-man line" spark interest; once learned, the terms are hard to forget.
4. **The Japanese know Western methods; the West did not know theirs.** The Japanese use a combination of candlesticks and Western tools — the West can do the same.
5. **A "win-win" situation.** The same data needed for bar charts (open, high, low, close) is needed for candlestick charts, so all standard Western analyses (moving averages, trendlines, Elliott Wave, retracements, etc.) still apply — but candlesticks add signals not available from bar charts.

## Book Structure
- **Part I:** Basics on constructing, reading, and interpreting over 50 candlestick chart lines and patterns.
- **Part II:** How to meld candlestick charts with Western technical analysis techniques — where the "true power" of candlecharts is manifested; this is how the author uses them.
- Drawn illustrations are representative examples only; real patterns need not look exactly like the exhibits to provide valid signals.
- Examples span futures, fixed-income, equity, London metal, and foreign exchange markets, and span intra-day, daily, weekly, and monthly time frames. Daily data is most often referenced.
- Two glossaries at the end: a candlestick glossary (with a visual glossary of all patterns) and a Western technical terms glossary.
- Variable definitions exist; Nison chose, in cases of conflict, the rule that increased the probability the pattern's forecast would be correct. Example: for the dark-cloud cover, one Japanese author says the open has to be above the prior close; Nison requires the market to open above the prior day's high (more bearish).
- "Surpassed" in Japanese material is equated by Nison with a close above.
- "High-price area" and "low-price area" are open to interpretation.
- The author references John Murphy's *Technical Analysis of the Futures Markets* (New York Institute of Finance) for further background on technical analysis.

## Subjectivity & Definitional Choices
- There is subjectivity in deciding whether a candlestick formation meets the guidelines for a given pattern — but this is no different from other charting techniques (e.g., is gold's $400 support broken by an intra-day touch or only by a close under $400?).
- Guidelines and principles are illustrated; real-world examples will not always match their ideal formations.
- The author states that technical analysis is more of an art than a science, so rigid rules should not be expected — just guideposts. He speculates that some Japanese vagueness may trace to the feudal era, when commoners faced samurai unpredictability.
- The book's content may be swayed by the author's trading philosophy (e.g., close-based versus intra-day signals).

## Limitations of Candlestick Analysis
- Patterns are subject to the user's interpretation.
- Extended experience in a market specialty will reveal which patterns and variations work best.
- Drawing individual candlestick chart lines requires a close; the trader may have to wait for the close (market-on-close order, an order placed a few minutes before the close, or to wait for the next day's open). The author notes this is not unique to candlesticks — many moving-average systems also need a closing price.
- **Workaround:** Hourly candlestick charts can be used to get a signal rather than waiting for the daily close (e.g., a potentially bullish daily pattern confirmed intraday by a bullish hourly candlestick indicator may justify buying even before the close, given a prevalent uptrend).
- The opening price is also important; equity traders without on-line quote machines may not see openings in newspapers — the author hopes newspapers will include stock openings as candlesticks become more common.
- **No price targets.** Candlestick charts do not provide price targets. Other methods (prior support/resistance, retracements, swing objectives, etc.) must be used.
- Some Japanese practitioners enter on a candlestick signal and stay until another candlestick pattern tells them to offset; Nison adds that patterns should always be viewed in context with what came before and other technical evidence.
- The book contains hundreds of charts with both patterns that worked and patterns that did not work; candlesticks are not infallible.

## The Importance of Technical Analysis
Nison states technical analysis is important for five reasons:
1. **Measures the emotional component of markets.** Fundamentals gauge supply/demand, P/E ratios, economic statistics, etc., but include no psychological component. Quote attributed to John Maynard Keynes: "There is nothing so disastrous as a rational investment policy in an irrational world." Technical analysis is the only mechanism to measure the "irrational" (emotional) component.
   - Illustrative story (from *The New Gatsbys* by Bob Tamarkin): During a drought in the Illinois Soybean Belt, a few drops of rain on a Chicago Board of Trade window triggered frantic sell/buy orders, and soybean prices collapsed — even though it was sunny 300 miles south in the soybean fields. "To the market nothing matters unless the market reacts to it. The game is played with the mind and the emotions."
   - Analogy: a piece of paper called "money" is exchanged for tangible goods only because of shared psychology; if that psychology evaporates, money becomes worthless.
2. **Disciplined trading.** Discipline mitigates the trader nemesis of emotion. Technicals put objectivity back in the driver's seat by providing entry/exit points, risk/reward ratios, and stop/out levels — fostering risk and money management. It is human nature to see the market as one wants to see it; technicals force stepping back.
   - Quote attributed to Jesse Livermore: "one can see the whole better when one sees it from a distance."
3. **Technicals can be a major market-moving factor.** Even if one does not fully believe in their use, they should be watched because they sometimes drive market moves.
4. **People remember prices from day to day.** Random walk says today's price has no bearing on tomorrow's, but this academic view omits the human component — people remember prices and act on them, and price also affects people's reactions. Thus, price itself is an important component in market analysis.
5. **Price action is the most direct and easily accessible method of seeing overall supply/demand relationships.** Unknown fundamental news is already in the price: those with advance knowledge buy or sell until current prices reflect their information, possibly discounting the event when it occurs. Current prices should reflect all available information, whether known by the public or a select few.

## Historical Background — Feudal Japan to Tokugawa
- **1500–1600 (Sengoku Jidai, "Age of Country at War"):** Japan was 60 provinces in constant war as daimyo ("big name" — feudal lords) fought for neighboring territories.
- **Early 1600s:** Three generals unified Japan over ~40 years — Nobunaga Oda, Hideyoshi Toyotomi, and Ieyasu Tokugawa. Japanese saying: "Nobunaga piled the rice, Hideyoshi kneaded the dough, and Tokugawa ate the cake." All three contributed, but Tokugawa, the last, became shogun.
- **Tokugawa Shogunate (1615–1867):** Family rule under Tokugawa; relative stability allowed expansion and ease in domestic trade; by the 17th century a national market replaced local isolated markets. This centralized marketplace indirectly led to the development of Japanese technical analysis.
- **Military/battlefield analogies pervade candlestick terminology** because trading requires the same skills as battle: strategy, psychology, competition, strategic withdrawals, and luck. Examples: "night and morning attacks," "advancing three soldiers pattern," "counter attack lines," "gravestone."

## Rise of Osaka as Commercial Center
- Hideyoshi Toyotomi regarded Osaka as Japan's capital and encouraged its growth as a commercial center.
- Easy sea access (at a time when land travel was slow, dangerous, costly) made Osaka a national depot; it became Japan's greatest city of commerce and finance, dubbed the "Kitchen of Japan."
- Osaka smoothed regional supply differences and contributed to price stability.
- Life in Osaka was permeated by the desire for profit (unlike other cities, where money-making was despised). The traditional Osaka greeting is "Mokarimakka" — "are you making a profit?"
- The social system had four classes (descending): Soldier, Farmer, Artisan, Merchant. It took until the 1700s for merchants to break down the social barrier.

## Yodoya Keian & the First Rice Exchange
- Yodoya Keian became a war merchant for Hideyoshi.
- Yodoya had extraordinary abilities in transporting, distributing, and setting the price of rice; his front yard became so important that the first rice exchange developed there.
- He became very wealthy — too wealthy. In 1705 the Bakufu (military government led by the shogun) confiscated his entire fortune on the charge of living in luxury not befitting his social rank. The Bakufu was apprehensive about merchant power.
- In 1642, certain officials and merchants tried to corner the rice market. Punishment: their children were executed, the merchants were exiled, and their wealth was confiscated.

## Dojima Rice Exchange & Birth of Futures
- The rice market originally developed in Yodoya's yard was institutionalized with the **Dojima Rice Exchange**, set up in the late 1600s in Osaka. Merchants graded the rice and bargained to set its price.
- Up until 1710 the exchange dealt in actual rice.
- After 1710 the exchange began to issue and accept rice warehouse receipts — "rice coupons." These became the first futures contracts ever traded.
- The Dojima Rice Exchange became the world's first futures exchange.
- **Why rice coupons were needed:** No currency standard existed (prior hard-currency attempts failed due to debasing of the coins); rice became the de facto medium of exchange. A daimyo needing money sent surplus rice to Osaka to be placed in a warehouse in his name and received a coupon. He could sell the coupon whenever he pleased. Daimyo often sold rice coupons against next season's rice tax delivery (taxes were paid in rice — usually 40% to 60% of the farmer's crop); sometimes multi-year crops were mortgaged.
- Rice coupons sold against future rice deliveries were the world's first futures contracts; coupons were also called "empty rice" coupons (rice not in physical possession).
- **Scale of activity:** In 1749, 110,000 bales of empty-rice coupons were traded in Osaka, while only 30,000 bales of rice existed in all of Japan.
- Rice brokerage became the foundation of Osaka's prosperity. There were more than 1,300 rice dealers.

## Munehisa Homma — "God of the Markets"
- Born 1724 into a wealthy family. Saying of the era: "I will never become a Homma, but I would settle to be a local lord."
- Gained control of the family business in 1750 and began trading at his local rice exchange in Sakata, a port city and rice collection/distribution area.
- Because he came from Sakata, the expression "Sakata's Rules" appears in Japanese candlestick literature — these refer to Homma.
- Youngest son, yet placed in charge of family assets (unusual; the eldest son usually inherited). This was probably due to his market savvy.
- Used his funds to trade rice futures at the Dojima Rice Exchange in Osaka.
- The Homma family had a huge rice farming estate, providing access to rice-market information; he also kept records of yearly weather conditions.
- To study investor psychology he analyzed rice prices back to the Yodoya-yard era.
- Set up his own communications system: at prearranged times, men on rooftops signaled by flags from Osaka to Sakata.
- After dominating Osaka he traded in the regional Edo (now Tokyo) exchange and used his insights to amass a huge fortune. He was said to have 100 consecutive winning trades.
- Folk song from Edo: "When it is sunny in Sakata (Homma's town), it is cloudy in Dojima (the Dojima Rice Exchange in Osaka) and rainy at Kuramae (the Kuramae exchange in Edo)." This reflected Homma's sway over the rice market.
- In later years he became a financial consultant to the government and was given the honored title of samurai. He died in 1803.
- His books on the markets — *Sakata Senho* and *Soba Sani No Den* — were said to have been written in the 1700s.
- His trading principles as applied to the rice markets evolved into the candlestick methodology currently used in Japan.
- Nison's transliteration note: Homma's first name is sometimes translated as Sokyu and his last name sometimes as Honma (same Japanese symbols, different translators). Nison uses the English translation used by the Nippon Technical Analysts Association.

## Construction — Mapping OHLC to a Candlestick
- The same data needed to draw a daily bar chart is needed for a daily candlestick chart: open, high, low, close.
- **Bar chart mapping:** The vertical line depicts the high and low of the session; the horizontal tick to the left is the open; the horizontal tick to the right is the close.
- **Candlestick mapping:** The same four data points are arranged differently — see pattern entries below.
- A daily candlestick line can be redrawn for any time frame (intra-day, daily, weekly, monthly); the principles are the same across time frames.

## Anatomy of a Candlestick Line
- **Real body:** The thick part of the candlestick line. It represents the range between that session's opening and closing prices.
- **Upper shadow:** Thin line above the real body; its peak is the session's high.
- **Lower shadow:** Thin line below the real body; its bottom is the session's low.
- **Shaven head:** A candlestick line with no upper shadow.
- **Shaven bottom:** A candlestick line with no lower shadow.
- **Visual flavor:** Individual lines often resemble candles and their wicks, hence "candlestick charts."
- **Japanese view of the components:** The real body is the essential price movement; the shadows are usually considered extraneous price fluctuations.

## Color Conventions
- **Black real body:** The close was lower than the open (down session / "yin" in Chinese, *in-sen* in Japanese — "black line").
- **White real body (empty/hollow):** The close was higher than the open (up session / "yang" in Chinese, *yo-sen* in Japanese — "white line").
- Classical Japanese candlestick chart colors are red and black; red can replace the white candlestick (useful for computer displays), but photocopies and most computer printouts will not be useful because all real bodies would come out as black.
- The Japanese emphasize the relationship between the open and the close because they are the two most emotionally charged points of the trading day.

## Significance of the Open
- Japanese proverb: "the first hour of the morning is the rudder of the day" — the open is the rudder of the trading session and the first clue about that day's direction.
- All overnight news and rumors are filtered and joined into a single point in time at the open.
- The more anxious a trader, the earlier he wants to trade — so on the open, shorts may scramble for cover, potential longs may want to emphatically buy, hedgers may need a new position or to exit an old one.
- The open provides a benchmark from which buyers and sellers can later gauge the session.
- The open provides an early view of the "battlefield" with a provisional indication of friendly and opposing troops.
- **Morning attack:** A large trader pushing the market on the open by executing a large buy or sell order (another military analogy in the candlestick lexicon).

## Significance of the Close
- Margin calls in the futures markets are based on the close, so heavy emotional involvement attaches to how the market closes.
- Many technicians wait for a close to confirm a breakout from a significant chart point.
- Many computer trading systems (e.g., moving average systems) are based on closes; this is why a surge of activity often occurs in the final minutes of a session.
- **Night attack:** A large buy or sell order pushed into the market at or near the close with the intention of affecting the close.

## Candlestick Terminology and Market Emotion
- The names of candlestick patterns form a colorful mechanism describing the emotional health of the market at the time the pattern forms.
- Pattern names themselves signal the market's emotional state — e.g., "hanging man" and "dark-cloud cover" are both bearish and their names convey an unhealthy market state.
- An emotionally unhealthy state at the pattern's appearance does not preclude the market from becoming healthy again; it does mean longs should take defensive measures, or new shorts could be initiated depending on the general trend and other factors.
- The descriptive names are easier to remember and recall as bullish or bearish — e.g., the "evening star" (appears before darkness) is bearish; the "morning star" (appears just before sunrise) is bullish.

## Long Black Candlestick
- **Type:** Bearish · single-line (one candle) reflection of a bearish period.
- **Structure:** Long black real body; market opened near its high and closed near its low.
- **Recognition:** A real body that is black (filled in), long relative to typical bodies in that market, and whose close sits near the session's low while the open sits near the session's high.
- **Psychology:** Sellers dominated from the open onward, driving the market down through the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Reflects a bearish session; should be read in context with surrounding price action and other technical evidence.
- **Nison's notes:** Considered a basic bearish building block; real-world examples need not look exactly like the ideal drawn version.

## Long White Candlestick
- **Type:** Bullish · single-line reflection of a bullish period.
- **Structure:** Long white (empty) real body; market opened near its low and closed near the high of the session, with a wide range.
- **Recognition:** A real body that is white (hollow/empty), long relative to typical bodies in that market, and whose close sits near the session's high while the open sits near the session's low.
- **Psychology:** Buyers dominated from the open onward, driving the market up through the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Reflects a bullish session; should be read in context with surrounding price action and other technical evidence.
- **Nison's notes:** The opposite mirror of the long black candlestick; a basic bullish building block.

## Spinning Top
- **Type:** Neutral · single-line · small real body.
- **Structure:** Small real body; can be white or black. Shadow size does not matter — it is the diminutive size of the real body that defines a spinning top.
- **Recognition:** A candlestick with a small real body, indicating the open and close are close together.
- **Psychology:** A tug of war between bulls and bears; in lateral trading bands it is neutral.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Neutral on its own. Spinning tops do become important when part of certain formations — notably as part of star and harami patterns.
- **Nison's notes:** A neutral line in isolation, but its role changes when embedded in multi-candle formations.

## Doji Line
- **Type:** Neutral or potentially significant reversal component · single-line · no real body.
- **Structure:** No real body — open and close are the same or very close to the same. Shadow lengths can vary.
- **Recognition:** A session where the open and close are at the same price or nearly the same (e.g., two- or three-thirty-seconds in bonds, a quarter cent in grains, and so on).
- **Psychology:** Indecision / equilibrium between bulls and bears; open and close meet at essentially the same level.
- **Signal & confirmation:** So important that an entire chapter is devoted to them (Chapter 8, "The Magic Doji," referenced but not included in this excerpt).
- **Nison's notes:** The visual cue is the absence of a real body, leaving horizontal lines; classification as a particular doji sub-type is treated at length later in the book.

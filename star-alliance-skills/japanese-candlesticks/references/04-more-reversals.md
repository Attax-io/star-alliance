# More Reversals — Harami, Tweezers, Crows, Counterattack, Towers

## Harami Pattern
- **Type:** Bullish or bearish · reversal (secondary/weak) · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A small real body that is contained within a prior relatively long real body. The long candlestick is the "mother" and the small candlestick is the "baby"/"fetus." The color of the second session is unimportant; in most instances, however, the real bodies are oppositely colored. The size of the shadows is usually not important.
- **Recognition:** The reverse of the engulfing pattern — a long real body precedes, and a small real body follows. The small real body's open/close range must lie within the prior tall real body's open/close range. No specific quantitative rule defines "small"; it is subjective, but the smaller the second real body, the more potent the pattern.
- **Psychology:** Displays a disparity about the market's health. After a bull move, the long white real body's vitality is followed by a small real body's uncertainty — bulls' upward drive has weakened, so a trend reversal is possible. During a bear move, heavy selling pressure reflected by a long black real body is followed by the second day's vacillation — the small real body is an alert that the bears' power has diminished.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A brake has been applied to the market; the immediate preceding trend should end and the market will often come to a lull. After a harami the market usually eases into a congestion band. The pattern does not preclude a resumption of the prior trend. Confirmation example: a buy would not be recommended until indecision had been resolved via a close above the highs of the harami.
- **Nison's notes:** Usually not as significant a reversal signal as the hammer, hanging man, or engulfing patterns. It is more effective at warning of trend changes — especially at market tops. Useful even if there is no evident trend before it appears. The decisive feature is the relative size of the second real body, not the shadows. Comparable to the Western inside day (a Western inside session requires the high and low be within the prior session's range and is usually thought of as having little or no forecasting importance), but the harami requires a narrow opening and closing range to be within the prior wide opening and closing range.

## Harami Cross
- **Type:** Bullish or bearish · major reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A doji for the second day of the harami pattern instead of a small real body. The first candle is a long real body (white or black). The doji's open/close range is contained within the prior tall real body's open/close range.
- **Recognition:** A doji preceded by a long real body. Sometimes referred to as the petrifying pattern.
- **Psychology:** Combines the harami's trend-decay message with the potent doji's indecision signal — a strong trend is suddenly confronted with total market uncertainty.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Where the regular harami is not a major reversal pattern, the harami cross is a major reversal pattern. A harami cross occurring after a very long white candlestick is a pattern a long trader ignores at his own peril. More effective at tops than at bottoms, though it can call bottoms as well. Confirmation example: a hammer-like session after the doji of the harami cross that successfully tests the recent lows gave further proof of a bottom.
- **Nison's notes:** Carries more significance than a regular harami pattern because of the doji's extreme ambivalence (the smaller the real body, the greater the ambivalence and the more likely a trend reversal). The second line's color is unimportant.

## Tweezers Tops and Bottoms
- **Type:** Reversal (weak by itself) · two or more candlestick lines.
- **Structure:** Two or more candlestick lines with matching highs (tweezers top, in a rising market) or matching lows (tweezers bottom, in a falling market). The tweezers can be composed of real bodies, shadows, and/or doji. They occur on nearby or consecutive sessions.
- **Recognition:** The highs match (top) or the lows match (bottom). For the bearish top view to prevail, the market should not close above the tweezers top. For a bottom, the prior period's low is successfully tested.
- **Psychology:** Prices reach the same high twice (top) or test the same low twice (bottom), indicating the prior trend is being challenged. Confirmation comes from accompanying candlestick evidence.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Not a vital reversal signal on their own. Take on extra importance when they occur after an extended move or contain other bearish (for a top reversal) or bullish (for a bottom reversal) candlestick signals. On weekly or monthly charts, tweezers tops and bottoms made by consecutive candlesticks could be important reversal patterns even without other candlestick confirmation because a low/high made the preceding session that is successfully tested this session could be an important base for a rally. Less important on a daily chart, where a low made yesterday that is successfully tested today is less likely to be a base for a rally.
- **Nison's notes:** Named because they are compared to the two prongs of a tweezers. Examples given in the text combine tweezers with other candlestick signals.

### Tweezers Top and Harami Cross
- **Type:** Bearish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** In an uptrend, a long white line is followed by a doji whose high matches the prior session's high. This two-candlestick pattern is a harami cross with the same high.
- **Signal:** Can be a significant reversal signal because it combines the harami cross's major reversal implication with the matching-high tweezers.

### Tweezers Top and Hanging Man
- **Type:** Bearish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long white candlestick followed by a hanging-man line during the next session, with the highs matching.
- **Signal:** If the market opens under the hanging-man's real body, odds are strong that a top has been reached. The market should not close above the tweezers top in order for the bearish view to prevail. This two-line mixture can also be considered a harami, making it a top reversal pattern during an uptrend.

### Tweezers Top and Shooting Star
- **Type:** Bearish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long white candlestick and a second bearish session that touches the prior session's high then falls.
- **Signal:** Although not a true shooting star (the real body is not above the prior real body), the second line is bearish based on the price action which creates it: the market opens near its lows, rallies to the prior session's high, then closes near its low for the session. This would also be considered a harami pattern.

### Tweezers Top and Dark-Cloud Cover
- **Type:** Bearish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A variation on the dark-cloud cover. The second day opens above the prior day's close (instead of above the prior day's high). The black candlestick day's high touches the prior period's high and then falls.
- **Signal:** Could be viewed as a combination of a dark-cloud cover and a tweezers top.

### Tweezers Bottom and Hammer
- **Type:** Bullish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A hammer session that successfully tests the prior long black candlestick's lows.
- **Signal:** The hammer, and the successful test of support, proves that the sellers are losing control of the market. This two-line combination can also be viewed as a harami, providing another reason to view this action as important support.

### Tweezers Bottom and Piercing Pattern
- **Type:** Bullish reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A variation on the bullish piercing line with a tweezers bottom added. A true piercing line would open under the prior day's low; in this variation, it opened under the prior day's close.
- **Signal:** Combines the piercing line's bottom reversal implications with the matching-low tweezers.

## Belt-Hold Lines
- **Type:** Reversal · one individual candlestick line.
- **Structure (Bullish):** A strong white candlestick that opens on the low of the day (or with a very small lower shadow) and moves higher for the rest of the day. Also called a white opening shaven bottom.
- **Structure (Bearish):** A long black candlestick that opens on the high of the session (or within a few ticks of the high) and continues lower through the session. Sometimes called a black opening shaven head.
- **Recognition:** The open is at (or extremely near) the high (bearish) or low (bullish) of the session, with a long real body in the trend direction.
- **Psychology:** A sumo wrestling term — yorikiri — "pushing your opponent out of the ring while holding onto his belt" — captures the dominance of the trend on the open and its persistence through the day.
- **Signal & confirmation:** If the market is at a low price area and a long bullish belt hold appears, it forecasts a rally. If prices are high, the appearance of a bearish belt hold is a top reversal. A close above a black bearish belt-hold line should mean a resumption of the uptrend. A close under the white bullish belt-hold line implies a renewal of selling pressure. A bullish belt-hold line can confirm a tweezers bottom if it maintains the prior week's lows.
- **Nison's notes:** The longer the height of the belt-hold candlestick line, the more significant it becomes. Belt-hold lines are also more important if they have not appeared for a while. Back-to-back bearish belt holds signal sharp weakness (a sharp but brief selloff in one cited example, ended by a bullish morning star).

## Upside-Gap Two Crows
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · three candle lines.
- **Structure:** The market is in an uptrend. A long white candlestick (the real body preceding the first black candlestick) is followed by two black candlesticks whose real bodies gapped up away from the white body's real body. The ideal upside-gap two crows has the second black real body opening above the first black real body's open, and closing under the first black candlestick's close. The first black candlestick still closes above the prior day's close, giving bulls temporary succor.
- **Recognition:** An opening gap between the first black real body and the prior white real body, with a second black real body that opens higher than the first black's open and closes below the first black's close. Two black candlesticks (the "crows") are analogized to crows peering ominously down from a tree branch.
- **Psychology:** The market opens higher on an opening gap, but the new highs fail to hold and the market forms a black candlestick. The third session paints a more bearish portrait with another new high and another failure to hold these highs into the close — and more negatively, it closes under the prior day's close. If the market is so strong, why did the new highs fail to hold and why did the market close lower? If the next (fourth) session prices fail to regain high ground, expect lower prices.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal. Nison suggests placing a stop on a close above the second black candlestick's high.
- **Nison's notes:** The pattern is a bearish three-candle reversal. The first three candlesticks of the mat-hold pattern are similar to the upside-gap two crows (the mat hold can have two, three, or four black candlesticks; the upside-gap two crows can have either two or three small black candlesticks, with the appearance of the white candlestick at the end being the main difference, turning the pattern bullish). Both patterns are relatively rare.

## Mat-Hold Pattern
- **Type:** Bullish · continuation · five (or more) candle lines.
- **Structure:** Occurs in a bull market. The first three candlesticks are similar to the upside-gap two crows (a strong white candle, a black candlestick gapping higher, then small black candlesticks), but another black candlestick follows before the final white candlestick completes the pattern. The pattern can have two, three, or four black candlesticks.
- **Recognition:** A strong white candle followed by a black candlestick that gapped higher, two (or more) more small black candlesticks, and finally a white candlestick.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish continuation. If the next candlestick is white and gaps above the last black candlestick's upper shadow or closes above the last black candlestick's high, then buying is warranted.
- **Nison's notes:** One of the few candlestick continuation patterns. Related to the upside-gap two crows, with the appearance of the white candlestick at the end being the main difference (turning the pattern bullish). Both the upside-gap two crows and the mat-hold are relatively rare.

## Three Black Crows
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · three candle lines.
- **Structure:** Three declining consecutive black candlesticks. The three lines should close at, or near, their lows. Each of the openings should also be within the prior session's real body. The analyst would also like to see the real body of the first candlestick of the three crows under the prior white session's high.
- **Recognition:** Three black candlesticks appearing at high-price levels or after a mature advance. Sometimes called three-winged crows. Likened to the image of a group of crows sitting ominously in a tall dead tree.
- **Psychology:** The Japanese have an expression, "bad news has wings" — an appropriate saying for the three-winged crow pattern.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Presage lower prices if they appear at high-price levels or after a mature advance.
- **Nison's notes:** If the open of the second and third black candlesticks are at, or very near, the close of the prior black candlestick, this is referred to as identical three crows. It is regarded as especially bearish, but it is a very rare pattern.

## Counterattack Lines
- **Type:** Reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** Opposite colored candlesticks that have the same close. Composed of a bullish and bearish variant.
- **Recognition:** A two-candle pattern where the second candle's close returns to the prior candle's close.
- **Psychology:** On the opening of the second day, the market has moved strongly in the direction of the original trend. Then, by the close, it moves back to unchanged from the prior session — the original trend has been bridled.
- **Nison's notes:** The closes do not have to be identical in order to make the pattern valid.

### Bullish Counterattack Line
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long black candlestick during a decline, followed by a session that opens sharply lower and then springs back, closing at the prior session's close. The market opens sharply lower and then springs back to the previous close, while an in-neck line opens slightly lower and then moves back to the prior close.
- **Recognition:** The second candle is a long white candlestick whose close matches the prior black candle's close.
- **Signal:** The prior downtrend has been bridled. Comparable to the bullish piercing line (the bullish counterattack line does not usually move into the prior session's white real body; it just gets back to the prior session's close, while the piercing pattern's second line pushes well into the black real body). The piercing pattern is a more significant bottom reversal than is this bullish counterattack line. Comparable to the in-neck pattern, but the white bullish counterattack line is a longer candlestick than the white candlestick of the in-neck line. The bullish counterattack line should be respected, though it is weaker than the piercing line.
- **Nison's notes:** The opens are robustly lower, demonstrating that the surprise reversal comes despite the dominant direction. The pattern can be useful even when the close after the bullish counterattack merely signals that the prior downtrend was over as the trend went from down to sideways, rather than reversing direction.

### Bearish Counterattack Line
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · two candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long white candlestick, followed by a session that opens higher (gapping up) and then falls, closing at the prior day's close.
- **Recognition:** The second candle is a long black candlestick whose close matches the prior white candle's close.
- **Signal:** A top reversal pattern in that it should stall the prior rally. Related to the dark-cloud cover: the bearish counterattack, like the dark-cloud cover, opens above the prior day's high. Unlike the dark-cloud cover, the close does not go into the prior day's white candlestick. Thus, the dark-cloud cover sends a stronger top reversal signal than does the bearish counterattack line.
- **Nison's notes:** The opening gaps higher, demonstrating the bears' decisive comeback after the opening surge.

## Three Mountain Tops
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal (longer-term topping pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** If the market backs off from a high three times or makes three attempts at a high, it is deemed a three mountain top. Similar to the Western triple top.
- **Recognition:** Three peaks (or three attempts at a high). Some Japanese technicians view the three mountain as three attempts at new highs, like three waves up, where the third wave up is supposed to be a crest — after the third failed push, the bulls surrender. Others view the three mountains as repeating tests of the same price peaks.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A major top. The high point of the final mountain should be confirmed with a bearish candlestick indicator (for example, a doji or dark-cloud cover).
- **Nison's notes:** The emphasis on triple tops and bottoms by the Japanese probably has to do with the importance of the number three in the Japanese culture. The Japanese would not necessarily see anything special about the three peaks; the Western view would be that double tops and, more rarely, tops which have been tested four times can be just as significant as triple tops. "Three times lucky" is a saying expressing the Japanese belief in the auspiciousness of three. (The number four is viewed as foreboding because in Japanese the pronunciation for the number four and the word death are the same.)

## Three Buddha Tops
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal (longer-term topping pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** A three mountain top in which the central mountain is the highest. Named because, in Buddhist temples, there is a large central Buddha with smaller Buddhas (saints) on both sides.
- **Recognition:** Three peaks where the middle peak is the highest — the head and shoulders pattern analogy.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A perfect analogy to a head and shoulders pattern. A harami (black real body within the prior white real body) at the peak of the high central mountain provides additional confirmation.
- **Nison's notes:** Although the three Buddha top is an analogy to the Western head and shoulders pattern, the theory about the Japanese three Buddha pattern was used over a hundred years before the head and shoulders was known in America. The earliest reference to a head and shoulders pattern in the United States was by Richard Schabacker in the 1930s. (Schabacker was Edwards's father-in-law; much of the material in Edwards and Magee's Technical Analysis of Stock Trends is based on Schabacker's work.) As a Japanese proverb expresses, "The tone of a bird's song is the same everywhere."

## Three River Bottoms
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal (longer-term bottoming pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** The opposite of the three mountain top. Occurs when the market tests a bottom level three times. The peak of the troughs should be exceeded to confirm a bottom. The equivalent of the Western head and shoulders bottom (also called an inverted head and shoulders) is called the modified three river bottom pattern or the inverted three Buddha pattern.
- **Recognition:** Three troughs. In order for the three river bottoms to provide a buy signal, there should be a close via a white candlestick above the peaks of the troughs.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A major bottom. Bullish candlestick indications at the troughs (e.g., hammers, morning stars, piercing lines) add weight. Once the bulls gapped above a downward sloping resistance line, the trend turned up.
- **Nison's notes:** The Japanese view gaps (windows) as continuation patterns: a gap higher is bullish and a gap lower is bearish. The pattern often includes a doji star and tweezers bottom building a platform for another leg higher after a selloff.

## Inverted Three Buddha / Modified Three River Bottom
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal (longer-term bottoming pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** The equivalent of the Western head and shoulders bottom (also called an inverted head and shoulders), with three troughs where the central trough is the lowest.
- **Recognition:** Three troughs in which the central trough is the lowest. Confirmation via a close above the peaks of the troughs, which then may convert to support on subsequent selloffs.
- **Nison's notes:** In one cited example, a three Buddha top formed subsequently. Each of the bottoms had a bullish candlestick indication: a hammer at the first, a hammer that was part of a morning star at the second (with the morning star rally ending with a dark-cloud cover), and a piercing line (almost a bullish engulfing pattern) at the third.

## Unique Three River Bottom
- **Type:** Bullish · bottom reversal · three candle lines.
- **Structure:** An extremely rare pattern. Its first line is an extended black candlestick, the second line is a black real body candlestick that closes higher than the first candlestick's close, and the third line is a very small real body white candlestick. Closest relative in candlesticks is the evening star pattern.
- **Recognition:** A long black candle, then a black candle closing higher (but still black), then a small white candle.
- **Psychology:** The third (small) line displays a market whose selling pressure has dried up.
- **Nison's notes:** Closest relative is the evening star pattern. Extremely rare.

## Dumpling Tops
- **Type:** Bearish · reversal (longer-term topping pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** Usually has small real bodies as the market forms a convex pattern. The same as the Western rounded bottom top (a rounding top). The dumpling top should have a downside window as proof of a top.
- **Recognition:** A convex (rounded) top made of small real bodies, with confirmation by a downside gap (downside window).
- **Signal & confirmation:** Confirmation of a dumpling top occurs when the market gaps down. A doji at the peak with a downside window confirms the pattern. A black candlestick after the window being a black belt-hold line is another reason for a bearish outlook.
- **Nison's notes:** The towers usually have more volatile candlesticks than the dumpling tops. The major differences from tower tops are that the dumpling top has windows; towers have long real bodies before and after the market turns. All these patterns (tower top, dumpling top, tower bottom, fry pan bottom) are viewed as major reversal patterns — do not worry about whether a top is a tower top or a dumpling top.

## Fry Pan Bottoms
- **Type:** Bullish · reversal (longer-term bottoming pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure:** A market that is bottoming and whose price action forms a concave design, then a window to the upside opens. The same appearance as a Western rounded bottom, but the Japanese fry pan bottom should have a window in an upmove in order to confirm the bottom.
- **Recognition:** A concave (rounded) bottom followed by confirmation from an upside gap (upside window).
- **Signal & confirmation:** A window in early May substantiated that a fry pan bottom had been put into place. Strong white belt-hold lines can also act as bullish confirmation even if no exact window forms (a missed window by a few ticks can still count alongside a strong white belt-hold line as confirmation).
- **Nison's notes:** The towers usually have more volatile candlesticks than the fry pan bottoms. The major differences from tower bottoms are that the fry pan bottom has windows; towers have long real bodies before and after the market turns. All these patterns (tower top, dumpling top, tower bottom, fry pan bottom) are viewed as major reversal patterns — do not worry about whether a bottom is a tower bottom or a fry pan bottom.

## Tower Tops and Tower Bottoms
- **Type:** Reversal (longer-term pattern) · multi-candle.
- **Structure (Tower Top):** Occurs while the market is in an uptrend. A strong white candlestick (or a series of white candlesticks) appears. The market's rise then slows and the highs start falling. Completed with the appearance of one or more large black candlesticks. The long candlesticks resemble tall towers — hence the name.
- **Structure (Tower Bottom):** Develops at low price levels. After one or more long black candlesticks there is a short-term lull. Then one or more large white candlesticks emerge. This creates a bottom with towers on both sides — long candlesticks on the way down and long candlesticks on the way up.
- **Recognition:** Tall candlesticks (real bodies) on both sides of a turn — left tower and right tower — with intervening consolidation (in a tower bottom, narrow/sideways candlesticks; in a tower top, slowing ascent and falling highs).
- **Signal & confirmation:** A major reversal pattern. The three black candlesticks of a tower top can also be three black crows.
- **Nison's notes:** Closest Western analogy is the spike, or V, reversal — the market is in a strong trend and then abruptly reverses to a new trend. Similar to the dumpling top and fry pan bottom; the major differences are that the towers have long real bodies before and after the market turns and the dumpling top and fry pan bottom have windows. The towers usually also have more volatile candlesticks than the dumpling tops or fry pan bottoms. All these patterns are viewed as major reversal patterns.

## The Importance of the Number Three in Candlestick Analysis
- The emphasis on triple tops and bottoms by the Japanese probably has to do with the importance of the number three in the Japanese culture. Westerners would not necessarily see anything special about three peaks; the Western view is that double tops and, more rarely, tops which have been tested four times can be just as significant as triple tops. The Japanese think differently.
- In pre-modern Japan, the number three had an almost mystical association. There is a saying "three times lucky" that expresses this belief. Parenthetically, while the number three is regarded as lucky, the number four is viewed as a foreboding figure because in Japanese the pronunciation for the number four and the word death are the same.
- Specifics of the frequency of three in candlestick charting include:
  - Three white soldiers that presage a rally.
  - Three black crows that portend a price fall.
  - Top patterns include the three mountain top and its variation; the three Buddha pattern.
  - Three river bottoms.
  - Three windows (define the extent of a move).
  - Three methods.
  - Three candlestick patterns including the morning and evening stars.
- The Japanese believe that if a window (during a rising market) is not closed within three days the market will rally.

## Japanese View of Gaps (Windows)
- Gaps are called windows by Japanese technicians.
- The Japanese view gaps (windows) as continuation patterns: a gap higher is bullish and a gap lower is bearish.
- The dumpling top should have a downside window as proof of a top.
- The Japanese fry pan bottom should have a window in an upmove in order to confirm the bottom.
- The Japanese believe that if a window (during a rising market) is not closed within three days the market will rally.

## The Quote from John Murphy on the Number Three in Western Technical Analysis
- Quoted from John Murphy's Technical Analysis of the Futures Markets: "It's interesting to note how often the number three shows up in the study of technical analysis and the important role it plays in so many technical approaches. For example, the fan principle uses three lines; major bull and bear markets have three phases (Dow theory and Elliott Wave Theory); there are three kinds of gaps; some of the more commonly known reversal patterns, such as the triple top and the head and shoulders, have three prominent peaks; there are three different classifications of trend (major, secondary, and minor) and three trend directions (up, down, and sideways); among the generally accepted continuation patterns, there are three types of triangles — the symmetrical, ascending, and descending; there are three principle sources of information — price, volume and open interest. For whatever the reason, the number three plays a very prominent role throughout the entire field of technical analysis."
- Nison's point: Murphy was referring to Western technical analysis, but "the number three plays a very prominent role" is especially true of Japanese candlestick analysis.

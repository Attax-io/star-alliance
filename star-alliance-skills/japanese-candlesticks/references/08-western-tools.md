# Candlesticks with Western Tools — Trendlines, Retracements, MAs, Oscillators, Volume

## Support and Resistance Lines

- **Upward Sloping Support Line:** Constructed by connecting at least two reaction lows. Demonstrates buyers are more aggressive than sellers as demand steps in at higher lows; indicative of a market trending higher.
- **Downward Sloping Resistance Line:** Constructed by connecting at least two reaction highs. Shows sellers are more aggressive than buyers, with sellers willing to sell at lower highs; reflects a market trending lower.
- **Potency Factors:** The strength of a support or resistance line depends on (1) the number of times the line has been successfully tested, (2) the amount of volume at each test, and (3) the time the line has been in force.
- **Candlestick Advantage:** Whatever can be done with a bar chart can be done with a candlestick chart — e.g., a basic head and shoulders neckline can be drawn on a candlestick chart as easily as a bar chart. Candlesticks add depth to trendline analysis.
- **Upward Sloping Resistance Line:** Trendline that connects a series of higher highs. Not as popular as a downward sloping resistance line, but useful for longs. When the market approaches this line, longs should take defensive measures (taking some profits, moving up protective stops, or selling calls). Pullbacks should be temporary since the major trend is up, but failure from this line could be a tentative indication of a new downtrend.
- **Downward Sloping Support Line:** Trendline with a negative slope; indicative of a downtrend. When the market successfully holds this kind of support line, shorts should take defensive measures in preparation of a price bounce.

## Protective Stops

- **Purpose:** Technicals should be used to set up risk/reward parameters, providing a risk and money management approach. Defining risk means using protective stops to help protect against unanticipated adverse price movements.
- **Placement Rule:** A stop should be placed at the time of the original trade, when one is most objective. Stay in the position only if the market performs according to expectations. Exit if subsequent price action contradicts or fails to confirm expectations.
- **Key Facts:** (1) All long-term trends begin as short-term moves. (2) There is no room for hope in the market — the market goes its own way.
- **Mindset Rules:**
  - "Lose your opinion, not your money."
  - "Be proud of the ability to catch mistakes early."
  - "Good traders will not hold views too firmly."
  - The one thing worse than being wrong is staying wrong.
  - Think of a stop as a cost of doing business.
- **Stops as Risk Control:** Warren Buffett's two rules: (1) capital preservation, and (2) don't forget rule 1. Stops are synonymous with rule 1.
- **Battlefield Analogy:** Each trade is a battle. The trader's goal is to preserve capital and equanimity. Sometimes temporary tactical retreats are required. Japanese saying: "a hook's well lost to catch a salmon."

## Spring Pattern

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · multiple sessions (false-breakout formation).
- **Structure:** A prior low is pierced (new lows made), but those new lows fail to hold and prices spring back above the broken support area.
- **Recognition:** Prices trade below a defined support level (creating a new low), then reverse back above the old support area. New lows could not hold.
- **Psychology:** Bears briefly break the bulls' defensive line, but the bulls reassert control and reclaim the territory.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Buy signal when prices push back above the old lows. The objective is a retest of the congestion zone's upper band. The stop is placed under the lows made on the day of the spring. Effectiveness comes from a clear target (opposite end of the trading range) and clear stop (the new low made with the false breakout). Especially powerful when a bullish candlestick indicator coincides.
- **Nison's notes:** Springs are effective because they offer a defined target and a defined stop. Best applied when markets are in a lateral "wa" (tranquility) state. Estimating markets are in nontrending mode as much as 70% of the time.

## Upthrust Pattern

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · multiple sessions (false-breakout formation).
- **Structure:** An unsustained penetration of resistance — prices push above a prior high, then return back under the old highs that had been "penetrated."
- **Recognition:** Prices exceed resistance but fail to hold the new high; new highs do not hold.
- **Psychology:** Bulls briefly break the bears' defensive line, but the bears reassert control and reclaim the territory.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Sell/short signal on the failed breakout. The price target is a retest of the lower end of the congestion band. Stop is placed above the new high. Especially powerful when joined with a bearish candlestick indicator (e.g., shooting star, bearish engulfing).
- **Nison's notes:** The opposite of a spring. Works because it provides a clear target and clear stop. Shooting stars are sometimes part of an upthrust; this combination is a powerful incentive to sell.

## "Wa" — Lateral Range Concept

- **Definition:** Japanese word for tranquility and calm. Markets bounded in a horizontal trading zone are said to be in a state of "wa."
- **Application:** In this state, neither bulls nor bears are in charge. Upthrusts and springs are particularly useful tools in such environments, providing attractive entry points.
- **Frequency:** Markets are estimated to be in a nontrending mode as much as 70% of the time.

## Scouting Parties and Beachhead Analogy

- **Concept:** A "scouting party" (Nison's term, not a candlestick expression) is a small test of the opposing side's resolve. Big traders, commercial accounts, or locals may send such tests.
- **Bullish Beachhead:** If a bullish scouting party can set up camp in enemy territory (close above resistance for a few days), a beachhead is made. Fresh bull troops should join, and the market should move higher. As long as the beachhead is maintained (the market holds the old resistance area as new support), the bulls will have control.
- **Failed Beachhead:** If the bulls only succeed intra-day in pushing above resistance but cannot close above it (no beachhead), the bulls retreat, often back to the bottom of the congestion band.

## Change of Polarity Principle

- **Definition:** Old support becomes new resistance; old resistance becomes new support. Sometimes described as "a red lacquer dish needs no decoration" — the principle embodies simple beauty.
- **Potency Factors:** (1) The number of times the old support/resistance levels have been tested, and (2) the volume and/or open interest on each test.
- **Three Groups of Buyers at Old Support (when support will become resistance after break):**
  - **Group 1:** Those who were waiting for the market to stabilize after a prior selloff, now having a point at which the market found support. New longs pulled in on a successful test of that support.
  - **Group 2:** Those who were previously long but were stopped out during the selloff. They believe they were right about the bull market, just timed the original purchase incorrectly. They wait for a pullback to support to re-enter, wanting vindication of their original view.
  - **Group 3:** Those who bought at the original support levels. They see a rally, may want to add to their position at a "good price." A pullback to support gives them that good price — bringing in more buyers.
- **Why It Works:** When support breaks, the original buyers at those levels are now in losing trades. They will use rallies back to the level (their original entry) to exit with the least damage. Thus, the original buyers become sellers — the main reason old support becomes new resistance. The same rationale, reversed, is why old resistance becomes support.
- **Key Psychological Insight:** The most important price on any chart is the price at which the trader entered the market. People become strongly, keenly, and emotionally attached to the price at which they bought or sold. The more trading at a price area, the more people emotionally committed to that level.
- **Candlestick Confirmation:** The change of polarity rule is especially powerful when melded with candlestick indicators (e.g., a doji after a tall white real body at the new resistance confirms trouble).

## Bullish Morning Star

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long black real body, followed by a small real body (the "star") that gaps down, followed by a white real body that pushes well into the first session's black body.
- **Recognition:** Three sessions; the third white candlestick should push well into the first session's black body. If the third candlestick does not push well into the first body, it is a "variation" of the pattern.
- **Psychology:** Bears in control, then indecision, then bulls take over.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal signal. Strengthened by other technical evidence (e.g., positive stochastic divergence, %K line crossing above %D line). If a variation occurs, scan other technical evidence before deciding the strength of the signal.
- **Nison's notes:** Sometimes the first white candlestick of a variation may not push as deeply into the first body as ideal. Stochastic indicators can provide added proof.

## Bullish Hammer

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A small real body at the upper end of the trading range with a long lower shadow (and little or no upper shadow). A "hammer-like line" is a variation.
- **Recognition:** Small body near the top of the range with a long lower shadow. May be a white or black candle.
- **Psychology:** Bears tried to push prices lower but failed; bulls recovered to close near the high.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. Confirmation often comes from a successful test of a support line, the hammer's low being used as a protective stop-out level, or being part of a spring.
- **Nison's notes:** When a hammer coincides with a successful test of a support line, a bottom reversal signal is generated. The hammer's low can be used as a protective stop.

## Hanging Man

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A small real body at the upper end of the trading range with a long lower shadow. Identical in shape to a hammer but appears at the top of an uptrend.
- **Recognition:** Small body near top of range with a long lower shadow during an uptrend. A "variation" has a lower shadow that is not long enough to be classic.
- **Psychology:** Bears are starting to test the bulls' resolve during a rally.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Warning of an end to the uptrend. A hanging man the day prior to a market high often gave warning of the end of the uptrend. Confirmation often comes from a shooting star, bearish engulfing pattern, or failure at a resistance line.
- **Nison's notes:** A variation of a hanging man (lower shadow not long enough to be classic) can still be a valid warning, especially when combined with other tools like RSI or bearish divergence.

## Shooting Star

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A small real body at the lower end of the trading range with a long upper shadow. A white candlestick with a long upper shadow at resistance is described in the text.
- **Recognition:** Small body near the bottom of the range with a long upper shadow, especially at resistance or in an uptrend. "Shooting-star line with its attendant very long upper shadow and small real body."
- **Psychology:** Bulls tried to push higher but failed; bears pushed the price back down.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal, especially at resistance. Shooting stars are sometimes part of an upthrust, in which case they are a powerful incentive to sell. Confirmation often comes from a bearish engulfing pattern, hanging man, or other bearish indicator.
- **Nison's notes:** "Shooting stars are sometimes part of an upthrust. At such times, it is a powerful incentive to sell."

## Bearish Engulfing Pattern

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A small white real body followed by a larger black real body that engulfs (covers) the prior white body.
- **Recognition:** Black candlestick's body fully engulfs the prior white candlestick's body. Can also engulf more than one prior body for extra significance.
- **Psychology:** Buyers initially in control, then sellers overwhelm and reverse the move.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Strong bearish reversal. Often appears at resistance or at the top of a rally. Confirmation can come from MACD bearish crossover or hanging man the next session.
- **Nison's notes:** When a bearish engulfing pattern emerges at highs that also fail at a resistance line, the bearish case is reinforced. The text describes bearish engulfing pattern 1 as a warning to longs and bearish engulfing pattern 2 as occurring a few weeks later with the highs also failing at a resistance line.

## Bullish Engulfing Pattern

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A small black real body followed by a larger white real body that engulfs (covers) the prior black body. The engulfing white line can engulf more than one prior body for extra significance.
- **Recognition:** White candlestick's body fully engulfs the prior black candlestick's body. Constructive implication is reinforced by RSI positive divergence.
- **Psychology:** Sellers initially in control, then buyers overwhelm and reverse the move.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Strong bullish reversal. Confirmation often comes from positive RSI divergence, springs, support test, or higher volume.
- **Nison's notes:** "The constructive outlook implied by this piercing pattern was reinforced by the positive divergence of the RSI." Engulfing can also engulf two black bodies for extra significance. Sometimes combined with a tweezers bottom.

## Harami

- **Type:** neutral (can be bullish or bearish reversal) · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long real body followed by a small real body that is contained within the prior body. The first candle's color determines the trend context; the second candle's color indicates potential reversal direction.
- **Recognition:** Second candle's real body is fully inside the first candle's real body. A "harami cross" variation has the second candle as a doji.
- **Psychology:** The prior trend's force is being absorbed; uncertainty prevails.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Often a warning of indecision or impending reversal. The harami can be the "first inkling of trouble" before a major reversal. Confirmation by hanging man, dark-cloud cover, or other reversal indicator.
- **Nison's notes:** A harami can give the first inkling of trouble. A harami cross is a variation where the second session is a doji.

## Piercing Pattern

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A black real body followed by a white real body that opens below the prior low and closes above the midpoint of the prior black body.
- **Recognition:** White candle closes above the midpoint (50% level) of the prior black candle's body.
- **Psychology:** Bears initially in control, then bulls push the price back into the prior range.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. Often appears at support or at a successful test of a downward sloping support line. Confirmation by RSI positive divergence, hammer, or successful spring.
- **Nison's notes:** Used in the gold example for the end of a 1987 selloff and again on October 19–20 (second piercing pattern).

## Long-Legged Doji

- **Type:** reversal (context-dependent) · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A doji with very long upper and lower shadows. Open and close are near each other in the middle of the trading range, with long shadows on both sides.
- **Recognition:** Very long upper and lower shadows with a doji (open ≈ close).
- **Psychology:** Intense indecision; the market has tested both directions and could not hold either.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Caution warranted, especially at resistance. When it appears at resistance, a long-legged doji is a reason to be cautious and watch for bearish confirmation.
- **Nison's notes:** "A long-legged doji arose at B. The fact that this doji also surfaced near the resistance level set by A was a reason to be cautious."

## Rickshaw Man

- **Type:** reversal (context-dependent) · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A long-legged doji where the opening and closing prices are in the middle of the range.
- **Recognition:** Doji with long upper and lower shadows; the open and close are both near the middle of the trading range.
- **Psychology:** Maximum indecision.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Can warn of market weakness. In the cotton example, a long-legged doji (rickshaw man) was followed by a hanging man as the end of a powerful bull move.
- **Nison's notes:** "the long-legged doji (a rickshaw man since the opening and closing were in the middle of the range) and the hanging man."

## Tweezers Bottom

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Two or more candles with approximately equal lows, often occurring at a support level.
- **Recognition:** Two consecutive sessions (or more) whose lows are virtually the same.
- **Psychology:** The market tested support and found a floor.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal. Stronger when combined with a hammer, spring, or harami.
- **Nison's notes:** "combination tweezers and harami bottom" was the start of a rally in crude oil. "Hammer and a tweezers bottom created noteworthy bullish evidence" in unleaded gas.

## Tweezers Top

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Two or more candles with approximately equal highs, often occurring at a resistance level.
- **Recognition:** Two consecutive sessions (or more) whose highs are virtually the same.

## Dark-Cloud Cover

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A white real body followed by a black real body that opens above the prior high and closes below the midpoint of the prior white body.
- **Recognition:** Black candle opens above the prior white candle's high and closes below its midpoint.
- **Psychology:** Bulls initially in control, but bears push the price sharply down into the prior range.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal. Strengthened when it occurs at resistance, at a moving average, or near a 50% retracement level.
- **Nison's notes:** Three dark-cloud covers at a resistance line confirm the line's importance as a supply area. A dark-cloud cover in cocoa confirmed the resistance at the $1,230 level.

## Evening Doji Star

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long white real body, followed by a doji that gaps up, followed by a black real body that pushes down into the first white body.
- **Recognition:** Doji in the middle session with gaps separating it from the surrounding candles.
- **Psychology:** Uptrend momentum exhausting, then indecision, then reversal.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish reversal at the end of a rally. In the live cattle example, an evening doji star formed in mid-March near the top of the trading band.

## Bullish Belt Hold

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A long white candlestick with no lower shadow (or very small lower shadow) and possibly an upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Long white body opening at the low and closing near the high.
- **Psychology:** Bulls took control from the open and held it through the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal signal, especially when it successfully tests prior support.

## Bearish Hanging Man (Variation)

- **Type:** bearish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A candlestick similar to a hanging man but with a lower shadow not long enough to qualify as a classic hanging man. "A candlestick similar to a hanging man. (The lower shadow was not long enough for it to be a classic hanging man, though.)"
- **Recognition:** Small body at upper end of range with a long-but-not-classic lower shadow, in an uptrend.
- **Psychology:** A weaker hanging-man signal of bears testing the bulls' resolve.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Combined with other bearish tools (RSI negative divergence, doji) to confirm.

## Inverted Hammer

- **Type:** reversal (context-dependent) · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A small real body at the lower end of the trading range with a long upper shadow.
- **Recognition:** Small body at the bottom of the range with a long upper shadow.

## Doji

- **Type:** reversal (context-dependent) · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** Open and close at virtually the same price.
- **Recognition:** No or near-zero real body.
- **Psychology:** Indecision between bulls and bears.
- **Signal & confirmation:** "Doji are a warning signal during uptrends. But, like all technical clues, they can sometimes mislead you. One way to filter out the misleading clues is to add other technical tools." Two consecutive doji in a support area can be more significant. Doji after a tall white real body means trouble.

## Harami Cross

- **Type:** reversal (context-dependent) · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A long real body followed by a doji contained within the prior body.
- **Recognition:** A harami where the second candle is a doji.
- **Psychology:** Indecision absorbing the prior trend.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish when at a support area (as in cocoa), often leading to a bullish engulfing.

## Bullish Belt Hold

- **Type:** bullish · reversal · 1 candle line.
- **Structure:** A long white real body with no lower shadow; opens at the low and closes near the high.
- **Recognition:** Long white candle; opens at the low of the session.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish when it successfully tests a prior support level; a long white line was a bullish belt hold that successfully tested support.

## Retracement Levels

- **Common Levels:** 38%, 50%, and 62%. Derived from Fibonacci ratios: 38.2% (or inverse 2.618), 61.8% (or inverse 1.618), and 50% (a Fibonacci ratio).
- **Fibonacci Origin:** 13th-century mathematician Fibonacci derived a special sequence of numbers; comparing them gives Fibonacci ratios.
- **50% Most Widely Monitored:** The 50% retracement is the most popular and is used by Gann, Elliott Wave, and Dow Theory practitioners.
- **Application in Uptrend:** From low B to high A, retracement levels measure potential support on pullbacks.
- **Application in Downtrend:** From high A to low B, retracement levels measure potential resistance on rallies.
- **Calculation:** 50% retracement is found by taking half the difference between the high and low and adding it to the low (for an uptrend) or subtracting it from the high (for a downtrend).
- **Usage with Candlesticks:** Retracements help predict resistance or support zones. Confirmation is sought from a candlestick signal at the retracement level.

## Simple Moving Average (SMA)

- **Definition:** The average of all the price points used. For example, a 5-day SMA of closes $380, $383, $394, $390, and $382 is the sum divided by 5.
- **Formula:** Sum of last n prices divided by n.
- **"Moving" Concept:** As the newest data is added, the oldest data is dropped; the average is always moving.
- **Weighting:** Each day in a 5-day MA contributes 1/5; in a 9-day MA, each day contributes 1/9. The longer the MA, the less impact an individual price will have. The shorter the MA, the closer it will "hug" prices (more sensitive but more whipsaws). Longer-term MAs provide more smoothing but are less responsive.
- **Popular Periods:** 4-, 9-, and 18-day for shorter-term traders; 13-, 26-, and 40-week for position players. The 13- and 40-week MAs are popular in Japan.
- **Price Source:** Most systems use closing prices, but MAs of highs, lows, and the mid-point of highs and lows have been used. Sometimes MAs of MAs are used.

## Weighted Moving Average

- **Definition:** Assigns a different weight to each price used to compute the average.
- **Front-Loading:** Almost all weighted moving averages are front-loaded — the most recent prices are weighted more heavily than older prices.
- **Weighting Method:** How the data is weighted is a matter of preference.

## Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

- **Definition:** A special type of weighted moving average. Like basic weighted MAs, it is front-weighted.
- **Unique Feature:** Unlike other MAs, the exponential MA incorporates all prior prices used in the data. It assigns progressively smaller weights to each of the past prices, with each weight exponentially smaller than the previous weight.

## MACD (Moving Average Convergence-Divergence)

- **Composition:** Two lines. The first line is the difference between two exponential MAs (usually 26- and 12-period EMAs). The second line is an EMA (usually 9-period) of the difference between the two EMAs used in the first line. The second line is called the signal line.
- **Sell Signal:** When the more volatile signal line crosses below the slower moving dashed line. The text notes a sell signal in bonds corroborated by the bearish implications of two bearish engulfing patterns.
- **Buy Signal:** When the signal line of the MACD pushes above the slower moving line.

## Moving Average Uses

- **Trend Indicator:** Compare price versus MA (e.g., prices above 65-day MA = intermediate uptrend; above 40-week MA = longer-term uptrend).
- **Support/Resistance:** A close above the specified MA is bullish; close below is bearish.
- **Moving Average Bands (Envelopes):** A certain percentage above or below the MA; serve as support or resistance.
- **Slope Monitoring:** If the MA levels off or declines after a sustained rise, it may be bearish. Drawing trendlines on MAs is a simple way to monitor slope.
- **Dual Moving Average System:** Crossover of two MAs (see Golden Cross and Dead Cross).

## Dual Moving Averages

- **Overbought/Oversold Oscillator:** Subtract the shorter-term MA from the longer-term MA. Plus value = short-term above long-term (overbought if too high). Minus value = short-term below long-term (oversold if too low). Compares short-term momentum to longer-term momentum.
- **Crossover Signals:** Short-term MA crossing above long-term MA = Golden Cross (bullish). Crossing below = Dead Cross (bearish).
- **Divergence Use:** As prices increase, the technician wants to see the short-term MA increase relative to the longer-term MA (increasing positive values). If prices advance and the differential narrows, short-term momentum is running out of steam.
- **Histogram Use:** Histogram of differential. Below 0 = short-term MA under long-term (bearish dead cross); above 0 = bullish golden cross. A histogram can show divergence with price.
- **Example Use:** The 5-day MA subtracted from current close shows overbought/oversold. In copper, readings about 400 points ($.04) overbought coincided with harami, doji patterns. Overbought markets "usually should not be shorted" but used by longs to take defensive measures.

## Golden Cross (Japanese Term)

- **Definition:** A bullish signal that occurs when the shorter-term moving average crosses above a longer-term moving average (e.g., 3-day MA crossing above 9-day MA).
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish. In the Deutschemark example, a bullish golden cross and a fry pan bottom occurred. The fry pan bottom was confirmed by a window.

## Dead Cross (Japanese Term)

- **Definition:** A bearish signal that occurs when the shorter-term moving average crosses under a longer-term moving average.

## Oscillators

- **Definition:** Mathematically derived techniques that provide a more objective means of analyzing the market. Includes the Relative Strength Index, stochastics, and momentum. They are the basis of many computerized trading systems.
- **Three Main Uses:**
  - **Divergence Indicators:** A negative (bearish) divergence occurs when prices are at a new high but the oscillator is not — implies internal weakness. A positive (bullish) divergence occurs when prices are at a new low but the oscillator does not — implies selling pressure losing steam.
  - **Overbought/Oversold Indicators:** Notify if the market is overextended and vulnerable to correction. Best applied in lateral price environments. Using an oscillator as overbought/oversold when a new major trend is commencing can cause problems — a breakout can keep the oscillator overbought while prices ascend.
  - **Trend Confirmation:** Measure market momentum. Velocity should increase as the trend is underway. A flattening of momentum could be an early warning that the move is decelerating.
- **Trading Rule:** Use oscillator signals in the direction of the dominant trend. A bullish oscillator signal should be used to buy if the major trend is up, and to cover shorts if the major trend is down. Do not short on a bearish oscillator signal unless the prevailing trend is heading south.

## Relative Strength Index (RSI)

- **Definition:** A popular technical tool comparing the relative strength of price advances to price declines over a specified period. Nine and 14 days are common periods. The text notes this RSI differs from the equity-technician "relative strength" (which compares a stock's performance to a broader market index).
- **Computation:** Adds the total gains made on up days over the period (close-to-close basis) and divides by the period length. Same for down days. These figures provide the relative strength (RS). The RS is plugged into the formula to convert the data into an index with a range between 0 and 100.
- **Formula:** RSI = 100 − (1 / RS) where RS = average up points for period / average down points for period.
- **Overbought/Oversold Use:** RSI above 70 or 80 is overbought; below 30 or 20 is oversold.
- **Divergence Use:** Negative (bearish) divergence = prices make new high, RSI does not. Positive (bullish) divergence = prices make new low, RSI does not. Divergences are more meaningful when RSI readings are in overbought/oversold regions.
- **Crossover Confirmation:** Some technicians view the RSI as bullish only when (1) a positive divergence occurs AND (2) the RSI moves above its prior high. Joining candlestick indications with RSI's positive divergence can produce a signal earlier.
- **RSI Trendlines:** Some technicians draw trendlines on the RSI. An RSI uptrend support line can hold in spite of new price lows.

## Stochastics

- **Definition:** A popular oscillator that provides overbought/oversold readings, signals divergences, and compares a shorter-term trend to a longer-term trend. The stochastic compares the latest closing price with the total range of price action for a specified period. Stochastic values are between 0 and 100. A high reading means the close is near the upper end of the range; a low reading means near the lower end.
- **Logic:** As the market moves higher, closes tend to be near the highs of the range; as it moves lower, prices cluster near the lows.
- **%K (Fast Stochastic):** The most sensitive line. Formula: %K = (Close − Low of N) / (High of N − Low of N) × 100. If the close equals the high for the period, fast %K = 100. Periods are in days, weeks, or intra-day; 19, 14, and 21 are common.
- **%K Smoothing:** Because fast %K is volatile, it is smoothed by taking a 3-period moving average of %K to get the slow %K. Most technicians use slow %K.
- **%D Line:** A 3-day moving average of the slow %K — essentially a moving average of a moving average. Comparable to a longer-term MA; %K is comparable to a shorter-term MA.
- **Use with Crossover:** Some technicians require slow %K to cross under %D for a sell signal, or %K to move above %D for a buy signal. A buy signal requires the market to be oversold (25% or less for %D), there is a positive divergence, and %K is crossing above %D.
- **Crossover Significance:** More significant if from oversold readings (under 25%).
- **Doji in Uptrend:** "Doji are a warning signal during uptrends" — especially with negative stochastic divergence.
- **Variation Pattern Caution:** When a pattern (e.g., morning star) is a variation and not ideal, scan other technical evidence (e.g., positive divergence) before placing importance on the pattern.

## Momentum

- **Definition:** Also called price velocity. Measures the difference between the closing price today and the closing price a specified number of days ago (e.g., 10-day momentum compares today's close to that of 10 days ago). If today's close is higher, momentum is positive; if lower, negative.
- **Trend Logic:** In a progressing trend, price differences (velocity) should rise at an increasing rate. If prices are rising and momentum begins to flatten, a decelerating price trend is in effect — early warning of a possible end to the prior price trend.
- **Zero Line Cross:** Momentum crossing under 0 is a bearish sign; above 0 is bullish.
- **Overbought/Oversold:** When the momentum index is at a relatively large positive value, the market may be overbought. Momentum usually hits its peak before prices, so a very overbought momentum reading could presage a price peak.
- **Confirmation Use:** Bearish candlestick confirmation increases the odds of a top reversal with an overbought momentum reading. Hammer candles accompanied oversold momentum levels.

## Volume

- **Core Theory:** The greater the volume, the greater the force behind the move. As long as volume increases, the current price trend should continue. If volume declines as a price trend progresses, the trend is less likely to continue.
- **Top/Bottom Confirmation:** A light volume test of a support level suggests diminution of selling force (bullish). A light volume test of a previous high is bearish (draining of buying power).
- **Limitations (especially futures):** Volume is reported a day late. Spread trading may cause aberrations in volume figures, especially on individual contract months. Option dominance in many futures markets can skew volume figures due to arbitrage strategies.
- **Usage with Candlesticks:** Volume can be merged with candlestick charting to confirm double tops or bottoms, and to assess the force behind candlestick signals.
- **Heavy Volume Bullish:** A white candlestick with the largest volume in months forcibly proves the conviction of the bulls.
- **Light Volume Retest:** A light volume retest of lows by a candlestick similar to a hammer confirms a solid base.

## On Balance Volume (OBV)

- **Definition:** A net volume figure. When the market closes higher compared to the prior close, the volume for that day is added to the cumulative OBV figure. When the close is lower, the volume is subtracted from cumulative OBV.
- **Trend Confirmation Use:** OBV should move in the direction of the prevailing price trend. If prices and OBV are ascending, increased volume from buyers (even at higher price levels) is bullish. If both are declining, stronger volume from sellers means lower prices should continue.
- **Lateral Range Use:** If OBV escalates and prices are stable (preferably at a low price area), this exhibits accumulation — bullish. If prices move sideways and OBV declines, it reflects distribution — bearish, especially at high price levels.
- **Divergence Use:** OBV can be used for divergence. A positive divergence in OBV alongside a hammer at new price lows supplied signs of a near-term bottom.

## Tick Volume™

- **Definition:** Shows the number of trades per intra-day period (e.g., 50 trades per hour). Does not show the number of contracts per trade. Tick Volume™ is not a true volume figure but is the only means of measuring volume on a more timely, albeit less accurate, basis.
- **Purpose:** Circumvents the problem of volume being reported a day late in the futures market, giving a "feel" for volume on an intra-day basis.
- **Usage with Candlesticks:** A bullish engulfing pattern with extra significance (engulfing two black bodies) and tick volume™ expansion confirms buyer control. A hammer with declining tick volume™ during advance signals lack of buyer conviction.

## Open Interest

- **Definition:** The total number of long or short contracts (but not a total of both) that remain outstanding. A new contract is created when a new buyer and a new short seller agree to a trade.
- **Trend Confirmation Principle:** Open interest helps confirm the current trend if open interest increases. Rising open interest in an uptrend = new longs are more aggressive than new shorts; rally should continue. Rising open interest in a downtrend = new short sellers are more aggressive; selling pressure should continue.
- **Declining Open Interest Warning:** If open interest declines during a trending market, the trend may not continue because old positions are exiting. A rally with declining open interest = short covering (and old bulls liquidating); the market is vulnerable to further weakness once old shorts have fled.
- **Hose Analogy:** Rising open interest is like fresh water pumped from a main water line into a hose; the trend continues while the spigot is open. Declining open interest is like closing the spigot; the trend should dry up.
- **Trap Warning:** Unusually high open interest coinciding with new price highs can presage trouble. Rising open interest means new shorts and longs enter; stop losses are at increasingly higher prices. A sudden fall can trigger a chain reaction of sell stop losses. New longs not stopped out are "trapped" at higher levels, and any rallies will be used to exit.
- **Other Considerations:** Seasonality is among other factors to consider (not detailed in the excerpt).

## Western Tool Relationships with Candlesticks — Key Examples

- **Hammer + Support Test = Buy Signal:** A bullish hammer coinciding with a successful test of a support line is a bottom reversal signal. The hammer's low can be used as a protective stop.
- **Hammer + Spring = Strong Buy:** When a spring is complimented by a hammer (and a tweezers bottom), noteworthy bullish evidence is present.
- **Piercing + RSI Positive Divergence = Bullish Confirmation:** "The constructive outlook implied by this piercing pattern was reinforced by the positive divergence of the RSI."
- **Engulfing + RSI Positive Divergence = Earlier Bullish Signal:** Joining the bullish engulfing with the RSI's positive divergence gave a bullish signal earlier than waiting for the RSI to cross above its prior high.
- **Dark-Cloud Cover + Moving Average Failure = Bearish:** A dark-cloud cover occurring as a failure at the moving average's resistance area.
- **Shooting Star + Upthrust = Strong Sell:** "Shooting stars are sometimes part of an upthrust. At such times, it is a powerful incentive to sell."
- **Hanging Man + Resistance Failure = Bearish:** A pullback from a resistance line combined with a shooting star and hanging-man lines gave clear warnings of an upcoming correction.
- **Hanging Man + OBV = Bottom Signal:** "the positive divergence in OBV, the failure of the bears to hold the new lows, and the hammer line supplied signs of a near-term bottom."
- **Piercing + Downward Sloping Support Line = Cover Shorts:** "It was time to cover shorts — or at least take defensive measures such as lowering stops or selling puts. It was not time to buy because the major trend was down."
- **Hammer + 65-Day MA Support = Buy:** 65-day moving average offered support at areas 1, 2, and 3 in soybeans. Bullish engulfing at area 1, hammer and harami at 2, hammer-like line at 3.
- **Dark-Cloud Cover + 65-Day MA Resistance + 50% Retracement = Bearish:** A confluence of (1) moving average resistance, (2) dark-cloud cover, and (3) 50% retracement resistance occurred.
- **Hammer + 50% Retracement = Buy:** 50% retracement support plus a daily hammer plus an intra-day hourly hammer = a rare and significant occurrence.
- **Hanging Man + Upward Sloping Resistance Line = Defensive Action for Longs:** The failure of a shooting star at an upward sloping resistance line gave a signal for longs to take protective measures.
- **Piercing + 62% Fibonacci + Old Resistance Support = Buy:** A Fibonacci 62% retracement of a prior rally coincided with old resistance (now support) — provided a level for a harami pattern to signal an end to a downmove.
- **Engulfing + MACD Bearish Crossover = Sell:** Bearish engulfing patterns corroborated by bearish crossovers of the MACD indicators.
- **Morning Star + Stochastic Positive Divergence + Crossover = Buy:** A variation morning star was given added weight by a positive stochastic divergence and a positive %K-over-%D crossover.
- **Engulfing + Volume = Buy:** "A light volume retest of these lows by a candlestick similar to a hammer confirmed a solid base." Conversely, a "white candlestick with the largest volume in the last few months" forcibly proved the conviction of the bulls.
- **Doji + Volume Decline + Support Test = Bottom:** A doji's importance becomes amplified when followed by another doji plus a tweezers bottom plus light volume (a light volume test of a support area is bullish).
- **Bearish Engulfing + Hanging Man + Bearish Divergence = Sell:** Bearish engulfs, hanging man, and bearish stochastic/negative divergence all signaling weakness.
- **Hanging Man + Shooting Star + Bearish Engulfing = Top:** A series of bearish indicators including a hanging man, shooting star, and bearish engulfing pattern marked a top.
- **Harami + Stochastic Overbought = Caution:** Overbought stochastic readings coinciding with harami or doji patterns in copper warned of vulnerability.
- **Inverted Hammer + Golden Cross = Bullish:** A bullish inverted hammer appeared a few days before a golden cross.
- **Dark-Cloud Cover + Failed Golden Cross + Resistance = Bearish:** Prices rallied but the short-term MA could not get back above the long-term MA (oscillator remained under 0), and a dark-cloud cover formed.
- **Piercing + 50% Retracement = Support Confirmed:** Piercing pattern formed near a 50% retracement level confirmed support.
- **Hanging Man + Resistance Failure = Top:** A hanging man in a chart in cocoa at $1,230 resistance level warned of a top.
- **Hanging Man + Bearish Engulfing = Continued Weakness:** Bearish engulfing pattern after a hanging man reinforces the bearish case.
- **Bearish Engulfing + Hanging Man = Top:** A series of bearish candlestick patterns (engulfing, hanging man) plus bearish stochastic divergence marked a top.
- **Piercing Pattern + 50% Retracement = Buy:** A piercing pattern formed near the 50% retracement support level (e.g., gold at $425 from the A-to-B selloff).
- **Bearish Engulfing + Retracement Resistance = Sell:** A bearish engulfing pattern formed near a 50% retracement resistance level (e.g., gold near $464) gave a sell signal.
- **Piercing + Spring = Buy:** Piercing patterns formed in conjunction with springs, increasing the bullish case.
- **Hammer + Tweezers Bottom = Buy:** "Spring, complimented by a hammer and a tweezers bottom created noteworthy bullish evidence."
- **Hammer + Harami + Support = Bottom:** "a bullish engulfing pattern at area 1, a hammer and harami at 2, and another hammer-like line at 3" all coincided with 65-day moving average support.
- **Engulfing + Spring = Buy:** "Furthermore, the lows on that session constructed a bullish engulfing pattern" — the spring day was also a bullish engulfing pattern.
- **Hammer + Spring = Buy:** In live cattle, the spring day was also a hammer. "This union of bullish signals gave plenty of warning."
- **Engulfing + Volume Spike = Buy:** A bullish engulfing pattern with the largest volume in months confirmed buyer conviction.
- **Harami + Tick Volume Decline + Sideways = Caution:** Small real bodies on declining tick volume™ during an advance signal lack of buyer conviction.
- **Doji + Hanging Man + Bearish Shooting Star = Top:** Multiple bearish indicators in cocoa preceded a sharp decline after a stop hunt.
- **Hanging Man + Bearish Candlestick Pattern + Resistance = Sell:** When a hanging man appears with other bearish indicators at a resistance level (e.g., $1,230 in cocoa), the bearish case is strong.
- **Evening Star + Harami Cross = Top:** An evening star and then a harami cross at an overbought momentum level warn of a top.

- **Dark-Cloud Cover + 50% Retracement + MA Resistance = Sell:** A dark-cloud cover that was also a failure at the moving average's resistance area, also within 7 ticks of a 50% retracement, gave a sell signal.

## Candlestick Reliability & Caveats (Nison's Notes)

- "Doji are a warning signal during uptrends. But, like all technical clues, they can sometimes mislead you. One way to filter out the misleading clues is to add other technical tools."
- Variation patterns require additional technical evidence for confirmation.
- "The fact that the market made another price surge … and although this was a new price high, RSI levels continued to decline. The result was a pullback to the March support area" — illustrates divergence use.
- The change of polarity principle is "an under utilized gem" but is universally effective across time horizons and markets.
- "Pick up just about any chart, be it intra-day, daily, weekly, or longer and the chances are high that you will see examples of this change of polarity in action."
- "Do not let the simplicity of the rule fool you. It works — especially when melded with candlestick indicators."
- Moving averages are lagging indicators; they can only catch a trend after it has turned.
- "The more popular moving averages include the 4-, 9-, and 18-day averages for shorter-term traders and the 13-, 26-, and 40-week moving averages for position players."
- "The 13- and 40-week moving averages are popular in Japan."
- "Overbought markets usually should not be shorted. Instead, they should be used by longs to take defensive measures. The reverse is true in oversold markets."
- "The reason has to do with the raison d'être of technical analysis; to measure the emotions and actions of the trading and investing community. Thus, the better a technical tool measures behavior, the better that tool should work."
- Trading springs and upthrusts is effective because "they provide a clear target (the opposite end of the trading range) and protective stop (the new high or low made with the 'false breakout')."
- "Momentum usually hits its peak before prices. Based on this, a very overbought momentum oscillator could be presaging a price peak."
- "Stops are synonymous with rule 1" (capital preservation).

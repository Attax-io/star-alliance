# Continuation Patterns — Windows, Tasuki, Three Methods, Soldiers

## Introduction to Continuation Patterns

- Most candlestick signals are trend reversals; this chapter covers continuation indicators.
- The Japanese expression: "there are times to buy, times to sell, and times to rest."
- Many continuation patterns imply a brief rest or breather before the prior trend resumes.
- Patterns reviewed: windows (and patterns containing windows), rising and falling three methods, and three white soldiers.

## Windows (Gaps)

- Japanese term "window" corresponds to the Western "gap."
- "Closing the window" = "filling in the gap."
- **Type:** Continuation indicator; can act as support or resistance.
- **Structure:** A gap between the prior session's price extreme and the current session's price extreme (i.e., no overlap between the high/low ranges of the two adjacent sessions).
- **Recognition:** Identified as a price range with no trading activity between the prior session's high (or low) and the current session's low (or high), depending on trend direction.
- **Psychology:** A window is said by Japanese technicians to "go in the direction of the window" — i.e., momentum is strong enough to skip a price range.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - A window in an uptrend implies a further price rise and should act as a floor on pullbacks.
  - A window in a downtrend implies still lower levels and should act as resistance on rebounds.
  - Traditional Japanese analysis asserts that corrections return to the window.
  - If the pullback closes the window AND selling pressure continues afterward, the prior uptrend is voided.
  - If a rally closes a downside window and buying persists, the downtrend is done.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - Two criteria to end a downtrend: (1) close the large window, (2) continuation of buying force after the window closes.
  - The three most important aspects of the market are trend, trend, and trend — candlesticks (or any tool) must be considered in the context of the prevailing trend. A bullish reversal pattern in a major downtrend is not a buy signal.
  - Candlestick techniques and procedures are guidelines, not hard and fast rules; some subjectivity is allowed in interpretation.

## Windows from Congestion Zones or New Highs

- **Recognition:** A window that opens from a congestion zone or from a new high.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Such windows deserve special attention because they offer dual support/resistance — the window itself plus the old resistance-turned-support (or support-turned-resistance) area.
- **Nison's notes:** A window made with a small black candlestick from a low price congestion area can signal a meaningful upside breakout, especially when confirmed as support by the next session producing a long white candlestick that opens on its low and closes on its high.

## Three Windows Concept

- **Recognition:** Three consecutive up or down windows in the same direction.
- **Signal & confirmation:** After three up windows, odds are strong that a top is near; after three down windows, a bottom is near — especially if a turning point candlestick (doji, piercing pattern, dark-cloud cover, or a hanging-man line) appears after the third gap.

## Eight to Ten New Record Highs/Lows

- **Recognition:** Approximately eight to ten new highs (or new lows) in a row without a meaningful correction.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Strong odds that a significant correction will unfold.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - Each new high/low in a move is called a "new record high" or "new record low" by the Japanese.
  - The Japanese expression "the stomach is 80% full" refers to roughly eight new highs without a meaningful correction, warning of an approaching top.
  - When this condition coincides with a window acting as resistance, it provides an extra strong signal to be cautious about the long side.

## Upward-Gap Tasuki

- **Type:** Bullish · continuation · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: Long white candlestick in an existing uptrend.
  - Line 2: Black candlestick that gaps above the white line, opens within the white real body, and closes under the white candlestick's real body.
  - The real bodies of the two candlesticks should be about the same size.
- **Recognition:** White candle, then a small upward gap, then a black candle opening within the prior white real body and closing beneath it.
- **Psychology:** After a strong advance, a small pullback day that gaps up shows the bulls still have momentum despite a one-day setback.
- **Signal & confirmation:** The buy point is the close of the black candlestick. If the market fills the gap (closes the window) and selling pressure continues, the bullish outlook of the upside-gap tasuki is voided.
- **Nison's notes:** The pattern is rare.

## Downward-Gap Tasuki

- **Type:** Bearish · continuation · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Mirror of the upward-gap tasuki — a long black candlestick in a downtrend, followed by a white candlestick that gaps lower, opens within the black real body, and closes above the black real body.
- **Recognition:** Black candle, small downward gap, white candle opening inside the prior black real body and closing above it.
- **Psychology:** The same concepts as the upward-gap tasuki, reversed.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Continuation of the downtrend; negated if the gap is closed and buying pressure persists.
- **Nison's notes:** The pattern is rare; the real bodies of the two candlesticks should be about the same size.

## High-Price Gapping Play

- **Type:** Bullish · continuation · 3+ candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: A sharp, strong white candlestick (one- to two-session advance).
  - Lines 2+: A series of small real bodies that consolidate the gains near the recent highs.
  - Final line: An upside gap (window) opening from these small real bodies.
- **Recognition:** Tall white candle, followed by a small congestion band of small real bodies, then an upward gap.
- **Psychology:** The market is undecided during the consolidation; the upside gap signals that the bulls have reasserted control and prices should continue higher.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A buy signal. The window formed typically becomes a support level.
- **Nison's notes:** Called "high-price" because prices hover near their recent highs and then gap to the upside.

## Low-Price Gapping Play

- **Type:** Bearish · continuation · 3+ candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: A sharp, strong black candlestick (steep one- to two-session decline).
  - Lines 2+: A series of small real bodies that initially stabilize the decline (looks like a base forming).
  - Final line: A downside gap (window) breaking lower from the small real bodies.
- **Recognition:** Tall black candle, followed by a small congestion band, then a downward gap.
- **Psychology:** Although the small candlesticks initially suggest a base is forming, the downside gap dashes the bullish hopes and confirms the prior sellers still dominate.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A sell signal.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - For a gapping play, the consolidation should not last more than 11 sessions.
  - The pattern allows for some subjectivity — even when the exact gap criteria are not perfectly met, a close resemblance to the pattern can be acted upon.

## Upgap Side-by-Side White Lines

- **Type:** Bullish · continuation · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: White candlestick in an uptrend.
  - Line 2: Another white candlestick of similar size with about the same opening, gapping above Line 1.
- **Recognition:** Two consecutive white candlesticks with a small upside gap between them, similar size and similar opens, in an existing uptrend.
- **Psychology:** The two white candles near the highs show strong, sustained buying.
- **Signal & confirmation:** If the market closes above the higher of the two side-by-side white candlesticks, another leg up should unfold.
- **Nison's notes:** The pattern is rare.

## Downgap Side-by-Side White Lines

- **Type:** Bearish · continuation · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: White candlestick in a downtrend.
  - Line 2: Another similar white candlestick with about the same opening, gapping below Line 1.
- **Recognition:** Two white candlesticks with a downward gap between them during a downtrend.
- **Psychology:** White candles in a falling market are viewed as short covering — once the short covering evaporates, prices should move lower.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Continuation of the downtrend.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - The pattern is even more rare than the upgap variety, because black candlesticks are more natural in a market that lowers gaps.
  - If in a falling market a black candlestick gaps lower and is followed by another black candlestick with a lower close, the market should experience another price decline.
  - In practice, the pattern can be flexible — opens need not be perfectly identical, and the white lines can be separated by one day.

## Rising Three Methods

- **Type:** Bullish · continuation · 5 candle lines (typically).
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: A long white candlestick.
  - Lines 2–4 (ideal): A group of small real body candlesticks that fall back; the ideal number is three, but two or more than three are acceptable as long as they basically hold within the first long white candlestick's range (including shadows). The small candlesticks can be any color, but black is most common. The pattern resembles a three-day harami pattern (holding within the first session's range including shadows).
  - Line 5: A strong white real body session that opens above the close of the previous session and closes above the first day's close.
- **Recognition:** Tall white candle → small candles holding within the first candle's range → another tall white candle closing above the first.
- **Psychology:** The market is "taking a breather" — a rest from trading and a rest from battle. The original bulls then resume the advance.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - Bullish continuation.
  - A higher close in the final white candlestick is normally required; if the last white candlestick only barely misses closing above the first's close (e.g., by a few ticks), it can still be considered a rising three methods, awaiting bullish confirmation the next session.
  - Volume that expands on the white sessions and contracts on the small candlestick sessions lends more significance to the pattern.
  - The top of the pattern can become a support area on subsequent tests.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - The concept dates from the 1700s.
  - Resembles the Western bull flag or pennant formation.
  - Color of the small real bodies is not required to be black — as long as they hold within the first session's range, the formation has potential to be a three methods pattern.

## Falling Three Methods

- **Type:** Bearish · continuation · 5 candle lines (typically).
- **Structure:**
  - Line 1: A long black candlestick in a downtrend.
  - Lines 2–4 (ideal): A group of small real body candlesticks (about three, usually white) whose real bodies hold within the first candlestick's range (including shadows).
  - Line 5: A long black candlestick that opens under the prior close and closes under the first black candlestick's close.
- **Recognition:** Tall black candle → small counter-trend candles holding within the first candle's range → another tall black candle closing below the first's close.
- **Psychology:** A brief consolidation before sellers drive prices to fresh lows — a rest from battle before the assault resumes.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - Bearish continuation; after the last black candlestick, the market should head lower.
  - Tick volume expanding on the black candlesticks and shrinking on the intervening white candlesticks confirms the bearish action.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - Resembles a Western bear flag or pennant formation.
  - More than three small candlesticks are acceptable (e.g., four) as long as the real bodies hold within the first day's range.
  - Important principle: do not act on a formation until that formation is completed. If the last candlestick does not close under the first's close, the falling three methods is not completed; if the closes are close, further bearish confirmation the next session is required.
  - A harami cross (doji joined with prior black real body) or other reversal pattern can negate the bearish implications.

## Three Advancing White Soldiers (Three White Soldiers)

- **Type:** Bullish · reversal at low (used here as context for related continuation patterns) · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** A group of three white candlesticks with consecutively higher closes. Each white line opens within or near the prior session's white real body, and each closes at or near its high.
- **Recognition:** Three consecutive white candles, each opening within the previous real body, each closing higher, and each closing near its high.
- **Psychology:** A gradual and steady rise, reflecting healthy bullish control.
- **Signal & confirmation:**
  - If this pattern appears at a low price area after a period of stable prices, it is a sign of strength ahead.
  - If the white candlesticks are very extended, the market may be overbought and caution is warranted.
  - The pattern carries more weight when starting from a low price level rather than after an extended advance.
- **Nison's notes:** Has a military association (soldiers).

## Advance Block

- **Type:** Bearish warning · not a top reversal per se · 3 candle lines (modification of three white soldiers).
- **Structure:** Three white candlesticks with consecutively higher closes where the second and third, or just the third, candlestick shows signs of weakening.
- **Recognition:** Progressively smaller white real bodies on the latter two candles, and/or relatively long upper shadows on the latter two white candlesticks.
- **Psychology:** The lead (advance) soldier has been "blocked" — the rally is running into trouble.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Longs should protect themselves. Be especially cautious during a mature uptrend. The pattern can sometimes precede a meaningful price decline.
- **Nison's notes:** Used to liquidate or protect longs (e.g., sell covered calls) but not to short. Generally more consequential at higher price levels. A shooting star or hanging-man line as the last small white real body adds to the warning.

## Stalled Pattern (Deliberation Pattern)

- **Type:** Bearish warning · not a top reversal per se · 3 candle lines.
- **Structure:** Two long white candlesticks making a new high, followed by a small white candlestick.
- **Recognition:** The small white candlestick can either gap away from the long white body (becoming a star) or be "riding on the shoulder" of the long white real body (i.e., be at the upper end of the prior long white real body).
- **Psychology:** The small real body discloses a deterioration of the bulls' power; the bull's strength has been at least temporarily exhausted.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A time for longs to take profits. The pattern can sometimes precede a meaningful price decline.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - Also called a "deliberation pattern" — a time of deliberation before the market decides its next trend.
  - Not usually a trend reversal in itself; it often signals a pause before continuation.
  - Used to liquidate or protect longs but not to short.
  - Generally more consequential at higher price levels.
  - Can combine with other patterns (e.g., forming a harami or containing a shooting star/hanging man as the small real body).
  - Can be negated if a window opens after the congestion band to complete a bullish high-price gapping play, indicating a resumption of strength.

## Separating Lines (Bullish and Bearish)

- **Type:** Continuation patterns (bullish and bearish) · 2 candle lines.
- **Structure:**
  - Bullish Separating Line: A black real body (especially a relatively long one) is followed by a white candlestick whose opening gaps higher to open at the previous black session's opening price. The white line should be a bullish belt hold (open on the low of the session) and should close higher.
  - Bearish Separating Line: The mirror image — a white real body followed by a black candlestick whose opening gaps lower to open at the previous white session's opening price, and closes lower.
- **Recognition:** Two consecutive opposite-color candlesticks with the same open (in contrast to the counterattack line, which has the same close). The first candle's open equals the second candle's open.
- **Psychology:**
  - Bullish: The black candle suggests the bears may be gaining control, but the gap up to the prior open shows the bears lost control; if the white candle closes higher, the bulls have regained control and the prior price rise should continue.
  - Bearish: Mirror of the above.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Continuation of the prior trend.
- **Nison's notes:** Distinguished from the counterattack line, which has the same close and is a reversal signal. The separating lines have the same open and are a continuation pattern.

## General Principles Stated in the Chapter

- **Trend primacy:** The three most important aspects of the market are trend, trend, and trend. Candlestick signals must be evaluated in the context of the prevailing trend.
- **Pattern completion:** Do not act on a formation until the formation is completed or confirmed. Confirmation may come in the following session.
- **Flexibility:** Candlestick techniques are guidelines, not hard and fast rules. Subtle variations (e.g., a gapping play without a true gap) can still provide actionable signals when the pattern is closely enough resembled.
- **Dual technical reasons:** A pattern is often more reliable when confirmed by two independent reasons (e.g., window support plus old resistance-turned-support).
- **Volume as enhancement:** Patterns (such as the three methods) gain significance when volume on the dominant-color sessions is higher than on the small candlestick sessions.

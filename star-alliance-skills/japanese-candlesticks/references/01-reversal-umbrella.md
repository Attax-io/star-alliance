# Single-Line Reversals — Umbrella Lines & Belt-Hold

## Reversal Patterns — General Philosophy

- **Definition:** Technical clues that can alert technicians to a shift in market psychology and trend.
- **Western counterparts:** Double tops and bottoms, reversal days, head and shoulders, island tops and bottoms.
- **Misnomer caveat:** The term "reversal pattern" is somewhat of a misnomer. Trend reversals usually occur slowly, in stages, as the underlying psychology shifts gears. A reversal signal implies only that the prior trend is likely to change — not necessarily reverse.
- **Possible outcomes after a top reversal signal:**
  - Prior uptrend converts to sideways action, then a new downtrend begins.
  - Old uptrend resumes.
  - Uptrend abruptly reverses into a downtrend.
- **Car analogy:** A car moving forward at 30 m.p.h. puts on the brake lights and stops. The brake light signals the prior forward trend is about to end — but the car may then go in reverse, stay stopped, or go forward again. More clues are needed to know.
- **Preferred terminology:** Nison notes that "trend change patterns" would be a more accurate label, but he uses "reversal patterns" to stay consistent with technical analysis literature.
- **Trading rule:** Place a new position based on a reversal signal only if that signal is in the direction of the major trend. Example — in a bull market, a top reversal pattern is a bearish signal that warrants liquidation of longs but does NOT warrant a short sale. In a prevailing downtrend, the same top reversal formation can be used to initiate short sales.
- **Importance:** Most candlestick indicators are reversal patterns.

## Hammer and Hanging Man Lines

- **Dual nature:** The same candlestick line is bullish in one context and bearish in another, depending on where it appears in the trend. This is analogous to Western island tops and island bottoms (an island after a prolonged uptrend is bearish; the same island after a downtrend is bullish).
- **Appearance:** Long lower shadow, small real body, real body near the top of the daily range.

### Hammer (Bullish Umbrella Line)

- **Type:** Bullish · reversal · single-line pattern.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the upper end of the trading range; long lower shadow; no or very short upper shadow. Real body can be white or black (color not important).
- **Recognition:**
  1. Real body is at the upper end of the trading range. Color of real body is not important.
  2. A long lower shadow should be twice the height of the real body.
  3. It should have no, or a very short, upper shadow.
  - The longer the lower shadow, the shorter the upper shadow, and the smaller the real body, the more meaningful the pattern.
- **Psychology:** The market is "hammering out" a base. The Japanese word for this line is *takuri*, meaning "trying to gauge the depth of the water by feeling for its bottom." The session opened at/near the high, sold off sharply, and then bounced back to close at or near the session's high — showing buyers regained control after an intraday selloff.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bullish reversal when it appears during a downtrend. A white real body is slightly more bullish than a black real body (a white body means the close was at/near the high after the bounce). Confirmation is advisable: a subsequent white candlestick that closes higher than the hammer's close (used after hammers that form in a context with bearish prior price action, e.g., a long black shaven-head/shaven-bottom day or a break of old support).
- **Nison's notes:**
  - The "twice the height of the real body" rule is a guideline, not a rigid rule. A long lower shadow that is not quite twice the real body height can still give a valid reversal signal.
  - Candlestick guidelines, like all pattern recognition guidelines, are flexible.
  - Hammers should always be viewed in the context of prior price action (e.g., a hammer after a strongly bearish preceding session and a break of support requires bullish confirmation before acting on it).
  - Multiple consecutive hammers can reinforce each other and show bulls regaining control after the bears fail to extend new lows.

### Hanging Man (Bearish Umbrella Line)

- **Type:** Bearish · reversal · single-line pattern.
- **Structure:** Small real body at the upper end of the trading range; long lower shadow; no or very short upper shadow. Real body can be white or black. The name derives from the visual: it looks like a hanging man with dangling legs.
- **Recognition:** Same three geometric criteria as the hammer (real body at upper end of range, long lower shadow ideally twice real body height, no/very short upper shadow). Color of the real body is not of major importance.
- **Psychology:** The market is in an uptrend full of bullish energy. On the hanging-man day, the market opens at or near the highs, then sharply sells off, then rallies to close at or near the highs. This may not initially look bearish, but it shows that once selling begins, the market has become vulnerable to a fast break.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Bearish top reversal when it appears after a rally.
  - Confirmation is especially important with the hanging man.
  - A black real body is slightly more bearish than a white real body (a black body means the close could not get back to the opening price level).
  - **General principle:** The greater the down gap between the real body of the hanging-man day and the opening the next day, the more likely the hanging man will mark a top.
  - **Method 1 — Gap-down open:** The next day's open falls under the hanging man's real body, leaving those who bought on the open or close of the hanging-man day "hanging" with a losing position.
  - **Method 2 — Black confirmation candle:** A black real body session that closes lower than the hanging-man session's close.
  - **Method 3 — Consecutive hanging men:** A second hanging-man line can provide the confirmation that the first lacked.
- **Nison's notes:**
  - The same line is bearish (hanging man) or bullish (hammer) depending on its position in the trend; both can occur in the same market and on the same chart.
  - Without bearish confirmation, the uptrend may remain in force (illustrated by hanging-man lines that failed to top a rally and were followed by higher opens or a lack of lower close).
  - A reversal pattern does not mean prices will reverse; it implies the prior trend should end. The post-pattern trend may become sideways before (or instead of) reversing.
  - The "twice the height of the real body" guideline is flexible; a hanging man with a long but not-quite-twice lower shadow can still signal the end of an upturn when other criteria (real body at upper end of range, almost no upper shadow) are met and the next day confirms with a lower close.

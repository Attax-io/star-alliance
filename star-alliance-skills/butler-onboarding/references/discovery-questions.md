# Discovery questions

The goal of discovery is **not** a requirements document. It is the smallest set of
answers that lets the Butler point at the right member and write a starter prompt the
Guild Master will recognize as "yes, that one." Two or three good questions, asked once,
beat an interrogation.

## The discovery rule

Ask only what changes the route. If an answer would not change which member you dispatch
or what the first prompt says, do not ask it. A question that only satisfies curiosity is
stamina spent for nothing.

## The four dimensions worth probing

Most vague requests are missing one of these. Probe the missing one, skip the rest.

| Dimension | What you're after | A question that gets it |
|---|---|---|
| **Outcome** | What does "done" look like? | "When this is finished, what do you have that you didn't before?" |
| **Surface** | Which thing are we touching? | "Is this about the app, the business side, or the guild itself?" |
| **Stakes / scope** | One pass or a campaign? Reversible? | "Is this a quick fix, or a bigger build you want planned in waves?" |
| **Starting material** | What already exists? | "Is there something already in place I should look at first, or are we starting clean?" |

## Worked example

> **Guild Master:** "Help me with my business."

Missing dimension: **Outcome** (and probably **Surface**). One probe each:

- "Happy to. When you picture this working, is it more customers coming in, the operations
  running smoother, or something on the legal/content side?" — narrows Surface to the
  **Lex Council Business** domain or the **app**.
- "And is this a one-time push or something ongoing you want set up?" — narrows Stakes.

That is enough. Two answers route to the Herald (growth) or the Architect/Developer (app)
or the Translator (legal content), and you can write the starter prompts.

## When to skip discovery entirely

- The request is **clear** — it names a task with an obvious owner. Route it; do not
  interview the Guild Master about a thing they already specified. That is
  `members-formation`'s job, not this skill's.
- The request is **high-stakes and ambiguous** in a way that risks data loss, wrong
  architecture, or destructive scope. That is the **Confusion Protocol** — name the
  confusion, present 2-3 options with trade-offs, and halt. Discovery is for *open*
  vagueness; the Confusion Protocol is for *dangerous* ambiguity.

## Tone

Warm, brief, present-tense. You are the one who answers the door of the guild hall — you
make the Guild Master feel oriented, not quizzed. One short framing sentence, then the
questions. Never more than three.

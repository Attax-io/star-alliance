# Volume Price Analysis — The Next Level (Candle Methodology)

This section advances VPA from bar-based analysis to candlestick analysis, establishing five governing principles for reading candles, then walking through the "premier candles" (shooting star, hammer, long-legged doji) and the supporting candle types (wide spread, narrow spread, hanging man, stopping volume, topping out volume) that signal insider behaviour at every phase of the market cycle. The central thesis is that price action alone reveals sentiment, but volume is what places that sentiment in context — quantifying whether the signal is a minor pause or the start of a true reversal.

---

## The Five Principles of Candle Analysis

**What it is:** A governing framework that applies to every candle, every pattern, and every timeframe in VPA.

**Price action:** Read the candle's structure (wicks, body, open, close) before reading volume. The wick is always the first point of focus because it instantly reveals strength, weakness, or indecision. No wick signals strong sentiment in the direction of the close; a narrow body indicates weak sentiment; a wide body indicates strong sentiment.

**Volume:** Volume validates the price action revealed by the candle. The volume bar either confirms the candle's message or creates an anomaly that contradicts it.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Principle Five is the core logic: volume must confirm price. Start with the candle (the price message), then look for validation or anomaly from the volume bar. Effort and result must match — high volume with no price progress is an anomaly, just as low volume with a violent price swing is an anomaly.

**Insiders / psychology:** Wicks reveal where the insiders pushed price and where the market rejected it. A lack of wick reveals the insiders ran the session in one direction without opposition. The candle is never read in isolation — Principle Four states the same candle type has a completely different meaning depending on its location in the broader trend or consolidation phase.

**Signal:** These principles are diagnostic — they tell the trader what to look at first, in what order, and how to weigh the evidence. A single candle is significant; multiple appearances of the same candle in the same price area exponentially increase the bullish or bearish sentiment.

**Takeaway:** Always read the candle first (wicks, body, location in trend), then test that reading with volume. The same candle in a different location is a different signal.

## The Shooting Star Candle

**What it is:** The first of the three "premier" candles in VPA. Price rises during the session, then falls to close at or near the opening price. A classic signal of weakness, present in every market and timeframe.

**Price action:** Long upper wick; the body is small and the close sits at or near the open. The price has risen and then been overwhelmed by sellers before the close.

**Volume:** The price action is always weakness — there is no anomaly with a shooting star. Volume simply quantifies the strength of that weakness. Low volume = minor weakness or possible short-term pullback. Average volume = relatively strong signal. High or ultra-high volume = the professional money and insiders are selling out, and a major move is coming.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Volume always confirms the price action in a shooting star. There is never an anomaly. The only question is the magnitude of the confirmation. The candle is the signal; volume is the severity rating.

**Insiders / psychology:** In an uptrend, the shooting star reveals that the insiders have pushed price higher but found no demand willing to follow, so price falls back. In a downtrend, it confirms ongoing weakness, often appearing shortly after the start of the move lower as a test of demand. After a selling climax, a low-volume shooting star inside sideways congestion signals the insiders are testing demand, not buying. Deeper in a downtrend waterfall, the shooting star on above-average volume means the insiders are still selling inventory collected from panicked sellers into short-term pullbacks, unloading at "wholesale" rather than letting it sit in inventory.

**Signal:** Potential weakness — never an immediate reversal on its own. The candle signals that the market has been pushed higher and demand has failed. Significance increases exponentially with: (a) multiple consecutive shooting stars in the same price region (three at the same high with rising volume is a strong top), (b) appearance after a recent breakout from accumulation with a successful test (then reversal is unlikely), (c) confirmation from higher timeframe VPA, and (d) appearance at known price congestion / resistance areas.

**Takeaway:** Wait for confirmation from subsequent candles and from higher timeframes before acting. A single shooting star is a cue to pay attention, not a cue to trade.

## The Hammer Candle

**What it is:** The second premier candle. Price falls during the session, then recovers to close at or near the opening price. The classic candle of "hammering out a bottom." Signals either temporary strength or a longer-term reversal.

**Price action:** Long lower wick; small body with the close at or near the open. Selling was absorbed with enough force for buyers to overwhelm sellers and recover the session.

**Volume:** Like the shooting star, there is never an anomaly with a hammer. The price action is unambiguously strength; volume quantifies it. Low volume = minor strength, reversal is likely minor. Average volume = stronger signs of a possible reversal, often an intraday scalp opportunity. High or ultra-high volume = the insiders are buying heavily as part of a buying climax.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Volume always confirms. A single hammer — even on above-average volume — will not halt a fast-moving downtrend, because the insiders still have inventory to clear. The power is revealed only when two or three hammers appear in sequence on rising volume. At that point, a buying climax is in progress, and the trader waits for the insiders to finish before going long.

**Insiders / psychology:** The hammer signals "forced buying" by the insiders during a price waterfall. They have to clear inventory bought at higher levels before they can take price lower again. A hammer is often followed by a shooting star (insiders pushing price up then selling into it) as they manage inventory. The short squeeze — pause, pull back higher, continue lower — is the standard rhythm of a waterfall. Without a successful test (a hammer on low volume after the climax), the bottom is not confirmed.

**Signal:** Strength. Low-volume hammers with a follow-on shooting star on average-to-high volume, in a downtrend, confirm the trend is still intact and the position can be held. High or ultra-high volume hammer is the early warning of a true reversal; shorts should take profit and prepare for a long entry on breakout. Subsequent tests on low volume are required for confirmation.

**Takeaway:** A hammer is a signal of strength, but only the volume tells you how much. Always demand a successful test (low volume) before committing to a long position.

## The Long Legged Doji Candle

**What it is:** The third premier candle. Open and close are at or very near the same level, with long upper and lower wicks extending well beyond the body. Signals indecision — bullish and bearish sentiment are equally balanced. The only doji variant significant in VPA.

**Price action:** Small body (essentially a cross) with both upper and lower wicks long in comparison to the body. The market opened, moved sharply in one direction, reversed sharply in the other, then returned to the open. Wild intra-session swings, but the fulcrum of price remained in the middle.

**Volume:** Unlike the shooting star and hammer, the long-legged doji CAN produce an anomaly. A long-legged doji should always be validated by a minimum of average volume, preferably high or ultra-high. Low volume on a long-legged doji is the classic anomaly: huge price result (wide swings) with no effort (low volume).

**Reading (effort vs result):** Volatile price action requires effort. If volume is low, the effort is missing — the result is unearned. This is the signature of insider manipulation: the market makers are racking price around violently to trigger stops and limit orders, often around a news release, without themselves buying or selling.

**Insiders / psychology:** The long-legged doji appears most often during major news releases (the canonical example is the US monthly Non Farm Payroll data). The insiders use the news as a catalyst to whipsaw traders, taking out stops on both sides. The price is being moved violently while the insiders sit on the sidelines. The market is not validating the news — it is being driven by stop-hunting.

**Signal:** A long-legged doji on high or ultra-high volume can signal a genuine trend reversal (direction depends on the prior trend). A long-legged doji on low volume is NOT a reversal signal — it is an insider trap. The right response is to stay out and wait for subsequent candles to reveal what the insiders are doing next.

**Takeaway:** Whenever news is released, the volume reaction immediately tells you whether insiders are joining the move (validation) or standing aside (anomaly and trap). This is the fastest way to learn VPA in practice.

## Volume and News Releases

**What it is:** The interaction between volume and fundamental news is itself a diagnostic tool. News events produce the cleanest, most visible volume surges in any market.

**Price action:** Wide spread candles in the direction implied by the news; or a long-legged doji on the release.

**Volume:** Surge in volume on a clear directional move = the insiders are joining in and the news is validated. Low volume on a large move = the news is being ignored, and the move is mechanical or manufactured. Low volume on a long-legged doji during a release = stop-hunting.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Volume validates the news release. If a "big number" is released and risk assets surge on strong, rising volume, the move is real and insiders are in. If the move occurs on low volume, the move is fake and the insiders are not participating.

**Insiders / psychology:** The media is the insiders' primary vehicle. News releases are the moment the insiders act on, against, or aside from the public narrative. Watching volume during releases reveals which side of the trade the smart money is on.

**Signal:** Validate or ignore. Always wait for the volume to confirm before acting on news.

**Takeaway:** Use news releases as a free VPA training ground. They concentrate every variable — effort, result, anomaly, validation, trap — into a single observable moment.

## Wide Spread Candles

**What it is:** A candle whose body covers a large price range and closes at or near the high (up candle) or low (down candle). Sentiment for the session is strong — either strongly bullish or strongly bearish.

**Price action:** Large body, close at or near the extreme. No long opposing wick. The session is decisively one-directional.

**Volume:** Should be "strong" — above average — to validate the strong sentiment. If the wide spread appears on below-average or low volume, this is a warning: the price is being marked higher (or lower) with little effort. Many retail traders will join the move; the volume reveals the insiders are not.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Wide spread + high volume = effort and result in agreement, insiders are joining the move. Wide spread + low volume = price marked without effort, warning bell. If in a position, exit. If not in a position, stay out and wait for the next signal.

**Insiders / psychology:** On validation, the insiders are moving with the trend and absorbing the opposite side. On anomaly, the insiders are not participating and the move is being manufactured — typically marking up into a supply zone or marking down into demand.

**Signal:** Validation = strength and continuation. Anomaly = weakness disguised as strength; expect a reversal or pause.

**Takeaway:** A wide spread candle that is not supported by volume is one of the most reliable reversal warnings in VPA.

## Narrow Spread Candles

**What it is:** A candle with a small body and limited price movement. Sentiment is weak. Markets in general rise slowly and fall fast, so narrow candles are the building blocks of consolidation, pauses, and reversals.

**Price action:** Small body. Can be either direction; what matters is the lack of progress.

**Volume:** Low volume on a narrow spread is normal and uninteresting — effort and result agree. The interesting cases are the anomalies: above-average or high volume on a narrow spread. This is effort without result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** High volume should produce a wide spread. When it doesn't, the market is showing resistance to higher (or lower) prices. The insiders are struggling at this level. The next candle is likely a shooting star (in an uptrend) or a hammer (in a downtrend) to confirm the shift.

**Insiders / psychology:** In an uptrend with high volume and a narrow spread, the insiders are starting to absorb demand but the price is resistant — early distribution, market topping. In a downtrend with high volume and a narrow spread, the insiders are starting to support the market — early accumulation, market bottoming. This is the first sign of a potential reversal.

**Signal:** Anomaly = warning. Watch the next one or two candles for confirmation via shooting star, hammer, or long-legged doji.

**Takeaway:** The interesting narrow spread candles are always the ones with high volume — effort without result is the insiders signalling the end of the move.

## The Hanging Man Candle

**What it is:** A candle that looks identical to a hammer (long lower wick, small body, close at or near the open) but appears at the top of a bullish trend instead of the bottom. It is the same price action, the opposite meaning (Principle Four). The body can be red or blue; the close must be at or near the open.

**Price action:** Identical to the hammer — sharp sell-off during the session, recovery to close at or near the high. The difference is the location: top of a bullish trend.

**Volume:** Should be above average to register as a sign of weakness. The hanging man is a signal of selling pressure entering a market that has been rising steadily on rising volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The candle itself is the first sign of selling pressure in an established uptrend. The buyers are still supporting the market (the recovery), but the selling has appeared for the first time. This is the market moving toward an overbought condition.

**Insiders / psychology:** The insiders have spotted the same weakness. They are beginning to plan distribution. The candle is an early warning, not an immediate trigger.

**Signal:** Potential weakness, early warning only. A hanging man is validated by a follow-on shooting star in the next few candles, particularly with above-average or high volume. A hanging man immediately followed by a shooting star is an excellent combination. Even a later shooting star at the same level is a strong confirming signal.

**Takeaway:** The hanging man is the same price action as the hammer but the opposite message, because the trend context is different. Always read the candle in relation to where it sits in the larger move.

## Stopping Volume

**What it is:** The price action of the insiders applying the brakes to a falling market. The first sign that a downtrend is being arrested. Multiple sessions are usually required — markets do not reverse on a dime.

**Price action:** In a price waterfall, deep lower wicks with relatively deep bodies. The close should be in the upper half of the open-to-close range (a guideline, not a hard rule). The sequence typically runs: deep lower wick with deeper body → narrower body with deep wick → narrower body with deep wick → first hammer candle. This is the canonical "almost perfect" sequence for stopping volume.

**Volume:** Rising across the sequence. Each session the insiders absorb more of the selling pressure. By the time the first hammer appears, the buying is starting to overwhelm the selling.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A single session cannot reverse a waterfall driven by panic selling — the pressure is too great. The insiders are using multiple sessions to absorb the flow, just as a supertanker continues for miles after its engines are switched off. The wicks are the visible sign of the insiders stepping in.

**Insiders / psychology:** The insiders are buying into panic. They are filling their warehouses at wholesale prices. Stopping volume is the precursor to the buying climax — the moment when the last remnants of selling pressure are mopped up and the insiders are ready to drive the market higher.

**Signal:** Impending strength and potential reversal from bearish to bullish. This is the precursor to the buying climax; full alert for the move higher that follows.

**Takeaway:** Stopping volume is a multi-session signal. Wait for the full sequence and the buying climax to complete before committing to a long position; the trade is the one that comes after.

## Topping Out Volume

**What it is:** The mirror image of stopping volume — the insiders applying the brakes to a rising market. The first sign that an uptrend is exhausting. Multiple sessions are required; the market does not reverse on a dime.

**Price action:** Deep upper wicks on each subsequent candle. The spreads narrow and the price rise slows, creating an arcing pattern. The last candle in the canonical sequence is the shooting star. The market is being driven by greed and fear of missing out, but the insiders are selling into the demand they themselves have created.

**Volume:** High and rising. The insiders are selling into the demand, but the demand is now struggling to absorb the supply. Above-average to high or ultra-high volume across the sequence.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Rising price on rising volume looks healthy, but the narrowing spreads and deep upper wicks reveal that each push higher is meeting more supply. The momentum is fading even though volume is still high — the insiders are doing the work, and the public is following.

**Insiders / psychology:** The insiders have driven demand to its peak. They are now selling into it. This is the distribution phase, which will culminate in a selling climax before the market turns. The arcing pattern of narrowing spreads with deep upper wicks is the visual signature of a market running out of buyers.

**Signal:** Impending weakness and potential reversal from bullish to bearish. The final shooting star in the sequence marks the transition into the distribution phase proper.

**Takeaway:** The arcing pattern — narrowing spreads with deep upper wicks on high volume — is one of the most reliable topping signatures in VPA. Recognise it before the shooting star appears and you exit near the top.

## Reading the Cycle: From Signal to Position Management

**What it is:** The integration of every candle, every volume reading, and every timeframe into a single continuous read of insider behaviour — not just for entry, but for holding and exit.

**Price action:** No specific shape. The principle applies to any trend at any stage.

**Volume:** No specific level. The principle is to compare the current volume to what the price action implies and to recent context.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Every candle, every sequence, every timeframe is a question: is the volume validating the price action, or is it an anomaly? Validation = stay in or add. Anomaly = exit or stay out.

**Insiders / psychology:** The insiders never stop acting. They are accumulating, marking up, distributing, marking down, testing, and stopping throughout every phase. The candle types are the language they speak. VPA is the translation.

**Signal:** A single methodology that gets the trader into low-risk positions, keeps them in those positions through minor pullbacks (using VPA to confirm the trend is intact), and gets them out at the right time. The hardest part of trading is not entry; it is staying in and exiting with discipline.

**Takeaway:** VPA is an art, not a science. Multi-timeframe analysis, candle pattern analysis, VPA, and price congestion are all used together to answer the one question: is this a minor pullback or a true reversal?

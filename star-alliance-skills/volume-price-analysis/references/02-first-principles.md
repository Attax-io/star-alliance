# Volume Price Analysis — First Principles

This chapter establishes the foundational mindset and analytical framework for VPA trading, built on six guiding principles, Wyckoff's laws of effort vs result and cause and effect, and a three-step process of micro/macro/global analysis. The core logic is that volume (effort) must validate price (result) — when it does, the move is genuine; when it doesn't, an anomaly is signalling potential weakness, strength, or a trap by the insiders and market makers.

## Principle 1: Art Not Science

**What it is:** VPA is a subjective, interpretive skill, not a rule-based system or algorithmic technique.

**Key insight:** Most of the analysis is subjective — comparing price behaviour against the associated volume, looking for confirmations or anomalies, while comparing volume to its own history. Software cannot replicate this judgement.

**Application:** Once learnt, VPA can be applied to any instrument, in any market, on any time frame. The only ongoing cost is a live volume data feed.

**Takeaway:** Stop trying to automate judgement. Learn the art of interpretation through screen time.

## Principle 2: Patience

**What it is:** A signal is a warning, not an entry trigger — the market takes time to turn, like a super tanker.

**Price action:** The market rarely stops dead and reverses. After a directional move, expect sideways congestion as the remaining buyers or sellers are "mopped up."

**Volume:** Volume behaviour during the mopping-up phase is the key signal — absorption by the smart money precedes the next move.

**Insiders / psychology:** Some sellers (or buyers) hang on after a trend, believing it will continue. They get whipsawed out gradually as the market makes minor probes in the opposite direction. The market makers use this time to accumulate or distribute their full position.

**Signal:** Any single signal is a warning of impending change, not confirmation. The reversal will come, but not instantly from one candle.

**Takeaway:** Do not act immediately on a signal. Wait for validation. The "first pass" mops up most of the spill; the "second pass" completes the job.

## Principle 3: It's All Relative

**What it is:** Volume analysis is comparative, not absolute — consistency of the data feed matters far more than its theoretical accuracy.

**Key insight:** Candle closes vary from broker to broker, platform to platform, and depend on clock speed and geography. Tick volume may be imperfect, but perfection is irrelevant.

**Application:** Compare each volume bar against previous volume bars on your own feed. Comparing imperfect data with imperfect data cancels out the imperfection.

**Takeaway:** Stop obsessing about the provenance of the volume feed. Use one consistent feed and judge each bar relative to its predecessors. 70% accuracy with consistency beats 100% accuracy that varies.

## Principle 4: Practise Makes Perfect

**What it is:** VPA proficiency requires dedicated screen time, but the skill is permanent once mastered.

**Application:** The same techniques work on tick charts, intraday, daily, and weekly charts — equally valid for scalpers, day traders, and long-term investors.

**Takeaway:** After a few weeks or months of chart watching, you will begin to interpret and forecast the market's twists and turns. Invest the time.

## Principle 5: Technical Analysis

**What it is:** VPA is one component of a broader analytical toolkit, used in conjunction with other techniques for "triangulation."

**Key companions:**
- **Support and resistance** — the zones where the market pauses to execute its mopping-up operations
- **Trends** — context for judging the significance of any single signal
- **Price patterns** — validation of VPA signals

**Signal:** A breakout from a region of consolidation, coupled with confirming volume, is always a strong signal.

**Takeaway:** VPA tells you *what* the insiders are doing; technical analysis tells you *where* they are likely to do it. Use both.

## Principle 6: Validation or Anomaly

**What it is:** The two outcomes VPA constantly searches for in every single bar and every bar group.

**Validation:** Volume confirms price — the move is genuine, and continuation can be expected. Maintain any existing position in the direction of the trend.

**Anomaly:** Volume contradicts price — a warning of potential change. Alarm bells ring.

**Insiders / psychology:** An anomaly reveals that the market makers and specialists are not joining in the move. Either they are testing the waters (in a trap), absorbing supply/demand at a level, or quietly distributing/accumulating against the public flow.

**Signal:** The first step when an anomaly appears is to establish *where* in the trend it has occurred. A minor pull-back anomaly requires a different response than a major trend exhaustion anomaly.

**Takeaway:** Every analytical decision in VPA reduces to one question: is the volume validating this price, or is it an anomaly?

## Wyckoff's Third Law: Effort vs Result

**What it is:** Any meaningful price change requires a corresponding amount of volume. Effort (volume) and result (price spread) must match.

**Application:** This law applies equally to rising and falling markets. It takes effort for the market to rise *and* it takes effort for the market to fall. A market that moves with little volume is moving on thin effort and is vulnerable.

**Two levels of validation:**
1. **Per candle:** Does this single bar's volume match its price spread?
2. **Per trend:** Does the volume pattern across a group of candles match the directional move?

**Takeaway:** Big result, little effort = anomaly. Small result, big effort = anomaly. Matched effort and result = validation.

## Wyckoff's Second Law: Cause and Effect

**What it is:** The extent of a future price move (effect) is proportional to the size and duration of the preparation that preceded it (cause).

**Application:** A reversal signalled over a few candles is a small cause and will produce a small, short-lived effect — a minor pull-back. A reversal that builds over days, weeks, or months (a long sideways consolidation) is a large cause and will produce a dramatic, long-lasting trend move.

**Takeaway:** Be patient with the "mopping up" phase. The longer it lasts, the larger the eventual move. This is the time element of VPA.

## Validation: Wide Spread Up Candle, High Volume

**What it is:** A strong bullish bar where the volume matches the price effort.

**Price action:** Wide body, small wicks to top and bottom. Closes near the high of the session.

**Volume:** Well above average — effort matches result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Validated. A large change in price warrants a large volume bar. The price move is genuine and has not been manipulated.

**Insiders / psychology:** The specialists and insiders are joining in the move. Market sentiment is confirmed.

**Signal:** The market is bullish. Maintain any long position until an anomaly appears.

**Takeaway:** Rising prices + rising volume = genuine strength, low-risk long entry.

## Validation: Narrow Spread Up Candle, Low Volume

**What it is:** A modest bullish bar where the volume matches the modest price effort.

**Price action:** Narrow body, small wicks. Price has risen only marginally.

**Volume:** Well below average — effort matches result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Validated. A small price move requires only a small amount of volume.

**Insiders / psychology:** No smart money activity required to justify this move. The market is simply drifting.

**Signal:** Neither bullish nor bearish on its own. Establish context via surrounding bars and trend position.

**Takeaway:** Small result, small effort = quiet market, no cause for alarm.

## Validation: Multiple Bar Rising Prices With Rising Volume

**What it is:** A bullish trend in which each successive candle has a wider spread and higher volume, and the overall trend shows rising prices and rising volume.

**Price action:** Each candle is wider than the last. Wicks are small or absent. The trend is clean and directional.

**Volume:** Rising bar on bar. The trend itself is validated by rising volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Validated on two levels — each individual candle and the trend as a whole. Effort vs result applies not only to a single candle but also to a group of candles forming a trend.

**Insiders / psychology:** The specialists and insiders are joining in the move. The volume is buying volume — if there were selling volume, it would show up in the price action as wicks or pullbacks. The market is receptive to higher prices.

**Signal:** Genuine, low-risk long entry. "We join the insiders and buy."

**Takeaway:** Rising prices = rising volume. If you see this, the public, the specialists, and the insiders are all aligned.

## Validation: Multiple Bar Falling Prices With Rising Volume

**What it is:** A bearish trend in which each successive candle has a wider spread and higher volume.

**Price action:** Each candle is wider than the last. Wicks are small or absent. The trend is clean and directional downward.

**Volume:** Rising bar on bar. The trend itself is validated by rising volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Validated on two levels. Effort vs result applies to falling markets as much as rising ones — gravity analogies fail here; the market requires effort to fall just as it does to rise.

**Insiders / psychology:** The volume is selling volume. The specialists are joining in the move, selling. Market sentiment is firmly bearish.

**Signal:** Genuine, low-risk short entry. The public and the insiders are aligned to the downside.

**Takeaway:** Falling prices + rising volume = genuine weakness. Do not fight the move.

## Anomaly: Wide Spread Up Candle, Low Volume

**What it is:** A strong-looking bullish bar that is unsupported by the volume — a "big result from little effort" trap.

**Price action:** Wide body up, but volume is low.

**Volume:** Below average — effort does not match result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Anomaly. A wide spread up candle should be supported by high volume. The absence of volume means the market is not joining the move.

**Insiders / psychology:** The market makers are testing the level of buying interest. They push the price higher to "feel out" sentiment, but do not commit until they are sure buyers will follow. This is the classic opening-bell pattern in equity markets, repeated by market makers for the main index and individual stocks. They are pitching the price to discover the level at which genuine demand appears.

**Signal:** Potential trap-up move. Early warning of weakness. If long, begin questioning the position.

**Takeaway:** A wide spread up candle on low volume is a price action trader's dream and a VPA trader's warning. Volume reveals what price alone conceals.

## Anomaly: Narrow Spread Up Candle, High Volume

**What it is:** A small price advance on huge volume — a "huge effort for a tiny result" trap and the classic end-of-trend exhaustion signal.

**Price action:** Narrow body, but volume is very high.

**Volume:** Well above average — effort grossly exceeds result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Anomaly. The market has applied enormous effort but the price refuses to move. Further effort is meeting resistance. Buyers and sellers are battling at this level, with neither able to break the other decisively.

**Insiders / psychology:** In an established bullish trend, this is the moment the market makers and insiders begin selling out. Longs are taking profits after the trend run. New buyers (who always buy late) keep arriving, but the sellers' volume outweighs the buyers' ability to push price higher. The market makers know the public buys at the top — they exploit trader fear of "missing an opportunity." It is also common in a downtrend reversal, where the selling is being absorbed by the buyers.

**Signal:** Early warning of weakness at the top of a bullish trend, or strength at the bottom of a bearish trend. The market is overbought (or oversold in the mirror case). The insiders have shown their hand.

**Analogy:** Driving up an icy hill — power increases, wheels spin, but the car goes nowhere. Eventually it slides backwards with momentum. That backward slide is the next directional move.

**Takeaway:** A narrow candle with high volume is one of the most powerful VPA signals. It says the market is struggling and the insiders are moving against the public flow.

## Anomaly: Multiple Bar Uptrend Showing Decline

**What it is:** A bullish trend in which the second candle shows effort without result, and subsequent candles show rising price with falling volume.

**Price action:**
- Candle 1: Narrow spread up, low volume — validated.
- Candle 2: Slightly wider spread, but high volume — anomaly.
- Candle 3: Wider spread up, but lower volume than candle 2 — anomaly.
- Candle 4: Very wide spread up, even lower volume — anomaly.

**Volume:** Rising prices should be associated with rising volume, but here volume is falling away across the trend.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Multiple anomalies. The trend is being marked up, but the effort to do so is decreasing. Buying pressure is draining away. The market makers are no longer joining the move; they are withdrawing and trapping longs.

**Insiders / psychology:** The market makers and specialists have seen the weakness (which first appeared on candle 2) and are selling into the buying, clearing their own positions before marking the market lower. They maintain the appearance of a bullish market by pushing price higher, but the volume tells the truth — they are not committing their own capital. The high volume on candle 2 was the moment the selling overwhelmed the buying. Subsequent candles show the buyers losing steam as longs take profits and the specialists withdraw.

**Signal:** Pre-cursor to weakness. Could be a minor pull-back (small cause, small effect) or the start of a major reversal (large cause, large effect) — only context via support/resistance and global chart view can determine which.

**Takeaway:** An anomaly on one candle is a warning. The same anomaly confirmed on the next two or three candles is a high-confidence signal. Always wait for confirmation across multiple bars.

## Anomaly: Multiple Bar Downtrend Showing Absorption (Price Waterfall)

**What it is:** A sharp sell-off in which the second candle shows effort without result, and subsequent candles show falling price with falling volume.

**Price action:**
- Candle 1: Low volume — validated.
- Candle 2: Marginally wider spread, but high or very high volume — anomaly.
- Candle 3: Wide spread down, but only average-to-low volume — anomaly.
- Candle 4: Wide spread down, low volume — anomaly.

**Volume:** Falling prices should be associated with rising volume, but volume is falling away across the trend.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Multiple anomalies. The trend is being marked down, but the effort to do so is decreasing. Selling pressure is draining away. The market is becoming resistant to lower prices.

**Insiders / psychology:** The market makers and specialists have spotted the change in sentiment (first signalled on candle 2) and are buying into the selling, absorbing supply at this level. The high volume on candle 2 was the moment the buying overwhelmed the selling. They continue to mark prices lower to give the appearance of a bearish market and trap additional short-sellers into weak positions, but they are no longer selling — they have already bought their stock on candle 2.

**Signal:** Pre-cursor to strength. Could be a minor bounce or the start of a major reversal.

**Takeaway:** The mirror image of the uptrend anomaly. The same logic applies in reverse: candle 2 reveals the insiders' hand, candles 3 and 4 confirm it.

## The Three-Step Analytical Process

**What it is:** A structured, repeatable method for applying VPA to any chart.

**Step 1 — Micro:** Analyse each individual candle as it forms. Compare its volume to previous bars. Judge it as low, average, high, or very high. Ask: is volume validating this price, or is this an anomaly?

**Step 2 — Macro:** Analyse each candle in the context of the surrounding few candles. Is the trend itself validating (rising prices with rising volume, or falling prices with rising volume), or is an anomaly appearing across the group?

**Step 3 — Global:** Step back to the full chart. Establish where the price is in the larger trend. Bring in support and resistance, trend lines, candle patterns, and chart patterns to provide the framework against which the micro and macro signals are judged.

**Analogy:** A zoom lens in reverse — start close in on one candle, then the adjacent candles, then the entire chart.

**Takeaway:** The process is identical on every chart, in every time frame, in every market. Mastery takes a few months of daily chart watching.

## Multiple Time Frame Analysis

**What it is:** Using three (or more) time frames together to gauge the strength of a potential reversal and align trades with the dominant trend.

**Setup:** The "three-lane highway":
- **Fast lane (e.g. 5-minute chart):** The closest perspective on what the market is doing right now.
- **Middle lane (e.g. 15-minute chart):** Where trades are actually taken.
- **Slow lane (e.g. 30-minute chart):** The longer-term context and direction.

**Application:** A change first visible on the 5-minute chart is a small cause. If it ripples through to the 15-minute chart, it is a medium cause with a larger effect. If it ultimately reaches the 30-minute chart, the cause is large, the move has taken the longest to build, and the effect will be the most powerful and long-lasting.

**Analogy 1 — Clockwork car:** The more you wind the mechanism, the further the car runs. The longer the cause builds, the further the eventual trend travels.

**Analogy 2 — Ripples in a pond:** A stone dropped in the centre creates ripples that spread outward. Market sentiment moves from the fastest time frame outward to the slowest. Once a change appears in the slowest time frame, the move has the most momentum.

**Takeaway:** Combining VPA with multiple time frames unites the third law (effort vs result) and the second law (cause and effect) into a single approach. It works for any combination of time frames, from tick charts to weekly charts, for any trader from scalper to investor.

## Trend Context: The Framework for Every Signal

**What it is:** The recognition that the meaning of any single VPA signal depends on where in the trend it appears.

**Application:** A trend is always relative to the time frame being traded. A one-hour trend on a 5-minute chart is as valid as a multi-month trend on a daily chart. All that matters is that price has moved in one direction for some period in that time frame.

**Bearings — where are we in the trend?**
- At a possible bottom or top (major reversal territory)
- Halfway through a trend (minor pull-back territory)

**Tools for establishing bearings:** Support and resistance, candle patterns, individual candles, trend lines. These provide the perspective and framework within which VPA signals are interpreted.

**Takeaway:** The first response to any anomaly is to establish position in the trend. Without context, a signal is just a bar; with context, it becomes a trade.

## The Two Risks in Trading

**What it is:** Every trade carries two distinct forms of risk, only one of which is easily measured.

**Financial risk:** The monetary loss on the trade itself. Easily quantified and managed via simple money management rules (e.g. the 1% rule).

**Risk on the trade itself:** The probability that the trade is wrong. Difficult to quantify by conventional means, but VPA makes it quantifiable — a validated move is a low-risk entry; an anomaly warns that the trade carries elevated risk.

**Takeaway:** VPA is the analytical tool for assessing the second, harder form of risk. Combined with money management, it produces confident, low-risk trading decisions based on two leading indicators: price and volume.

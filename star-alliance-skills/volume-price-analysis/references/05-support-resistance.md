# Support and Resistance — The Foundation Stones of Price Analysis

This chapter introduces the first major analytical framework built on top of Volume Price Analysis (VPA). It explains how price creates visible "floors" and "ceilings" through the emotional behaviour of market participants, how to identify those levels, and how to validate any break of them with volume. The core logic throughout is that volume (effort) must confirm the price move (result), otherwise the move is a trap set by the insiders.

## The Three States of Price

**What it is:** The only three modes a market can be in at any time — trending up, trending down, or moving sideways in a consolidation/congestion phase.

**Price action:** Up = higher highs and higher lows. Down = lower highs and lower lows. Sideways = a contained oscillating range with a defined ceiling and floor.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Trending phases are the minority condition; sideways congestion is the dominant state (commonly cited as ~70% of market time vs ~30% trending). The implication is that a trader must be skilled at reading congestion, not just breakouts.

**Insiders / psychology:** Three forces drive congestion: (1) anticipation of major fundamental news (price narrows ahead of the release), (2) buying or selling climaxes in which warehouses/positions are being filled or emptied, and (3) the market returning to old price zones where weak traders are trapped and use the revisit to exit at breakeven or a small loss.

**Takeaway:** Always be prepared for sideways action; it is the default state of the market.

## The Floor and Ceiling Analogy

**What it is:** A conceptual model of support and resistance as a horizontal channel with a "floor" of support below and a "ceiling" of resistance above.

**Price action:** A repeating zig-zag of failures at the same upper level and bounces at the same lower level, creating a band of contained price action.

**Volume:** Volume during the contained moves is generally unremarkable; it is the breakout from the channel that requires exceptional volume (covered below).

**Reading (effort vs result):** Within the channel, effort (volume) is matched by result (contained price) — the market is balanced. The transition out of the channel is where the effort/result analysis becomes critical.

**Insiders / psychology:** At the ceiling, weak buyers who chased the last rally are trapped and relieved to exit at breakeven on a retest. At the floor, patient buyers who waited for a pullback accumulate. The constant flow of weak hands exiting at the extremes and being replaced by the next wave of weak hands creates the invisible bands, which are then made visible by joining the highs and lows.

**Takeaway:** Support and resistance are not lines on a chart — they are dense populations of trapped traders whose emotional exits enforce the level.

## Fear and Greed at the Top of a Bull Trend

**What it is:** The emotional sequence that builds a resistance ceiling after a long bullish run.

**Price action:** Market makes a high, reverses, then retests that high — the retest is the ceiling.

**Insiders / psychology:** At the first peak, greed and fear-of-missing-out drive emotional buyers in just as the trend exhausts. They are instantly trapped. On the pullback, patient buyers enter. On the retest, the trapped weak buyers exit in relief at a small loss or breakeven, generating the supply that forms resistance. The patient buyers who entered at the low have never been in loss — they are hopeful, not fearful — and tend to hold through the retest. Each new wave of weak buyers at the top is then liquidated by the next wave of strong sellers.

**Takeaway:** The "staircase" of resistance is created by successive waves of emotionally weak buyers being harvested at the same level; price is the footprint of that harvesting.

## Fear and Greed at the Bottom of a Bear Trend

**What it is:** The mirror-image emotional sequence that builds a support floor after a prolonged downtrend.

**Price action:** Market makes a low, bounces, then retests that low — the retest is the floor.

**Insiders / psychology:** Weak emotional traders finally overcome their fear and sell short just before the bounce, locking themselves into immediate losses. The bounce traps them. When price returns to their entry level, they cover in relief at a small loss. Strong traders are selling into the tops of the waves from a position of profit, and their activity creates the supply that defends the floor from above.

**Takeaway:** Floors are built the same way ceilings are — by successive waves of weak traders being harvested at the same level, but on the short side.

## First Principle — Rubber Bands, Not Rods of Steel

**What it is:** Support and resistance lines are elastic, not impenetrable.

**Price action:** The market may overshoot a level by a few ticks/pips, then snap back inside the range. The level is breached but not closed beyond.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A shallow overshoot with no follow-through and no expanding volume is a probe, not a breakout. A close clearly beyond the level is the threshold for a valid test of the band.

**Insiders / psychology:** The market makers test the bands to harvest stops on both sides and gauge supply/demand before committing to a real move.

**Takeaway:** Treat every S/R level as elastic; require a decisive close and confirming volume before treating it as broken.

## Second Principle — Wyckoff's Law of Cause and Effect

**What it is:** The size of the eventual move is proportional to the size and duration of the consolidation that preceded it.

**Price action:** A long, narrow, deep consolidation produces a long, wide, powerful move away. A shallow, brief consolidation produces a shallow, brief move.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The accumulation of effort (volume absorbed during congestion) is the "cause"; the trend that erupts is the "effect." The two are scaled to each other.

**Insiders / psychology:** The insiders use the congestion phase to build their positions. The longer they are allowed to accumulate or distribute quietly, the larger the inventory they acquire, and therefore the larger the move they will subsequently drive.

**Takeaway:** Range a breakout by the size of the base it came from — on every timeframe.

## Third Principle — Identifying Congestion in Real Time

**What it is:** A technique for spotting the start of a consolidation phase as it forms, not after the fact.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A single reversal bar is not a congestion phase; it is only a "possible short-term reversal in prospect." Two opposing reversal bars back-to-back confirm the entry into congestion.

**Insiders / psychology:** The first isolated pivot is the first sign that the prior trend's effort is being absorbed. The second opposing pivot confirms that the market has indeed shifted from trending to balanced.

**Takeaway:** Wait for both pivots before drawing the ceiling and floor of a congestion zone.

## Isolated Pivot High

**What it is:** A three-bar/candle reversal pattern marking a possible short-term top.

**Price action:** The centre candle posts both a higher high and a higher low than the candles on either side of it. The pattern is bracketed by two lower candles.

**Volume:** Typically accompanied by climactic or near-climactic volume, indicating effort that fails to extend the trend.

**Reading (effort vs result):** High effort (high volume) into a small result (a single candle that fails to follow through) is the textbook anomaly of absorption. Effort is being matched and neutralised by supply.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart money is using the demand surge to distribute stock to weak emotional buyers. The high volume is their selling, masked inside an up bar.

**Signal:** A warning of possible short-term weakness; the first leg of a possible congestion entrance. Becomes a confirmed ceiling only when followed by an isolated pivot low.

**Takeaway:** One pivot is a hint, not a signal.

## Isolated Pivot Low

**What it is:** A three-bar/candle reversal pattern marking a possible short-term bottom — the mirror of the pivot high.

**Price action:** The centre candle posts both a lower low and a lower high than the candles on either side. The pattern is bracketed by two higher candles.

**Volume:** Typically accompanied by high or climactic volume on the down bar — selling effort that fails to extend the downtrend.

**Reading (effort vs result):** High effort (heavy selling) into a small result (a single candle that fails to continue lower) is absorption of supply by the insiders. The market is being supported quietly.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart money is using the panic selling to accumulate stock from weak emotional sellers. The high volume is their buying, masked inside a down bar.

**Signal:** A warning of possible short-term strength; the first leg of a possible congestion entrance from below. Becomes a confirmed floor only when followed by an isolated pivot high.

**Takeaway:** A pivot low is the demand signature hidden inside a down bar.

## Congestion Entrance — Bullish Trend

**What it is:** The transition from an uptrend into a sideways range.

**Price action:** An uptrend prints an isolated pivot high first, then an isolated pivot low. Drawing a horizontal line through the high creates the ceiling; a line through the low creates the floor. Subsequent pivots at the same levels reinforce the boundaries.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The first pivot high signals the trend's thrust is being absorbed; the first pivot low confirms that supply and demand have reached balance.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart money is in the process of rotating from buyer to seller (distribution) or from seller to buyer (re-accumulation). The congestion is the evidence of that rotation.

**Takeaway:** A congestion entrance is the in-print footprint of an insider rotation.

## Congestion Entrance — Bearish Trend

**What it is:** The transition from a downtrend into a sideways range — the mirror of the bullish case.

**Price action:** A downtrend prints an isolated pivot low first, then an isolated pivot high. Ceiling and floor are drawn and reinforced as the range extends.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Identical logic to the bullish case — the first pivot low signals the trend's thrust is absorbed; the pivot high confirms balance.

**Insiders / psychology:** Smart money is rotating position, accumulating or distributing quietly while the public churns.

**Takeaway:** The pattern of pivots is symmetrical regardless of the prior trend direction.

## Breakout From Congestion — Bullish

**What it is:** A decisive close above the ceiling of a congestion zone, with volume validating the move.

**Price action:** A bar (or series of bars) closes clearly above the resistance line — "clear water" above the ceiling, not a marginal tick-over. Pullbacks to test the broken ceiling are normal and expected.

**Volume:** The initial thrust above the ceiling must come on well-above-average, rising volume. Pullbacks to retest the broken ceiling must come on low or falling volume. Once clear, a rising market should continue to be accompanied by rising volume on the thrust bars and lighter volume on the rest bars.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A breakout on heavy volume confirms that genuine effort is being applied to drag the market free of the dense population of trapped traders. A breakout on low volume is the classic anomaly — small effort producing a big result means the market is not actually being driven higher, it is being marked higher.

**Insiders / psychology:** Heavy-volume breakouts are the insiders allowing a real move because their position is built. Low-volume breakouts are the insiders lifting offers briefly to trigger stops on the short side and pull in weak long buyers, before fading the move back into the range.

**Signal:** Confirmed continuation (or trend reversal) of the move away from the range. Unconfirmed = "fake-out" / trap move.

**Takeaway:** A breakout without volume is a trap. Always require the close and the volume together.

## Breakout From Congestion — Bearish

**What it is:** The mirror of the bullish breakout — a decisive close below the floor of a congestion zone.

**Price action:** A bar closes clearly below support. Pullbacks to retest the broken floor are normal.

**Volume:** The break must come on well-above-average, rising volume. Retests of the broken floor must come on low volume. The subsequent downtrend should see rising volume on the down bars and lighter volume on any up bars.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Heavy volume on the break confirms real selling effort. Falling markets require rising volume to be genuine, just as rising markets do.

**Insiders / psychology:** Heavy-volume breakdowns are the insiders distributing into genuine demand or driving price through trapped longs. Low-volume breakdowns are stops raids by the insiders against the weak long side.

**Signal:** Confirmed bearish continuation or reversal; unconfirmed breakdown = bear trap.

**Takeaway:** The same rules of effort and result apply in both directions — falling markets need rising volume to be real.

## The "Clear Water" Rule

**What it is:** The threshold a price must exceed to be considered a breakout rather than a probe.

**Price action:** The closing price of the breaching candle must be visibly and unmistakably beyond the ceiling (or floor). A tick or two over the line does not qualify.

**Volume:** Even with a clean close, the breakout is unconfirmed until volume validates it.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The clean close is the price signal; the heavy volume is the effort confirmation. Both are required.

**Insiders / psychology:** Marginal closes beyond the level are probes — the insiders testing where the resting orders and stops lie.

**Takeaway:** No clear close and heavy volume = no breakout.

## Pullbacks to the Broken Level

**What it is:** The retest of a freshly broken ceiling (in a bullish breakout) or floor (in a bearish breakout).

**Price action:** Price returns to the broken level from the other side and pauses or bounces.

**Volume:** The retest must come on low or falling volume. The hold of the level on light volume proves that the prior trapped population is exhausted.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Low effort into the retest, with price holding the level, is the signature of an exhausted counter-trend force. If the level fails on the retest, the breakout is invalidated and the range has reasserted itself.

**Insiders / psychology:** The retest is the insiders' final opportunity to mop up the remaining trapped traders at breakeven before the real move begins.

**Takeaway:** Always expect a retest of the broken level; use volume to judge whether it will hold.

## The House Analogy — Polarity Reversal

**What it is:** A structural model of how broken resistance becomes support, and broken support becomes resistance, as the market progresses through successive ranges.

**Price action:** Market moves up from a ground floor range, breaks the ceiling, enters the first-floor range. The old ceiling (resistance) is now the floor (support) of the new range. On the way back down, when the market breaks the floor of the first-floor range, that floor becomes the ceiling (resistance) of the level below.

**Volume:** Breakouts of each ceiling/floor follow the same volume rules as any other breakout — heavy on the break, light on the retest.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Each level the market successfully transitions through leaves behind a polarity-flipped zone. The market's own structure provides future support and resistance.

**Insiders / psychology:** The polarity flip is the physical record of the smart money having completed one phase of accumulation/distribution and beginning the next.

**Takeaway:** Every level the market leaves behind is potentially a future level of opposite character — resistance becomes support, and vice versa.

## Natural Stops Around Broken Levels

**What it is:** The use of broken congestion levels as logical stop-loss placement zones.

**Price action:** Once a level is broken and a clear close achieved, the broken ceiling (in a long trade) or broken floor (in a short trade) becomes a visual "floor of protection" against which a stop can be placed in the lower (or upper) part of the old congestion zone.

**Volume:** Any move back to negate the breakout would require a fresh surge of effort — the trader is hidden behind the volume requirement of the invalidation level.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The stop is placed in a location where the only way it is hit is by a genuine volume-driven reversal, not by noise. It is the market's own structure defending the trade.

**Insiders / psychology:** The trader is using the trapped population of the old range as a buffer. Any stop-hunt through the level will reveal itself in volume before destroying the position.

**Takeaway:** Let the market draw your stop lines for you — the old S/R level is the natural place.

## Market Memory and Old Price Zones

**What it is:** The tendency of historical congestion zones to influence price action years later, even when they have been dormant.

**Price action:** When price returns to an old range, it typically pauses, tests, and either reverses or breaks with the same rules as a fresh congestion zone. The deeper and wider the old range, the more significant its influence.

**Volume:** Breakouts from old, deep congestion zones require exceptionally heavy volume to be valid. Their failure on volume is correspondingly significant.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A failure to break a long-standing old resistance level on rising volume suggests an extremely weak market. A successful break of a long-standing old resistance level on heavy volume is highly significant and creates a strong support platform.

**Insiders / psychology:** Old zones are densely populated with the original trapped traders, still waiting for breakeven exits. The market's apparent "memory" is in part the trapped population, in part the self-fulfilling nature of all traders seeing the same chart, and in part the underlying supply/demand character of the price (e.g. an old selling-climax high may now be fair value).

**Takeaway:** Old price zones never die; treat any revisit as a live S/R event, scaled by the depth and width of the original base.

## Time-Frame Scaling of Congestion

**What it is:** The same congestion pattern carries weight in proportion to the timeframe on which it formed.

**Price action:** A 5-minute range affects only the very short term. A daily range affects the medium term. A weekly/monthly range affects the long term. The shape of price action is identical; only the significance scales.

**Volume:** Volume is interpreted on the same relative basis — above-average on the breakout timeframe, regardless of which timeframe that is.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Higher-timeframe congestion dominates lower-timeframe congestion. A break of a daily level will override intra-day S/R noise; a break of a 5-minute level is irrelevant to the daily chart.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart money operates on every timeframe, but their largest positions are built on the higher timeframes. Their footprints are most visible in the deepest ranges.

**Takeaway:** Always trade in the context of multiple timeframes — let the higher timeframe define the significant levels, the lower timeframe define the entry.

## Three Practical Uses of S/R Zones

**What it is:** The three ways a trader applies a defined congestion zone once it is in place.

**Signal:** (1) If a position is already held and a breakout is validated in the position's direction, hold with confidence. (2) If no position is held, a validated breakout is a high-probability entry. (3) If a position is held and a breakout occurs against it, exit — the level has failed and the prior trend assumption is broken.

**Takeaway:** S/R zones serve entry, management, and exit — the three things a trader actually has to do.

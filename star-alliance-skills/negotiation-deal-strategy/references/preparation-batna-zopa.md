# Preparation — BATNA, reservation point, ZOPA, interests

Preparation wins more deals than table skill. The point of this stage is to walk in knowing your floor, their floor, the overlap between them, and the *why* under every stated demand. Do all of this before you open your mouth at the table.

## BATNA — your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement

Your real power is what you can do if this deal dies. A strong BATNA lets you hold firm; a weak one means you need this deal more than they do — know which you have.

- **Name it concretely.** Not "we have options" but "we have a $12k/mo client queued and starting in three weeks." Vague BATNAs give false confidence.
- **Improve it before you negotiate.** Every alternative you can line up (a second buyer, a backup supplier, an in-house option) raises your floor. The best move at the table is often made away from it.
- **Estimate their BATNA too.** What happens to them if they walk? Who else can they buy from, at what cost, with what delay? Their weak BATNA is your leverage; their strong BATNA is your warning.

## Reservation point — the worst term you'll accept

Translate your BATNA into a single walk-away number (or a small set of must-haves). Below the reservation point you are better off walking than agreeing. Fix it in writing *before* the table so the room's pressure can't move it. Then estimate theirs the same way.

## ZOPA — the Zone Of Possible Agreement

The ZOPA is the range between your reservation point and theirs where a deal benefits both.

- If your floor is **$9k** and they'll pay up to **$15k**, the ZOPA is **$9k–$15k** — any deal in that band beats both walk-aways.
- If your floor is **$15k** and they cap at **$10k**, there is **no ZOPA**. Recommend the walk, or change the variables (add issues, change scope) until a ZOPA exists. Do not negotiate against yourself into a losing deal.
- Where you land *inside* the ZOPA is what anchoring and concession strategy decide (see `structuring-and-concessions.md`).

## Interests vs positions

A **position** is the demand on the table ("net-30, 20% off"). An **interest** is the underlying need driving it ("we can't recognize cost before Q3 close"). Positions are scarce and collide; interests are plural and often reconcilable.

- For each stated position, ask **why** until you reach the interest. "Why net-30?" → cash-flow timing → solvable by a milestone schedule that costs you nothing.
- List **your** interests and rank them: must-have, want, nice-to-have. Do the same for them (your best guess, refined at the table).
- Look for **dovetails**: interests where giving them what they value costs you little. Those are the trades that expand the pie.

## The leverage map

Before the table, write a one-page map: both BATNAs, both reservation points, the ZOPA, both interest rankings, the deadline pressure on each side, and any external constraints (budget cycle, regulatory date, a competing bidder). Leverage isn't just price — it's time, alternatives, information, and who needs the deal more.

## The walk-away test

Run it before every negotiation and before every "yes": **is the term on the table better than my BATNA?** If yes, it's worth closing. If no, walk — a bad deal is worse than no deal. The discipline to walk is what makes every other tactic credible.

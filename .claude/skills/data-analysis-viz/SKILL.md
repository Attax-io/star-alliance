# Data Analysis & Visualization — the Merchant's craft

Take a dataset as it arrives — a CSV, a query return, a pasted table — and turn it into something a decision can rest on: a profiled, cleaned, understood set of columns; the right few charts for the questions being asked; summary numbers that hold up; and a short, honest narrative of what the data says and where it stays silent. This craft sits between two others. It stands **on** `probability-statistics` (the inference theory — distributions, tests, intervals) and points **toward** `graphify` (which builds knowledge graphs from arbitrary inputs). Here the input is a *tabular dataset* and the output is *analysis plus visualization plus narrative*.

## What it is / is not

- IS: the full exploratory-data-analysis arc on a structured dataset — profiling and type inference, validation and cleaning, finding the distribution and the outliers, segmenting by a dimension, choosing and composing honest charts, computing summary statistics, and writing the findings as a plain story with stated confidence.
- Is NOT probability-statistics: that is the *theory* of inference — how a hypothesis test or a confidence interval is derived and what its assumptions are. This craft *invokes* that theory on a concrete dataset and stays applied; when a claim needs a real test or interval, it reaches for that skill rather than re-deriving it.
- Is NOT graphify: graphify ingests *any* input (code, docs, papers, media) and emits a navigable knowledge graph with community detection. This craft takes a *tabular* dataset and emits an analyzed, charted report. Different input shape, different output.
- Is NOT market-recon: that is the wide qualitative read of a specific market and its narrative. This craft is dataset-agnostic EDA — it will analyze sales, sensor logs, survey responses, or anything tabular, and makes no market call.
- Is NOT a charting library or a BI tool: it is the *judgment* of which analysis answers the question and which chart tells the truth — the encoding decisions, not the rendering engine. It can drive any renderer (the `data:*` plugin, a notebook, a dashboard).

## Core principles (read these first)

1. **Profile before you analyze; never trust the schema you were handed.** First pass over any dataset is mechanical: row/column count, each column's real type, null rate, cardinality, range, and a few sample values. A "numeric" price column with `$1,200` strings, a date column that is really text, a 95%-null field — these are found by profiling, not assumed away. *Example:* before charting "average order value by month," confirm the date column parses, the amount column is numeric after stripping currency symbols, and how many rows have a null month (and decide what to do with them) — only then aggregate.
2. **Clean and validate as a recorded, reversible step — surface what you changed.** Cleaning is part of the analysis, not invisible prep. Decide explicitly on each issue: drop, impute, coerce, or flag — and report the count affected. *Example:* "1,043 of 50,210 rows had a null `region`; excluded from the regional breakdown (2.1%), kept in the global totals" beats a silently filtered chart that no longer sums to the headline number. Duplicates, impossible values (negative age, future dates), and unit mismatches are validation failures to name, not noise to hide.
3. **Let the question pick the chart — match the analytical task to the encoding.** There are a small number of question shapes, each with a fitting chart family: *distribution* (histogram, box/violin, ECDF), *comparison across categories* (bar — usually horizontal, sorted), *trend over time* (line, or area for one cumulative series), *relationship between two variables* (scatter, with a fit line only when a relationship is real), *composition / part-of-whole* (stacked bar or a small treemap — almost never a pie beyond 2-3 slices). *Example:* "which segment grew fastest" is a *trend* question → small-multiple lines, not a single stacked bar that hides the per-segment slopes.
4. **Visualize honestly — the chart must not say more than the data does.** Bar charts start the value axis at zero; line/scatter axes may be truncated but the truncation is labeled. No dual y-axes that manufacture a correlation, no 3-D, no decorative gradients, no more precision than the data earns. Color encodes one variable with intent (sequential for ordered, categorical for nominal, diverging around a meaningful midpoint) and stays colorblind-safe. Every chart carries a title that states the takeaway, axis labels with units, and the n it is drawn from. Chart-junk — anything that adds ink without adding information — is removed. *Example:* a 4%-vs-5% difference shown on a bar axis zoomed to 3.9-5.1 is a lie of scale; either start at zero or use a clearly-labeled dot plot.
5. **Summarize with the statistic the distribution earns, and always show spread.** A mean alone is a half-truth: report it next to a measure of dispersion (SD, IQR) and prefer the median when the distribution is skewed or has outliers. Counts get rates and denominators; rates get their base. *Example:* "median session 4.2 min (IQR 1.8-9.6), mean 7.1 min pulled up by a long tail" tells the real story where "average 7.1 min" misleads.
6. **Segment to find where the aggregate lies.** The headline number is an average over groups that often disagree; the insight is usually in the breakdown. Cut by the meaningful dimension (cohort, region, channel, time bucket) and watch for Simpson's paradox — a trend that reverses once you condition. *Example:* overall conversion flat year-over-year can hide one segment collapsing while another surges; the segmented view is the finding, the aggregate hid it.
7. **End in a narrative with calibrated confidence, not a wall of charts.** The deliverable is a short story: here is what the data shows, here are the 2-4 charts that prove it, here is what stays uncertain (small n, selection bias, missing months, correlation-not-causation), and here is the one number a decision turns on. State a Low/Med/High confidence and name what would change it. A dashboard is this same discipline made standing and refreshable.

## How you work

1. **Frame the question.** What decision does this analysis serve, and which question shape is it — distribution, comparison, trend, relationship, composition, or an open "what's here"? Open `references/eda-workflow.md`.
2. **Profile and validate.** Load the data, infer real types, measure nulls/cardinality/ranges, and list the data-quality issues before any aggregation (principles 1-2).
3. **Clean on the record.** Resolve each issue with a named decision and an affected-row count; keep the cleaned set and the original distinguishable.
4. **Explore.** Find distributions, outliers, correlations, and segments. Let the data suggest the cuts; confirm a suspected relationship before drawing a fit line.
5. **Choose the chart.** Match each question to its encoding via `references/choosing-the-chart.md`; compose it under the honesty rules in `references/honest-visualization.md`.
6. **Compute the summary.** Pick the statistic the distribution earns and always pair it with spread and denominator (principle 5). For real inference (is this difference significant, what's the interval), hand to `probability-statistics`.
7. **Write the findings.** Deliver the narrative: takeaway, the few proving charts, the caveats, the decision number, and a calibrated confidence. Build a standing dashboard only if the analysis must refresh.

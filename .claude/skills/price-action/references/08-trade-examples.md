# Trade Examples — Continuation & Termination

## Swing Trading Framework

- Top-down approach: start from bigger-picture money flows (e.g., relative performance across indexes and sectors), then narrow to precise patterns that offer clean entries and risk points.
- Position with the statistical tendency behind a pattern, not against it.
- The job is to take money out of one clean swing in the market — usually the next swing.
- Do not enter a position, add to it as it moves against you, and hope.
- Precise entries and active trade management are required; overstaying or trying to squeeze every penny tends to fail because later favorable moves may simply be random noise outside the pattern's predictive horizon.

## Risk and Trade Management Rules

- Every trade enters with a predefined stop-loss that is respected without question.
- The initial risk point also sets the first profit target at 1× risk.
- Take 25–50% of the position at the first target; in rare aggressive cases as little as 20%, in cautious cases up to 50%.
- "Aggressive" does not mean risking more — risk is a consistent percentage of net liquidation value across a trade class. Aggressiveness just means removing less risk and booking less profit early.
- Once a trade is working, tighten stops to reduce open risk. Trade management sometimes means only moving the stop, not executing.
- Pay yourself as the market makes profits available, so the position can survive elevated volatility.
- Long shadows in the direction of the prior swing often mark small climaxes — useful as potential exit or turning references.

## Reading the Setup (Two-Chart Method)

- The setup chart shows only what was visible at entry; the result chart shows the resolution with a heavy vertical line marking where the first chart ended.
- Studying the resolution can completely change the perspective on a pattern at the right edge.
- Spend time considering alternate ways the pattern could have resolved.
- Trade discussions focus on the most important elements only; deeper narratives are possible but not written.

## Emphasis on Pattern Failures

- Failures are common; many traders have tunnel vision and freeze when the unexpected happens.
- Understanding breakdowns is essential to building intuition about the dynamics supporting these trades.
- In your own work, focus more attention on the unexpected and on failed patterns than on perfect successful trades.

## Trend Continuation: Simple Pullback

- Bread-and-butter play; the backbone of a comprehensive program.
- Context from higher time frames matters: a buying climax on the weekly chart conditions what pullback trades are reasonable on the daily.
- Entry is typically a breakout of the pullback high (or breakdown for shorts), often informed by intraday structure (e.g., a small buying climax on the bar before entry).
- A two-legged complex pullback followed by a failed resumption is a warning, not a buy.
- Stop placement controls position size: a wider stop shrinks size, which is prudent in overextended or high-volatility conditions.
- Take partials at 1× risk — large gaps following news increase volatility, so locking in profit protects P&L even if the trend continues.
- A swing trader's job is to take one swing, not ride the full move.

## Trend Continuation: Lower Time Frame Climax Entry

- Different markets trend differently — currencies tend to trend longest, commodities intermediate, equities most prone to mean reversion.
- Rule: do not buy pullbacks that follow a buying climax; fake-outs and false entries are normal in those environments.
- A selling climax on the lower time frame (e.g., 60-minute) inside a daily consolidation flushes weak-hand longs and works off the buying climax.
- After the climax plus two bars of volatility compression, a small breakout is a reasonable entry.
- Stop tight, just under the consolidation — the climax should mark the bottom, so there is no reason to grant additional room.

## Trend Continuation: High and Tight Flag

- Pattern: a strong 1–3 bar thrust followed by consolidation near the extreme of that thrust.
- Consolidation near the highs (failed mean reversion) is strong evidence of underlying buying pressure.
- Be ready for opening gaps beyond the pattern boundaries; they often point to a powerful shift in dynamics.
- Entry: breakout (or breakdown) of the small pattern.
- Expect volatility expansion immediately; if the trade is going to work, it should work quickly — be suspicious of markets that go dull and flat after entry.
- A wider stop may be prudent when volatility and gap risk are high; smaller position size preserves risk.
- After a strong initial thrust fails into another consolidation, a small-range day followed by a lower open is the warning to be out of any remaining long exposure.

## Trend Continuation: Nested Pullback

- A small consolidation that forms inside a drive from a larger or higher time frame pattern.
- Bigger-picture context and motivation come from the larger pattern; this is what distinguishes it from a plain first pullback.
- Entry: same breakout techniques as for any small pullback.
- Useful for managing existing positions even when not strong enough to stand alone as an entry.
- A minor buying climax (parabolic acceleration, free bar, immediate sharp reversal) is the pullback not to buy — existing longs should exit somewhere in that move.
- Momentum divergence on indicators is helpful but not required: the price structure can be clearly climactic even when MACD does not show divergence.

## Trend Continuation: Complex Pullback

- Multiple legs in the pullback; often a higher time frame simple pullback in disguise.
- Moves out of complex pullbacks tend to be cleaner and stronger than moves out of simple pullbacks.
- Use tighter stops on complex pullbacks — failure out of a complex consolidation is more likely to produce a sharp reversal and a complete failure.
- Do not buy into the middle of the pattern; focus on breakouts with anticipated momentum or near potential support within the pattern.
- A press below a parallel trend line drawn across the top of the pattern can flag a very small selling climax and a potential long entry on the next session's strength.
- After a losing first-leg pullback trade, do not abandon the market — a complex pullback is the likely next step.
- Currencies tend to trade better on higher time frames because they do not trade as cleanly as other markets on lower ones.

## Pullback Failure at Previous Swing

- The most conservative target for any pullback is the previous swing point.
- A failure can still result in a profitable trade if the 1× target was reached on the way up to the swing.
- The common error is failing to recognize the failure and continuing to hold into a senseless loss.
- Best pullback entries show strong conviction at the breakout; weak follow-through and a swing that falls short of the prior high is the signal to exit.

## Pullback Failure: Strong Momentum Against

- After a working entry, tighten stops; if the trade turns back up, a complex consolidation is a likely outcome.
- Strong countertrend momentum against the trade means the probabilities have tilted back in favor of continuation — move on to better opportunities.
- The actual loss should be small because of the lowered trailing stop.
- Pattern failure can look catastrophic in retrospect, while in real time the loss is well controlled.

## Pullback Failure: Goes Flat After Entry

- Edge comes from imbalance of buying and selling pressure; a flat market signals equilibrium and the edge is gone.
- Sitting too long in a simple pullback without the anticipated breakdown means scratch the trade.
- Do not overstay by seizing on potential distribution signals (e.g., a Wyckoff upthrust) to justify remaining in a non-working trade.

## Trend Termination: Failure Test

- Countertrend trade near the trend extreme, requiring a distinct change of character to even justify consideration (e.g., a strong sell-off breaking the established pattern of the trend).
- Two valid entry options: the bar that makes a new trend extreme, or the following bar. The trade-off is location versus confirmation — intraday entries give better location but more losses; close-based entries give more confirmation but give up price movement.
- Stop at or just inside the previous trend extreme — slippage is reduced on nearly all exits, and the rare stop-out where the trade is still valid is more than compensated.
- Trade on smaller risk (relative to normal with-trend trades) for two reasons: stops are very tight, often less than a single day's range, and the danger of catastrophic gap failure through the old trend extreme is real.
- Many of these trades will reverse and continue the original trend, making the discipline of taking partials at 1× risk absolutely essential.

## Failure Test Variations

- Following a long consolidation (3–4× the normal length) near the trend extreme, be on guard for failure out of the pattern rather than treating it as a sign of with-trend pressure.
- Following a climax (three pushes, parabolic expansion, multiple free bars), do not try to get in front — wait for the climactic structure, the first reversal off it, and then watch for a failure test at the prior extreme.
- As an entry into higher time frame structure: failure tests on lower time frames can give precise entries into ongoing higher time frame continuation patterns.

## Failure Test Failures

- Failure by consolidation: the trade does not work cleanly; reduce exposure and consider scratching. The first profit target should not require waiting more than three or four bars — if it does, something is wrong.
- Failure with obligatory second entry: if the high of the original bar is exceeded, the short should be stopped out, and a fresh entry on the next bar can more than make up for the loss. Letting the first loss grow destroys the ability to take the second swing.
- Failure at or near the first target: targets are often touched overnight or in premarket, so keep GTC profit-taking orders working outside regular hours and respect the rules.
- Failure by a large adverse gap: a trend-continuation gap against a countertrend position is dangerous. Trading at half normal size is prudent.

## Trading Parabolic Climaxes

- These areas are disorderly and volatile; wide spreads, low liquidity, and rapid movement work against multiple entries.
- Two challenges: recognizing the market is parabolic, and standing apart from the crowd's emotional reaction.
- Stand-alone entries within the climax are usually problematic; the best plans use the climax as setup for other countertrend entries:
  - Failure test entry: wait for a retest of the trend extreme rather than fighting the climax directly.
  - Anti setup: look for an initial sharp countertrend move with a distinct change of character.
- Managing existing positions: tighten the stop to just under the previous day's low; ratchet the stop tighter every day even if no execution occurs.
- "Overbought" alone is dangerous and overemphasized in the literature; strong trends stay extended.
- Countertrend trades initiated in climax patterns are subject to failure by trend continuation. After partial profits and reduced risk, the realistic outcome is often a scratch or small profit rather than the full move.

## The Anti

- A pullback variant distinguished by its location: it comes after a potential trend change and tries to enter a new trend early.
- Setup: a strong countertrend thrust (e.g., a sharp sell-off in an uptrend) followed by a slow, reluctant bounce that fails to make new highs — strong evidence the dominant group has lost control.
- Entry: a breakdown of the previous bar's low (for a sell Anti); a buy Anti mirrors the structure.
- Stop options: just above the setup bounce (near), or above the high of the trend (far). Nothing in between — there is no probability compensation for the intermediate loss.
- Edge comes from trapped traders scrambling to adjust positions into the new trend; if that reaction does not develop, the probabilities tilt back toward balance.
- Asset class tendencies matter: an individual stock is more likely to reverse after a large up day, but currencies do not show that tendency.
- The Anti is, fundamentally, a pullback — what makes it an Anti is its location in the trend and the strong setup thrust that precedes it.

## Anti Failures

- Failure through consolidation: the market goes flat, the trapped-trader reaction does not develop, and the edge is gone. Reduce exposure, tighten stops, or exit.
- Failure near the previous swing: the market cannot get through the conservative target; reduce risk on the bars following the failure. A small consolidation forming at the high of a move back above support is a lower time frame consolidation and a strong warning of higher prices.
- Failure by strong momentum against: full-sized loss is likely; the market can even gap beyond the initial risk on the next open.
- Not every Anti is textbook clean — the setup leg does not always show a strong change of character, and better-looking patterns do not necessarily have higher expectancy.
- Failed patterns can be more powerful than successes; pattern failures often fail through other tradable patterns (e.g., a failed Anti can resolve into a complex pullback).

## Trading at Support and Resistance: Breakouts

- Breakouts are plays for the level breaking; failed breakouts are plays for the level holding.
- A failure test on the trading time frame usually displays a failed breakout pattern on lower time frames.
- Early entry in the base: a failure test of the prior pivot (a classic Wyckoff spring) gives a better trade location than waiting for the actual breakout. The trader at the bottom of the range has many more management options than the trader entering at the breakout point.
- Springs work because the downthrust flushes weak supply; the strongest springs penetrate previous support decisively.
- Stop should respect the limits of the higher time frame pattern when entering on a lower time frame pattern within a base, which usually results in a smaller position size that matches the larger pattern's risk.
- Adding to a position inside the base is fine, but if the added entry fails, exit more than was added to preserve consistency.
- The first pullback following a breakout is usually simple (not complex) — a sign of some urgency in the trend. Trade it with tight stops based on the geometry of the pullback itself, not on the breakout level.
- There is nothing magical about the breakout level: price action is more informative than the market's relation to a specific price.

## Breakout Failures

- At the breakout point: a failure test at the level. Breakouts often pull large groups of traders into overextended markets, so the reversals from failures can be violent.
- At the first consolidation: a failed pullback following the breakout. Initial postbreakout pullbacks are rarely complex, so complex pullback logic is not the first concern.
- Failure in the base: the breakout may never happen. Early entry has better location but lower probability; waiting for the breakout gives higher probability but worse location.
- In every case, the trading plan must address how to manage the losing trade as clearly as where to take profits.

The key universal themes across all these examples are clearly defining risk at entry, taking partial profits at 1× risk as the market makes them available, and managing the remainder of the position against tightening stops so the position can survive the volatility that often follows.

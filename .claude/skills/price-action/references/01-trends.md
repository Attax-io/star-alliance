# Trends — Structure, Pullbacks & Strength

## The Fundamental Pattern

Every trend, on every timeframe, is built from the same three-part structure: an impulse move in one direction, a countertrend retracement, and another impulse in the original direction. Tony Plummer calls this the "price pulse," and Grimes treats it as the foundational building block beneath all other chart patterns.

- **Definition:** A trend unfolds as impulse → retracement → impulse. In an uptrend the labels are A (up) → B (down) → C (up) → D (up).
- **Why it matters:** Markets are buyers and sellers negotiating prices. A trader needing to fill a large order buys a little, waits to avoid moving price, then buys more. The natural rhythm of large-order execution and the natural rhythm of a price discovery process both produce this same shape.
- **How to read it:** Locate the impulsive legs (sharp, directional, often range-expanding) and the corrective leg in between. The corrective leg is shallower and usually lower-volatility than the impulses that bracket it.
- **Rule:** This pattern is fractal — the same shape repeats on every lower timeframe nested within it, all the way down to the tick.

## Fractal Markets (Timeframe Nesting)

Market structure is self-similar across timeframes. Each trend leg is built from smaller impulse/retracement/impulse structures, and the whole pattern can itself be a setup leg for a higher-timeframe trend. Timeframes are an arbitrary but convenient structure imposed on continuous price action.

- **Lower timeframes build the trading timeframe.** Patterns on the trading chart are the emergent result of structures on the charts below it.
- **Higher timeframes provide context and motivation.** The chart above the trading timeframe tends to explain *why* the current move is happening.
- **Practical use:** Trades in the direction of the higher timeframe are more reliable; countertrend trades against the higher timeframe should be filtered out.
- **Rule:** The market is a set of nested structures. Reading only one timeframe produces an incomplete picture. Always check the timeframe above and below before sizing a read.

## Trend Structure & Imbalance

A trend exists when there is an imbalance between buying and selling pressure. The trend ends when a new equilibrium is found at a different price. Reading trend structure means asking whether the imbalance is still intact or whether it is resolving.

- **Definition:** A trend is a persistent directional move driven by a real imbalance of demand over supply (uptrend) or supply over demand (downtrend).
- **Why it matters:** Imbalance = trade. The trend will continue as long as one side keeps overpowering the other, and will end when the other side has absorbed enough flow to find a new clearing price.
- **How to read it:** Look for momentum on the impulse legs, shallow pullbacks held by the dominant side, and absence of sharp countertrend momentum on retracements. When countertrend pressure grows (deeper pullbacks, stronger bounces, momentum extremes against the trend), the imbalance may be resolving.
- **Rule:** Strong trends resolve in two ways — through a gradual narrowing of the impulse/retracement structure, or through a violent climax.

## Impulse & Momentum

The first leg of a trend (AB) is the impulse. In an uptrend it is a sharp advance where demand overwhelms existing supply and clears the liquidity on one side of the book. In a downtrend it is a sharp decline where supply overwhelms demand.

- **How it emerges:** After a period of balance (a range), a sharp range-expansion bar in one direction often signals a new imbalance. The break itself is usually obvious — "not subtle."
- **Indicator confirmation:** A strong impulse drives the MACD fast line to a significant new high (uptrend) or low (downtrend) relative to its recent values. The indicator lags price, so this is confirmation, not prediction.
- **Trading implication (read-only):** As long as each successive impulse is roughly consistent with the prior one, the probabilities favor another impulse after the next pullback. The structural signal that the trend is breaking is sharp countertrend momentum on a pullback — not a normal retracement, but a move that itself looks like a new trend in the opposite direction.
- **Rule:** Trends are driven by impulse moves. The trend is intact if the impulses are clean and the pullbacks do not show the character of a fresh directional move.

## Climaxes & Blow-Offs

A buying climax is one of the most common ways trends end. The pattern looks like extreme strength, but it actually represents the last eager buyers exhausting themselves, after which the market is hollow underneath.

- **Definition:** A blow-off (or parabolic move) is an accelerated, range-expanding move at the end of an extended trend, often closing on the extreme of the bar, where the last willing buyer has bought and the market becomes extremely vulnerable to a sell-off.
- **Why it matters:** Climaxes end trends because there is no natural demand left below the move. Trend-following longs who just bought are now weak-hand longs who will exit en masse as soon as real downside momentum appears, adding fuel to the reversal.
- **How to read it on a chart:**
  - Accelerating rate of trend (parabolic)
  - Bars pulling away from the moving average, with much larger ranges than prior bars
  - Many "free bars" — bars with a low above the upper Keltner channel (or vice versa)
  - Volume spike near the extreme
  - Many bars closing on the extreme (highs in a buying climax, lows in a selling climax)
- **Symmetric downside:** The same pattern works inverted in downtrends — accelerated decline, large bars, free bars below the lower channel, closes on the lows.
- **Important caveats:**
  - Climactic conditions are defined relative to recent history. A breakout from a range can look climactic but usually continues. Look for climax only after extended trends.
  - A climax alone is not a countertrend entry. At best it defines a clear risk point and warns against entering pullbacks in the direction of the climax.
  - Climax lows are often retested. Do not assume the climax low is the absolute low.
- **Rules:**
  - Do not enter pullbacks in the direction of a blow-off.
  - Treat a climax as a warning to lighten, tighten stops, or exit with-trend positions.
  - A climax is confirmed when sharp countertrend momentum emerges. Without that confirmation, the move may simply be very strong and continue.

## Three Pushes

Three pushes is a more common and more subtle climax pattern. It appears as three relatively symmetrical drives to a new high (or low) after a trend has run for some time.

- **How to read it:**
  - Three pushes spaced more closely than the prior swing highs in the trend
  - The pushes are roughly symmetrical in time and price
  - The third push often breaks a short trend line drawn across pushes one and two
- **Why it works:** Each push is itself a complete impulse/retracement/impulse on the lower timeframe, so the pattern is really a small three-legged structure nested inside the larger trend.
- **Rule:** Three pushes is a warning — tighten stops, reduce exposure, do not add risk into the next pullback. As with climax, it does not justify a countertrend position by itself, but it raises the probability that the next pullback will fail.

## Pullbacks: Simple & Complex

A pullback is the countertrend retracement after an impulse. It is also called a consolidation, because its function is to pause and consolidate the energy of the prior trend leg, usually in preparation for another impulse in the same direction.

- **Two types:**
  - **Simple pullback:** One countertrend movement, then the trend resumes. The pullback itself has a single low (in an uptrend) or single high (in a downtrend).
  - **Complex pullback (two-legged pullback):** A simple pullback, a failed resumption of the trend, and then a second leg against the larger trend. Complex pullbacks are themselves a complete impulse/retracement/impulse on the lower timeframe.
- **Rule of alternation (from Elliott):** Trends tend to alternate simple and complex pullbacks. It is unusual to see five trend legs separated by four simple pullbacks; far more common is two simple and two complex among four pullbacks.
- **Why this matters:** Many trend traders enter on pullbacks and stop out if the pullback extends. Complex pullbacks will systematically stop those traders out, because they make new countertrend extremes on the second leg. Any trend strategy must account for the possibility of a complex pullback.

## Measured Move Objective

The standard trend structure is three legs (impulse, retracement, impulse), and the second impulse is often roughly equal in length to the first.

- **Definition:** The measured move objective (MMO) is the projected target for leg CD obtained by adding the length of leg AB to point C.
- **How to use it:**
  - As a rough target for the trend leg that follows a pullback. Be flexible — the market may fall short, hit the level exactly, or overshoot with momentum.
  - In a complex pullback, as a potential termination area for the second leg of the pullback.
- **Caveats:**
  - Treat MMO as a guideline, not a precise rule. Markets do not conform to fixed ratios.
  - MMO is per swing, not per trade. A position held through multiple swings has a fresh MMO for each new leg.
  - The validity of the ratio reflects the normal fluctuation range of the specific market being traded.

## Winning Pullback Characteristics

Some pullbacks produce high-probability continuation entries; others do not. The difference is readable in the structure.

- **Aligned with the higher timeframe.** The best pullbacks are countertrend on the trading timeframe but with-trend on the higher timeframe. A pullback that is itself a countertrend move against the higher timeframe has a much lower probability of continuation.
- **Preceded by strong impulse / momentum.** Trade only pullbacks that follow a real, range-expanding move in the direction of the trend. The MACD should register a significant new high (uptrend) or low (downtrend) on the impulse leg.
- **No momentum divergence into the pullback.** A new price extreme accompanied by a non-new MACD extreme is a warning. The next pullback is a poor entry even if price does continue.
- **Location in trend.** With each successive trend leg, suspicion should grow. It is hard to justify the same risk on the fourth or fifth leg as on the first or second. But trends occasionally run for many more legs than expected — never add to a losing countertrend trade hoping for a runaway.
- **Retracement percentage.** A rule of thumb: expect retracements to terminate near 50% of the prior impulse, but accept anywhere from 25% to 75% as normal. Exact ratios are not useful.
  - **Special case:** In an extremely strong trend there may be no meaningful pullback at all — prices stair-step higher or grind along the upper channel. These trends are real, but the lack of pullback makes entries difficult and the occasional sharp counter-spike makes risk hard to define.
- **Symmetry and lower-timeframe character.** The best pullbacks are periods of lower activity. The lower-timeframe ranges inside the pullback should be smaller, and there should be less conviction in the countertrend direction. The most tradable pullbacks are geometrically clean and consistent in angle and depth.

## Failed Pullback Patterns

Pullback trades fail in three recognizable ways. Reading the failure is as important as reading the entry.

- **Flat pullback.** The market starts a pullback but then drifts into a sideways range — buyers and sellers are in balance, the original edge is gone, and breakouts from this new range are unreliable. Recognize the pattern within a few bars, book a small loss, and move on.
- **Sharp countertrend momentum violation.** The pullback breaks to the wrong side with strong momentum. This is the most dangerous failure because it can travel a long way beyond a geometric stop. Risk on these patterns must be controlled by position size, not by stop placement alone.
  - **Counter-setup:** The same failure can set up excellent entries in the *opposite* direction. When a clean pullback breaks sharply through its boundary with no follow-through, traders trapped on the wrong side of the break are forced to chase, which can fuel a strong move back through the pattern. This is one of the most attractive entry patterns for pullback traders.
- **Failure around the previous pivot.** The next impulse leg emerges from the pullback but stalls at, around, or just beyond the extreme of the setup leg. This is the most common failure and is the natural first profit target.
  - The case where price marginally exceeds the prior pivot before failing is the most treacherous — it traps the most trend traders and tends to produce the sharpest counter-momentum on the reversal.
  - The first profit target for any pullback trade is the previous swing extreme. Taking partial profits there is a defensive habit that often turns a failed trade into a profitable one.

## Dow Theory: Pivots & Trend States

The simplest, most robust trend definition is the Dow Theory construct based on swing pivots.

- **Uptrend:** Successively higher highs (HH) and higher lows (HL). Both legs matter — higher highs without higher lows means buyers are not in control.
- **Downtrend:** Successively lower lows (LL) and lower highs (LH).
- **Three states, not two:** A market can be up, down, or **uncertain**. Forcing a structure onto a sideways market is rarely useful. Good tools respect ambiguity.
- **How to read:** Mark each swing high and swing low. The relationship between successive pivots defines the trend state. A failure of either leg (no new HH in an uptrend, or no new LL in a downtrend) shifts the state to uncertain.

## Trend Change Patterns

Under the Dow Theory structure, trend changes come in two forms.

- **Type 1 — Failed extreme:** The market fails to make a new with-trend extreme. In an uptrend, the new swing high falls short of the prior high. The state becomes uncertain. The change is confirmed only when the prior swing low fails and a lower low prints. Only then is a lower high and lower low in place.
- **Type 2 — Sharp reversal through the prior pivot:** Prices retreat so aggressively from the new extreme that they take out the prior pivot low in a single swing. This is less common and usually indicates trapped trend traders. Confirmation requires:
  - The next rally falls short of the prior high (lower high), and
  - The market then takes out the prior low again.
- **Inverted for downtrends:** The mirror of both patterns applies when a downtrend changes to an uptrend.
- **Complication — complex consolidation:** A complex pullback will, under strict Dow Theory rules, flag a false trend change. This is the most important reason not to trade trend-change signals mechanically. The Dow structure is a labeling system that flags areas of interest, not a complete trade signal in itself.

## Trend Integrity: Length of Swing

In a healthy uptrend, upswings are larger than downswings — both in price and often in time. The reverse holds in a downtrend. Any break of that relationship is a warning.

- **Definition:** Length of swing analysis compares successive with-trend legs to successive countertrend legs in both vertical (price) and horizontal (time) distance.
- **Common warning patterns:**
  - Successive upswings shorter than the prior upswings — trend is losing energy.
  - The first downswing in an uptrend that is larger than the prior upswing (in time or price) — this is the single most important pattern in swing analysis. It usually marks a change in market character, may produce a lower low, and often sets up a shortable bounce.
- **Overlap with classical chart patterns:** Smaller upswings on top of a trend can be read as a rounding top, a double top, a triple top, or a head and shoulders. These are all essentially the same pattern.
- **Rule:** Do not assume the same risk on later legs as on early legs. A trend can run further than anyone expects, but most extended moves will print momentum divergences before resolving.

## Trend Lines: Standard, Parallel, and Micro

Trend lines are tools to define the geometric relationship between swings. They are useful but easily overused.

- **Standard uptrend line:** Drawn between higher lows, beneath prices, sloping upward. Wyckoff called this the demand line.
- **Standard downtrend line:** Drawn between lower highs, above prices, sloping downward. Wyckoff called this the supply line.
- **Hard requirements:**
  - They slope with the trend.
  - They connect real swing points. In a flat market with no definable swings, do not draw trend lines — they are noise, not signal.
- **Nonstandard lines to avoid:**
  - Lines drawn between lows in a downtrend.
  - Lines that ignore large price spikes to fit later data.
  - Best-fit lines through the middle of price when real swings are not present.
  - Lines drawn between highs in an uptrend.
- **Parallel (channel) trend line:**
  - Built by drawing the standard trend line, then anchoring a parallel line to the most extreme point on the opposite side of the trend (pivot highs in an uptrend, pivot lows in a downtrend).
  - The parallel line must not cut prices between the two anchor points of the standard line.
  - **Use:** The upper channel in an uptrend (and lower channel in a downtrend) is a natural area to reduce exposure or book partial profits. A pause at the channel followed by a press through it can be a strong vote of confidence from the dominant side and often produces an accelerated new trend.
- **Micro / one- to three-bar trend lines:**
  - Short-term lines drawn between the prior two bars can project a potential reversal point for the next bar. The line defines a volatility-based inflection, not a swing relationship.
  - A common application inside pullbacks: a small trend line at the boundary of a clean pullback is briefly broken, and the recovery from the break is the actual entry trigger. The break itself is a small lower-timeframe climax; recovery indicates trapped traders forced to chase back in.
  - **Rule:** These short lines should not be overused. They are precise timing tools, not big-picture structure tools.

## Rate of Trend

Markets do not trend at a single constant rate. Trend lines must frequently be redrawn.

- **Shallowing rate:** A break of the trend line often does not end the trend — the trend simply resumes at a shallower slope. A fan of progressively shallower trend lines is common in extended trends.
- **Steepening rate:** A break beyond a parallel channel, or a new catalyst, can produce a steeper trend. This is also where climax and blow-off conditions tend to develop. Trend lines may become nearly vertical before resolving.
- **Rule:** A break of a nearly vertical trend line carries very little information by itself. The structural signal is the rate change, not the line break. In accelerated, near-parabolic trends, be alert for collapse and responsive to early signs of reversal.

## Character of Trend Legs

The most powerful reads in technical analysis are partly subjective. Reading the character of a trend leg is the synthesis of many simple observations into a single judgment about conviction.

- **Why it is hard to formalize:** Two analysts can see the same chart and reach different conclusions, and the same analyst can call it differently on a different day. The subjective element lies in the synthesis, not in any individual input.
- **Inputs that build the read:**
  - **Length of swing on the trading timeframe.** Longer swings usually mean more conviction, but a swing two to three times the average length at the end of a trend can mark exhaustion.
  - **Number of bars in each leg** (usually redundant with slope).
  - **Height of bars near the beginning and end of legs.** Large bars near the end of a swing suggest exhaustion; large bars at the start of a swing suggest strong initiation.
  - **Position of closes within the bar.** Closes below the midpoint in an uptrend indicate lower-timeframe disagreement. Closes at the extreme high for multiple bars in a row are counterintuitively a sign of exhaustion, not strength.
  - **Behavior around prior resistance/support** on the trading timeframe.
  - **Lower-timeframe structure inside each leg.** Trend legs can be one-directional on the lower timeframe or hide ABC structures. Complex consolidations inside a leg can work off an overextended condition and set up another thrust.
  - **Higher-timeframe context.** A trend exhausting into higher-timeframe resistance is more dangerous than one exhausting into open space. A trading-timeframe trend that breaks out at the same point as a higher-timeframe bull flag is a high-probability continuation.
  - **Volume and trading activity.** How easily does the market move? Do unfilled gaps appear on the daily chart? Are price levels being skipped because large orders are clearing the book?
- **Rule for developing the skill:** Beginners have not seen enough data to synthesize these inputs reliably. Stick to objective elements first, take notes on subjective impressions, and let the synthesizing skill develop with exposure. The market feel that experienced traders rely on is built from many small objective observations, and that skill is what makes technical analysis more than a set of mechanical rules.

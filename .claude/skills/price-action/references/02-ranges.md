# Trading Ranges — Structure & Behavior

## Why Trading Ranges Are Harder Than Trends

Trends offer persistent directional movement because of an underlying imbalance of buying or selling pressure, so direction, magnitude, and timing are relatively predictable. Trading ranges are directionless equilibrium zones where price action, exit timing, and exit direction are all much less predictable — they tend to approximate a random walk.

- The defining patterns of a range are the tests of support and resistance at its edges.
- Because action inside the range is more random, much of the conventional wisdom about support and resistance is unreliable.
- The honest approach is to study support and resistance carefully, and to question long-held beliefs about them.

## The Classic Definition of Support and Resistance

Following Edwards and Magee, support is the price level at which enough demand emerges to stop, and possibly turn, a decline; resistance is the inverse. The intuition is sound — as price falls, lower prices attract more willing buyers, and at some point demand absorbs all the supply and the decline ends.

- This theoretical concept is sound, but real markets have so much noise and competing influence that prices frequently stop at random levels.
- Our innate pattern-seeking tendency then makes those random pauses appear meaningful on the chart.

## Potential, Not Magic Lines

Always attach the word *potential* when speaking or thinking about a support or resistance area. Prices are coming down to a potential support area, not to a support area.

- The best that can be said about such an area is that price may have a higher probability of pausing there.
- It is easy to point to these areas in the middle of a chart and claim they "worked," but decision-making under the hard right edge is quite different.
- Stay flexible and open to the message of developing price action; the qualifier *potential* is a discipline that keeps you humble.

## Zones, Not Lines

Support and resistance should be thought of as zones or areas, not exact lines. If drawn on paper, a crayon is a more appropriate tool than a drafting pen.

- It is unrealistic to expect price to stop precisely at a resistance level.
- A far more common outcome is that price stops in front of the level, or that the level is violated.
- If a level is widely watched, large spikes through it followed by renewed selling are common. Read this in the context of classic Wyckoff springs and upthrusts, and factor it into risk and trade management.

## Broken Support Becomes Potential Resistance

The classic rule: support, once violated, becomes resistance, and vice versa. The order-flow logic: traders who sold (or shorted) at the original level are now at breakeven on their losing positions. When price returns to that level, they cover (buy) to limit further loss, turning their earlier selling pressure into buying pressure that can temporarily halt a rally.

- Always attach *potential* to this conversion as well: support, once violated, becomes potential resistance.
- The schematic sequence: resistance holds, then breaks (A), and we can anticipate possible support on the retest.
- A common trap: price penetrates the level just enough to lure trapped traders into covering, which then becomes a catalyst for a strong move when those positions panic out.

## The Dark Secret: Random Lines Look Like Real Levels

Grimes hid the price bars on a chart, drew random horizontal or slanted lines without regard to prices or market structure, then revealed the bars. The lines held cleanly on retests, supported multiple tests, and produced textbook-looking reactions — they were random.

- Almost any random line drawn on a chart will function as believable support or resistance.
- The eye is fooled by the mere presence of a line; you cannot trust subjective evaluation of levels.
- Ask yourself whether the levels you actually use are better than random levels drawn blind. This is a question of profound importance that few traders consider deeply.

## The Random-Level Experiment

A repeatable way to pressure-test your own levels:

- Hide the price bars (set the bar color equal to the background).
- Draw random lines — slanted, horizontal, or even curved. Many will not touch prices; that is fine.
- Restore the bars and examine each touch as if those lines were important levels generated by your usual process.
- A refinement: draw lines on a higher timeframe, then switch to an intraday chart (5- to 30-minute) and look at the touches.
- Ask whether the levels you actually use beat these random ones. The honest answer is usually uncomfortable.

## Expected Value Around Support and Resistance

A common fallacy: in front of a support level, "downside is small and upside is large, so I can buy with a tight stop and a huge reward-to-risk ratio." If the market is a random walk, then with 10-to-1 reward-to-risk, the win rate must be a little over 9% to break even — and any adjustment to the target or stop simply moves the expected value back to zero.

- There is no way to profit in a random walk market. This is axiomatic.
- If you are buying in front of support or shorting in front of resistance, you must have an edge beyond an asymmetric reward-to-risk ratio.
- You must have a reason to expect nonrandom price action off the level.

## Clean vs. Sloppy Tests

Levels rarely hold perfectly clean tests. Allowing for sloppy tests is realistic, but it can also lure you into seeing levels where none exist.

- If a level has been violated many times with sloppy tests in the past, it is reasonable to assume future tests will also be sloppy.
- The market often "knows" where stop orders cluster, and a clean level is frequently targeted: a clean drop of support can trigger stops and reverse violently back above the level.
- Catalysts for an up move are often a clean breakdown that traps sellers, not a clean bounce off untouched support.
- Note also that even on charts with mostly clean tests, other points show sloppier tests; do not expect a single market to behave uniformly at all levels.

## Stop Placement Around Support and Resistance

There is a real trade-off between tight stops (lower per-trade risk, but lower probability, and crowded with other stops) and wider stops (higher probability, but larger and less frequent losses). Tighter stops can permit larger position sizing and better reward-to-risk on a per-share basis, so there is reason to keep stops as tight as reasonably possible. The right answer depends on your style, personality, and the specific market. Always have a stop at the moment the trade is initiated; without one, a single extended adverse move can do serious account damage.

Three candidate stop locations for a long near a support area:

- **B — a tick beyond the most extreme level of the support area.** This is usually the most logical stop: it respects the geometry of the pattern. But the level is visible to everyone, so slippage can be severe. Do not place the stop a few ticks further out, because if the market reaches the low of the bar, it will probably trade through it; a stop "given a little more room" is just more likely to be hit during strong momentum, when slippage is worst. Avoid stops in areas likely to suffer from poor liquidity and adverse order flow.
- **C — a more or less random distance below the low of the range.** There is no good answer for how far is far enough. This is almost the worst possible exit if you wanted to be a buyer at that level, because the market may simply fall through it on momentum. Some markets are more prone to spikes than others, but surprises happen in both directions.
- **A — inside the support level, before the B level is hit.** This will look foreign or even silly, but the A stop virtually eliminates slippage, because you exit long before the cluster of other stops is hit. The aim is to choose a point such that, if price reaches it, it is very likely also to drop below the B level. It is not perfect every time, but many traders find the A stop a valuable addition.

## Special Dangers of Clean Levels

Levels with multiple clean tests are particularly dangerous. Many traders are lulled into building large positions with stops just on the other side of the level. When the level finally fails, the cluster of triggered stops causes extreme slippage, and losses several times the intended risk are common — especially when position size is large because the perceived risk was small.

- Clean levels actually tend to set up good breakout trades beyond the level.
- Expect additional volatility and slippage. Real risk is probably at least twice what you think; in tight intraday stops, losses of 5 to 10 times the intended risk are possible.
- Do not give the trade "a little more room" beyond the level — consider that a clean failure may be the start of a substantial trend run.
- Clean levels are prone to large overnight gap risk, and these tend to be opening gaps that do not reverse.
- It is still possible to trade against these levels, but only with full respect for the additional risk they bring.

## Volume Around Trading Ranges

Grimes has not been able to substantiate the claim that volume and changes in volume are reliably important around accumulation and distribution structures. Closer inspection of many patterns finds that good signals occur as often without volume confirmation as with it, and volume is therefore another degree of freedom that complicates analysis without adding reliable edge. ## Springs and Upthrusts: Classic Accumulation and Distribution

The Wyckoff patterns are the cleanest signals of large-interest activity within a range.

- **Spring** — a quick drop below support, which immediately finds enough demand to push price back above support.
- **Upthrust** — a push above resistance, followed by an immediate failure as supply overwhelms the move.
- Both are essentially fake-outs that run stops just outside support and resistance. They do not require elaborate structures to interpret.

The psychology of a spring: a large buyer wants the best price possible, so they hold back and let price fall as far as it can. Once price falls *through* their target, they realize others are buying aggressively, fear having to chase, and are forced to buy very aggressively, driving price quickly back above support. The opposite psychology produces an upthrust: shorts covering a failed break are buying pressure; buying is buying regardless of motive.

## Price Rejection

The archetypical pattern of a level that holds is *price rejection* — an immediate, sharp, in-time-frame move away from the level. It feels as if price simply does not want to be there.

- Successful tests always have some element of rejection.
- The best tests usually combine an objective, clear level with some form of *overextension* — a large spike into support is more likely to bounce than a slow trend down into support.
- A market trending down into support is not showing rejection; that pressure often presages a significant break.
- The difficult trade is often the best trade. Buying a fast spike into support is hard to execute, but skilled traders are compensated for wide spreads, thin books, and the risk of a fast follow-through by quick profits and good expected value.
- A useful study tool is a very short-term (2- or 3-period) lower-timeframe moving average to smooth the smallest fluctuations and highlight intent. Drop down at least for study, then try to read the level without it.

## Multiple Tests

If a level is going to hold, it should be tested once, twice, or maybe three times, and then a strong, conviction-backed move should follow. Each successive return to the level *weakens* it: if genuine demand exists, why did buyers not chase price higher in the first place, and why is price back here?

- Traditional technical analysis says a level is more valid the more it is tested. Grimes considers this absolutely wrong.
- After two tests, be very suspicious. After three, probabilities begin to favor a break.
- The critical mistake is to keep buying in front of the same support level; confidence from earlier successes tempts traders to increase size, which is a recipe for disaster.
- The required signal on each retest is *renewed price rejection*. If price returns to the level and cannot push away with conviction, the level is failing.
- A market that goes *quiet and dull* at or near a level is often signaling impending failure.
- A useful contrast: weak initial rejection followed by consolidation at the level usually leads to a break, while a violent turn of a short-term moving average at the level signals a hold.

## No Action At All

A third possibility around a potential support or resistance area is *no action at all* — price approaches and trades through the level with no pause or unusual behavior. The anticipated level simply was not there.

- Plan for this possibility in advance. The unprepared trader is taken by surprise, becomes unbalanced, and makes poor decisions.

## Trading Ranges as Functional Structures

Markets alternate between trending periods and trading ranges. In context, every range has one of two structural functions relative to the prior trend: it is either a continuation pattern or a reversal pattern. A trader aware of these functions can position for the next major trend move *within* the range — this is positioning inside a higher-timeframe structure, not trading the noise of the trading-timeframe range itself.

- Most ranges, all else equal, are continuation patterns. The trend is "innocent until proven guilty."
- A higher timeframe often resolves an unreadable trading-timeframe range into a simple pullback within a higher-timeframe trend.
- A higher-timeframe reversal (overextension, momentum divergence, terminating formations on the higher timeframe) can tilt the bias of the trading-timeframe range to the opposite direction.
- Sometimes there is no justifiable directional call at all, and the correct action is to stay flat and wait for clarification.

## Parallel Ranges: The Box

The simplest range trades between roughly parallel prices; volatility is relatively constant across the range. Many of the best trends occur *within* higher-timeframe consolidations, and they typically terminate at the trading-timeframe support and resistance.

- The edges of the range may be violated, but violations are usually short-lived.
- Do not assume every break of a range is a true breakout. Many times the range simply expands and now trades between new, expanded levels.
- A diagonal range — a box with sloping sides — is a very common higher-timeframe pullback, and is in fact the most common pullback pattern. The diagonal range sloping *against* the higher-timeframe trend is the canonical complex pullback. Watch for springs near support in uptrends and upthrusts near the highs in downtrends; both offer exceptional entries into higher-timeframe trends.
- A tight consolidation against one side of the range signals pressure building for a breakout in that direction, and is a classic precursor of the range edge breaking. It is also the structural basis of the cup-and-handle pattern, but reading the simple market structure usually beats any named chart pattern.

## Converging Ranges

A common variation shows successively smaller swings, as if zeroing in on a target; the edges appear to converge and form a triangle. Traditional chartists catalog many named variants (symmetrical, ascending, descending, wedges, pennants), usually with the qualification that "volume is very important in these formations." Grimes treats them all as the same pattern: volatility compression, putting the trader in breakout mode.

- Grimes has not found the traditional rules for triangles (direction, targets) to be reliably effective.
- His one trading rule for triangles: do not fade the first breakout. Breakouts are not consistent enough to build a program on, but you do not want to be caught on the wrong side of a strong move.
- The one exception worth distinguishing is the *ascending triangle* (or its inverse), where price holds successively higher lows into a clearly defined resistance level. This shows strengthening buyer conviction — weakening price rejection from resistance — and usually resolves with a break above that resistance. It is another pattern that sets up good breakout trades.
- A large sharp move in one direction followed by a quick reverse move usually leads to a triangle and a poor trading environment; simply recognizing this avoids many losses.

## Expanding Ranges

Widening triangles and diamond formations show each swing larger than the last, then (in the diamond) contracting again. Grimes' rule is simple: the market is confused, and he does not have to have a position.

- Risk is hard to define and positions are hard to control when volatility is expanding nondirectionally.
- These areas tend to expend stored-up energy; strong moves out of them are uncommon, and the more typical resolution is into a random, directionless, low-volatility range.
- Where possible, avoid these environments or significantly reduce risk in them.

## Summary Discipline

Trading ranges are more complicated and more dangerous than trends. The highly random action inside ranges creates illusions of patterns that do not exist, and the lower liquidity in these zones creates real danger of large adverse spikes that produce much larger-than-expected losses. Yet market structure rests on the alternation of trends and ranges; the largest opportunities and the largest losses both come at the transitions between them, which is why a thorough reading of price action at support and resistance, and of the structural function of any given range, is non-negotiable craft.

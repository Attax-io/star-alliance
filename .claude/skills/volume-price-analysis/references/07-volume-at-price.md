# Volume At Price (VAP)

VAP is a three-dimensional evolution of classic Volume Price Analysis that dissects each volume bar by price level, exposing where buying and selling actually concentrated within a candle's spread. It reveals support and resistance visually as "painted" congestion zones on the chart, and its true power is unlocked only when used as a complement to — never a replacement for — traditional VPA.

## The Core Concept of Volume At Price

**What it is:** A histogram-style indicator that plots the volume traded at each individual price level across a defined lookback period, rather than aggregating volume into a single bar per period.

**Price action:** Price is read exactly as in VPA, but the Y-axis of the volume study is rotated to display volume vertically against price levels, producing a profile or "map" of volume against price.

**Volume:** A single VPA volume bar only shows the total volume associated with the entire price spread of that bar — it hides where within that spread the activity happened. VAP "cuts the bar open" to reveal the concentration of volume at each price point inside it.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The principle of effort vs result is extended one dimension deeper. Within a bar, if the bulk of volume occurred at the bottom of the spread, it is more likely to represent buying interest (buyers stepped in at the lows). If the bulk occurred at the top of the spread, it is more likely to represent selling pressure (sellers dominated at the highs).

**Insiders / psychology:** The indicator exposes where the crowd actually transacted during the bar — whether the "test" of price at a level attracted buyers or sellers. It is a visual record of who was willing to commit capital at which price, which is precisely the information smart money leaves behind.

**Signal:** Concentration zones become visible supply (resistance) and demand (support) pools. A re-visit of a price level where heavy volume once traded becomes a high-stakes test: will buyers defend it again, or will sellers overwhelm it?

**Takeaway:** VAP turns the single-volume-bar blind spot into a readable map of where inside the spread the real decisions were made.

## The Wholesaler Analogy — Testing the Market

**What it is:** The simple mental model Anna uses to introduce VAP — a wholesaler who raises and lowers price to find the level that maximises profit while preserving volume.

**Price action:** The product is offered at a succession of prices; the wholesaler watches what happens to sales at each level.

**Volume:** Sales volume falls as price rises (buyers perceive the product as overpriced) and recovers when price is dropped. The exception is when volume can be maintained at a higher price — in which case profit rises with no loss of volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A higher price producing flat volume is a bullish result for the seller (effort unchanged, result improved). A higher price producing falling volume is a warning that the ceiling has been found.

**Insiders / psychology:** This is exactly how markets behave. Smart money probes price levels to discover where supply appears and where demand absorbs. The VAP profile is the cumulative record of those probes.

**Signal:** Zones where volume spiked at a particular price are the levels at which a crowd of participants is "trapped" or committed — they will defend or abandon those levels on a re-test.

**Takeaway:** Every price on a chart has a story of who bought and who sold there; VAP makes that story visible.

## VAP as an Enhancement to VPA, Not a Replacement

**What it is:** The governing principle of how to combine the two methodologies.

**Price action:** Price action and price patterns are read in the standard VPA manner — bodies, spreads, wicks, closes, and context within the cycle.

**Volume:** Traditional VPA volume bars remain the primary tool for effort-vs-result analysis at the bar level. VAP layers on top to add a price-level dimension.

**Reading (effort vs result):** VPA answers "did this bar's volume confirm this bar's price move?" VAP answers "where inside that bar did the volume concentrate, and what does that concentration zone mean for future tests of that price?" The two questions are complementary, not interchangeable.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart-money footprint is multi-layered. Bar-level effort vs result reveals intent; price-level concentration reveals the battlefield map of where commitments were made.

**Signal:** Use VAP to identify the congestion zones; use VPA to read the bar-by-bar anomalies that confirm or contradict the strength of those zones.

**Takeaway:** VAP shows you where to look; VPA tells you what is happening when you look there. Never trade on VAP alone.

## Reading the Colours in a VAP Bar

**What it is:** Each horizontal slice of the VAP histogram is colour-coded to reflect the up/down candle balance that traded at that price.

**Price action:** No direct price reading — this is metadata about the candles that produced the volume at that level.

**Volume:** The colour split shows whether more up-candles or more down-candles contributed volume to that price slice. The "fulcrum" of the bar tilts blue when up-candles dominated and red when down-candles dominated.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A blue-weighted slice means buying-side effort prevailed at that price; a red-weighted slice means selling-side effort prevailed. The colour profile of a congestion zone reveals whether accumulation (more blue) or distribution (more red) characterises the base.

**Insiders / psychology:** When a congestion zone shows predominantly red volume at the top of the range and blue at the bottom, smart money has been selling into strength and buying the dips — a textbook distribution pattern ahead of a breakdown.

**Signal:** Compare the colour balance of one congestion zone with another to assess which is accumulation and which is distribution.

**Takeaway:** The colour of the VAP slice tells you the character of the activity — building a base (accumulation) or offloading into strength (distribution).

## Congestion Zones as Visualised Support and Resistance

**What it is:** The primary deliverable of VAP — areas of repeated price activity are painted on the chart as horizontal volume clusters.

**Price action:** Whenever the market pauses and trades sideways in a range, the VAP builds a visible band of horizontal volume at that price level. These bands are the "natural barriers" previously identified by VPA through price action alone.

**Volume:** The height and density of the VAP band reflects how much volume was committed at that price. A tall, dense band = many participants committed at that level. A short, thin band = minor interest.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A dense congestion zone represents a battleground where buyers and sellers have met in large numbers. On a re-test, this zone becomes either a launch pad (if buyers defend) or a trap door (if sellers overwhelm). The VAP density is your prior estimate of how much effort will be required to penetrate it.

**Insiders / psychology:** Smart money uses these congestion zones to accumulate or distribute. They prefer ranges precisely because they can build or offload large positions without telegraphing intent. Once the zone is built and the breakout occurs, the same zone becomes the defence line for the trade — and the ambush point for the reversal.

**Signal:** Dense VAP zones ahead of current price = high-probability support/resistance. Thin VAP zones = low-effort barriers that price will likely slice through.

**Takeaway:** VAP turns invisible support and resistance into something you can literally see on the chart.

## The Time Element — Duration vs Volume Concentration

**What it is:** The principle that longer congestion phases produce denser VAP profiles, and that comparing duration to volume density exposes anomalies.

**Price action:** Two congestion zones can sit at different price levels. One may have lasted three days; another may have lasted only a few hours. Both will produce VAP bands — but the bands should be sized in proportion to the time spent there.

**Volume:** A short congestion phase producing a VAP band as dense as a long one is an anomaly. Likewise, a long congestion phase producing a thin VAP band signals disinterest.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Compare the volume density of every congestion zone on the chart against a benchmark (typically the most established zone). Zones with disproportionately high volume for their duration are screaming for attention — they represent intense commitment at that level. Zones with disproportionately low volume for their duration will offer little future defence.

**Insiders / psychology:** When heavy volume appears in a narrow range over short duration, smart money is loading the boat aggressively — they are not patiently accumulating but urgently building a position because they fear missing the next move. When light volume appears over a long period, professional interest is absent; the range is the public trading each other.

**Signal:** Anomalously dense short-duration zones = high-confidence breakout platforms. Anomalously thin long-duration zones = weak barriers that price will breach easily.

**Takeaway:** Always normalise VAP density against the time spent in the zone. The anomalies are where the signals live.

## The Anomaly Test — Effort Without Result (and Result Without Effort)

**What it is:** The classic VPA test, re-expressed through the VAP lens, used to validate or contradict a congestion zone's strength.

**Price action:** Price is in a congestion range and should be doing one of two things — breaking out, or remaining contained. The character of the candles and the closes inside the zone, combined with the VAP profile, reveals which is more likely.

**Volume:** The defining anomaly is high volume (or ultra-high volume) in a narrow range with no upward progress. Effort is enormous; result is zero. This is the same effort-vs-result warning VPA has always flagged, now visualised at the price level.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A narrow range with ultra-high VAP volume and additional high traditional volume bars = the market is weak. Volume of that magnitude should be moving price, and the fact that it is not means the volume is selling into the range, not buying. The market is being distributed into.

**Insiders / psychology:** The smart money is offloading. They have found willing buyers at the top of the range and are using the supply of demand to disgorge stock. The narrow range hides the operation; the VAP spike exposes it.

**Signal:** Ultra-high VAP at the current price level with range-bound action is a high-confidence warning of imminent weakness. Confirmed when traditional VPA volume bars also show heavy volume with no progress, and ideally when a subsequent session opens with a gap or selling drive that confirms the distribution.

**Takeaway:** If the VAP tells you that massive volume is sitting at current prices and the market refuses to rise, the volume is selling. The breakout, when it comes, is likely to the downside.

## Using VAP to Validate Breakouts

**What it is:** The application of VAP to confirm or reject a breakout from a congestion zone.

**Price action:** Price has just broken out of a range to the upside (or downside). The question is whether the breakout is genuine or a fake.

**Volume:** A breakout from a VAP zone that shows a dense, well-established volume platform below the breakout is a structurally strong breakout — the support foundation is wide and deep. A breakout from a thin VAP zone, or one that leaves only a narrow platform behind, is structurally weak.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Combine the breakout candle's VPA signature (volume confirming the breakout) with the VAP profile of the zone being left behind. A breakout on heavy volume from a thick VAP base is a high-confidence continuation. A breakout on heavy volume from a thin VAP base is more vulnerable to reversal because there is little "trapped" support to defend.

**Insiders / psychology:** Genuine breakouts are powered by trapped participants on the wrong side. A dense VAP base means a large crowd is committed long below — they will buy dips to defend their position, providing fuel for the next leg. A thin base means there is no trapped crowd; the breakout is more likely to fail.

**Signal:** Use the density of the VAP zone being broken away from as a confidence multiplier for the breakout trade.

**Takeaway:** The VAP profile left behind is the fuel tank for the breakout. A full tank = the move can run. An empty tank = expect a return.

## Using VAP to Calibrate Stops and Risk

**What it is:** The practical application of VAP density to stop-loss placement and position sizing.

**Price action:** The trade is open and a stop is needed. The natural question is "where do I place it?"

**Volume:** The VAP profile of the congestion zone behind the trade tells you exactly how much volume — and therefore how much defensive interest — sits between current price and the next major level. A dense VAP band acts as a natural magnet for the stop (price rarely penetrates it cleanly on the first attempt); a thin VAP band gives you permission to place a tighter stop because the level is unlikely to hold.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Risk should be calibrated to the VAP structure. If the nearest dense VAP zone is far away, the stop can be wider, but the risk-reward deteriorates. If the nearest dense VAP zone is close, the stop can be tighter and the position sized more aggressively.

**Insiders / psychology:** Smart money knows exactly where the dense VAP zones sit because they helped build them. They will defend those zones to protect their own position — which is why price reacts there. Trading with awareness of those zones is trading with awareness of the other side's defence plan.

**Signal:** Tight stops behind thin VAP zones; wide stops behind dense VAP zones (or use the dense zone as the invalidation point rather than a price-based stop).

**Takeaway:** Let the VAP profile define the risk boundary — it shows you where the real support or resistance actually lives.

## Comparing Congestion Zones as a Benchmark Exercise

**What it is:** The technique of using one well-established VAP zone on the chart as a benchmark against which to measure every other zone.

**Price action:** Identify the longest-duration or highest-volume VAP zone on the chart. Treat it as the reference point.

**Volume:** All other VAP zones are compared in density to this benchmark. A zone matching or exceeding the benchmark density is a major structural level. A zone well below the benchmark density is a minor level.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A short-duration zone matching a long-duration benchmark's density is a high-effort anomaly — intense activity compressed into a small window. A long-duration zone producing only modest volume is a low-effort anomaly — time passed without commitment.

**Insiders / psychology:** The benchmark zone is where the most capital has been committed over the chart's history. It will be the level the market remembers longest. Zones that match it deserve equal respect; zones that fall short will be discarded by price on the first serious test.

**Signal:** Trade with the benchmark in mind. Expect major reactions at benchmark-equivalent zones; expect easy passage through sub-benchmark zones.

**Takeaway:** One zone is the ruler. Use it to grade every other zone on the chart.

## The VAP Workflow — How to Read Any Chart

**What it is:** The integrated sequence Anna recommends for using VAP alongside VPA.

**Price action:** Begin with the price story — where is the trend, where are the congestion zones, where are the obvious support and resistance levels from prior price action.

**Volume:** Layer in the traditional VPA volume bars and read them bar by bar for effort vs result, anomalies, and signals. Then bring in the VAP profile to see the price-level concentration of all that volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** VPA gives the bar-level verdict. VAP gives the price-level map. The two must agree. If VPA says the bar is weak (high volume, no progress) and VAP shows that volume is concentrated at current price, you have a confirmed distribution signal. If VPA says the bar is strong (high volume, big progress) and VAP shows the move is breaking away from a dense platform, you have a confirmed breakout.

**Insiders / psychology:** The complete story requires both dimensions. Bar-level anomalies tell you *that* something is wrong; VAP tells you *where* in the price structure the wrong thing is happening and what it means for the levels ahead.

**Signal:** A complete VAP + VPA read produces four outputs: validation of current price action, depth assessment of support/resistance, confidence rating on breakouts, and stop/risk placement guidance.

**Takeaway:** The order is always VPA first, VAP second. Read the story with VPA, then map the battlefield with VAP. Never invert the sequence.

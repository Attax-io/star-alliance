# Volume Price Analysis — Foundations (History, Why Volume, The Right Price)

This opening section lays the historical and philosophical foundation of Volume Price Analysis (VPA), establishes why volume is the only tool that reveals what the insiders (market makers) are doing, and introduces the price-action vocabulary (spreads and wicks) that volume must then validate. The unifying message is the one Anna repeats throughout: prices move on the basic economic principle of supply and demand, and the only way to read that battle on a chart is to combine price with volume — **volume must confirm price**.

## Nihil Sub Sole — Nothing New in Trading

**What it is:** The founding premise of the book: speculation is as old as the hills and the patterns of human behaviour driving price are constant.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Whatever happens in the markets today has happened before and will happen again. The same price–volume–activity–support–pressure indications appear whether you are forecasting the next half-hour swing or the next two- to three-week trend — "the same elements will be found in a drop of water as in the ocean, and vice versa."

**Takeaway:** Do not search for a new edge; master the time-tested one.

## Is Volume Still Relevant Today?

**What it is:** Confirmation that price-and-volume analysis remains a de facto standard of technical analysis.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Wyckoff's basic tenets — accumulation/distribution and the supremacy of price and volume in determining stock price movement — are still regarded as standards over a century after they were formulated.

**Takeaway:** Volume has not been displaced by anything that has come since.

## Universal Application of Volume Price Analysis

**What it is:** The claim that the methodology works on every market and every timeframe.

**Reading (effort vs result):** VPA can be applied to each and every market. The approach is universal. Once learnt you can apply it to any timeframe (from tick to monthly) and to every instrument — indices, cash equities, commodities, futures, and spot forex. The single proviso is that price and volume must appear on the same chart.

**Takeaway:** One method, many markets, many timeframes.

## Charles Dow — Volume Confirms Trends

**What it is:** Dow's founding principle that volume validates price movement.

**Price action:** Any directional move.

**Volume:** High or rising volume = valid move. Low volume = no conviction; many possible reasons, none confirmed.

**Reading (effort vs result):** When price continues in one direction with supporting volume, this is the signal of the start of a trend. Price alone on low volume is ambiguous; price with high/rising volume is confirmed.

**Insiders / psychology:** Smart money expresses itself through volume. The public sees the price; the tape reveals who is really behind it.

**Takeaway:** A trend is only confirmed when volume agrees with the move.

## The Three Phases of a Bullish Trend (Dow)

**What it is:** Dow's lifecycle model of any sustained up-move.

**Phase 1 — Accumulation:** The starting point of the trend. The smart money quietly builds positions while the public is uninterested.

**Phase 2 — Public Participation:** The longest phase. The technical trend-following stage, where the move broadens out and the crowd arrives.

**Phase 3 — Distribution:** Investors rush in, terrified of missing a golden opportunity. While the public is happily buying, the smart money is doing the exact opposite and selling into that demand, taking its profits.

**Reading (effort vs result):** All of this buying and selling activity is visible through the prism of volume — rising volume on the public's buying during distribution warns the smart money is unloading, not joining.

**Insiders / psychology:** Accumulation is stealth buying on quiet volume; distribution is stealth selling into euphoric, high-volume buying. The same chart pattern means opposite things depending on which phase the market is in.

**Takeaway:** The candle tells you what is happening; the phase tells you who is doing it.

## Richard Wyckoff — Founding Father of Volume and Price Analysis

**What it is:** Wyckoff's role and the practical framework he left behind.

**Insiders / psychology:** Wyckoff believed success required doing your own technical analysis and ignoring the "so-called" experts and the financial media. He believed this approach was an art, not a science.

**Takeaway:** VPA is a discretionary skill. It has never lent itself to automation, and anyone promising a shortcut is mistaken.

## Wyckoff's Three Laws

### The Law of Supply and Demand

**What it is:** Prices move on the basic economic principle of supply and demand.

**Reading (effort vs result):** When demand is greater than supply, prices rise to meet that demand. When supply is greater than demand, prices fall, with the oversupply being absorbed. (The "winter sales" analogy: prices fall and buyers come in to absorb oversupply.)

**Takeaway:** Every price move is the visible tip of a supply-demand imbalance.

### The Law of Cause and Effect

**What it is:** The effect is in direct proportion to the cause.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A small amount of volume activity will only result in a small amount of price action. A large cause produces a large effect; a small cause produces a small effect. (The sea-wave analogy: a large wave rolls the ship violently; a small wave has little or no effect.)

**Takeaway:** Use the volume "cause" of recent bars to size the "effect" of the next trend.

### The Law of Effort vs Result

**What it is:** The price action on the chart must reflect the volume action below.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Effort (volume) and result (consequent price action) should always be in harmony. The analyst examines each price bar with a forensic approach: if the law is maintained, the market is behaving as it should; if there is an anomaly, an alarm bell rings and the cause must be investigated.

**Takeaway:** Any bar where effort and result disagree is the bar worth studying.

## Jesse Livermore and the Ticker Tape

**What it is:** Livermore's career as the archetypal tape reader.

**Price action:** A constant stream of prices and orders.

**Volume:** Buy and sell orders.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The tape was a window on the world of the financial markets. When a stock price behaved in a certain way with the buying and selling, a significant price move was on the way. The tape was talking to him and revealing the innermost secrets of the market.

**Insiders / psychology:** Livermore was the "Boy Plunger" who turned $1,000 into $20,000 in two years and $200,000 by 21, then made ~$3 million shorting the 1907 panic and an estimated ~$100 million shorting the 1929 crash.

**Takeaway:** The tape — then and now — is the same story. Only the delivery mechanism has changed.

## Tape Reading — The Original Definition

**What it is:** Wyckoff's working definition of what a tape reader does.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Tape reading is forecasting from what appears on the tape **now** what is likely to happen in the **future**. It is rapid-fire horse sense. Its object is to determine whether stocks are being accumulated or distributed, marked up or down, or whether they are neglected by the large interests.

**Insiders / psychology:** The tape reader makes deductions from each succeeding transaction — every shift of the market kaleidoscope — and gauges the momentary supply and demand in particular stocks and in the whole market. Like a department-store manager, he notes the general trend but pays special attention to lines where demand is abnormally strong or weak, and acts accordingly.

**Takeaway:** Read the tape like a merchant watching his shelves — fullness, emptiness, and turnover.

## Richard Ney — The Insiders Exposed

**What it is:** Ney's framework of who the market makers are and how they behave.

**Insiders / psychology:** Ney exposed the tacit agreements between regulatory authorities, government, the exchanges, and the banks that allowed specialists to profit from transactions regardless of the actual value of the stock traded. The investor is left out or is an extraneous factor. The actual value of the listed stock is irrelevant — the name of the game is manipulation.

**Takeaway:** The manipulation has not stopped; only the headlines have changed.

## Ney's Eight Laws of the Specialist

**What it is:** The operating rules of a market maker viewed as a merchant with inventory.

1. As merchants, specialists will expect to sell at retail what they have bought at wholesale.
2. The longer the specialists remain in business, the more money they will accumulate to buy stock at wholesale, which they will then want to sell at retail.
3. Expansion of communications media brings more people into the market, tending to increase volatility of stock prices as they increase elements of demand-supply.
4. To buy and sell huge quantities of stock, Exchange members will seek new ways to enhance their sales techniques through the mass media.
5. To employ ever-increasing financial resources, specialists will have to effect price declines of ever-increasing dimensions in order to shake out enough stock.
6. Advances will have to be more dramatic on the upside to attract public interest, in order to distribute the ever-increasing accumulated inventories.
7. The most active stocks will require longer periods of time for their distribution.
8. The economy will be subjected to increasingly dramatic breakdowns causing inflation, unemployment, high interest rates, and shortages of raw materials.

**Takeaway:** Specialists are wholesalers; their actions are inventory-driven, and volume reveals the inventory transfers.

## The Parable of Uncle Joe

**What it is:** A working model of the market maker's playbook in three acts.

**Act 1 — Whisper campaign / Mark-up:** Uncle Joe leaks a "shortage" rumour through a gossipy neighbour. Demand rises; he gradually raises prices while selling into the buying. A few astute customers sell back, but he still has plenty of willing buyers. Eventually the warehouse is almost empty.

**Act 2 — Reverse rumour / Mark-down:** A rumour of a bigger competitor appears. Customers queue to sell. He drops prices quickly, increasing desperation. Panic selling follows; he buys back an enormous volume of widgets. Within weeks the panic is over.

**Act 3 — Re-accumulation:** He starts selling again at the old levels from a full warehouse. He can afford to take it easy; everyone soon forgets how or where the rumours started. Then he wonders if he can do it again.

**Insiders / psychology:** Insiders, specialists, and market makers are the same archetype: they accumulate at wholesale, distribute at retail, and use news (real or invented) to engineer the transitions. Whether a market maker could achieve this alone is irrelevant; they all see the same supply/demand picture and act in concert when their inventories demand it.

**Takeaway:** Every market is manipulated in one way or another — covertly by market makers in equities, openly by central banks in forex. Insider dealing by individuals outside is criminal; insider activity by licensed insiders is the mechanism of the market.

## Market Makers — Wholesalers with a Two-Sided View

**What it is:** Why market makers are uniquely positioned.

**Insiders / psychology:** They are licensed and regulated professional traders whose remit is to fulfil all orders, both buying and selling, and manage their order books (inventory) accordingly. They are usually large international banking organisations. Their unique advantage is the ability to see both sides of the market — the supply and demand — and to set their prices accordingly.

**Takeaway:** Their information will never be made available to you. If you were in their position, you would probably do the same.

## The Only Tool We Have — Volume

**What it is:** The justification for basing analysis on volume despite its imperfections.

**Reading (effort vs result):** In a manipulated market, volume reveals the truth behind the price action. In a pure market, volume reveals the truth behind market sentiment and order flow. Without volume, nothing moves; if it does move without volume agreement, something is wrong and an alarm bell rings.

**Signal:** If futures are rising on strong and rising volume, the price action is being validated and the major operators are buying. If the market is falling and volume is rising, volume is again validating price.

**Takeaway:** Volume is far from perfect, but it is the best tool we have to see "inside" the market.

## Volume in Different Markets

**What it is:** How VPA is adapted to each market type.

**Cash equities (manipulated):** VPA is the ultimate weapon to avoid being suckered in by the market makers.

**Futures (pure):** VPA validates price and reveals the true market sentiment of buyers and sellers; reversals are signalled using volume as the major operators move in and out.

**Spot forex (no true volume):** The proxy is tick volume.

**Takeaway:** The methodology is universal; the data source is adapted to the market.

## Tick Volume as a Proxy in Forex

**What it is:** The substitute for true volume data in spot forex.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Tick volume is the number of price changes per unit of time. It is activity, and that activity can be reflected in price. Tick data is simply changes in price — when the price is changing fast, significant market activity is in evidence. A 233-tick chart that takes a few minutes per bar before a major news release forms each bar in seconds during the release — that is volume.

**Signal:** Various studies over the years have shown that tick data as a proxy for "volume" is 90% representative of the true activity in the market.

**Caveats:** Tick volumes on one platform will differ from another (depending on the broker's data feed, including whether the broker is subscribed directly to the interbank liquidity pool). Quality of data is broker-dependent.

**Takeaway:** Tick volume is imperfect, but you will be considerably more successful using it than not.

## The Auction Analogy — Activity Equals Volume

**What it is:** A worked example of why tick/price-change data represents volume.

**Cold, empty provincial auction:** A single bid at the opening price; the hammer falls. One price change — no interest, no volume.

**Full, busy capital-city auction:** Bidding moves quickly, with floor and phone bidders; the item eventually sells. Several price changes — real interest, real volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The linkage between activity and price is valid. Activity and volume go hand in hand.

**Takeaway:** Use the speed and number of price changes as your proxy for bidder interest.

## All Volume is Relative

**What it is:** The only way volume can be read.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A first-time visitor to an auction cannot judge if activity is average, above average, or below average — no yardstick. A regular visitor judges instantly. Humans can judge relative sizes and heights extremely quickly, and the chart provides an instant picture of the relative volume bars, from ultra-fast tick chart through to long-term investing chart.

**Takeaway:** Always compare each bar's volume to recent bars on the same instrument and timeframe.

## Volume Without Price is Meaningless

**What it is:** The first of three key points about volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Remove the price from the chart and you have only volume bars. Volume on its own reveals interest, but that interest is just that, without the associated price action. Only when volume and price combine do you get the chemical reaction that creates the explosive power of Volume Price Analysis.

**Takeaway:** Volume + price = a complete picture. Either alone is half-blind.

## Time as a Key Component

**What it is:** The second of three key points about volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** An auction that lasts hours instead of minutes indicates subdued interest. In the market, the same amount of activity attempting to leave the pipe in the same amount of time through a reduced aperture produces greater pressure. Time plus restricted price movement equals rising pressure that will eventually resolve.

**Takeaway:** A price bar that takes a long time to form in a small range is storing energy.

## The Wholesaler's Perspective on Buying and Selling

**What it is:** The convention used throughout the book.

**Insiders / psychology:** The whole point of studying volume is to see what the insiders, the specialists, are doing — because whatever they are doing, you want to follow. Buying and selling is always from the wholesaler's perspective, because that is the order flow we ALWAYS want to follow.

**Takeaway:** Always read the tape as a market maker would — buy when they buy, sell when they sell.

## Buying Climax and Selling Climax

**What it is:** The two terminal climaxes of a trend, defined by the wholesaler's role.

**Buying Climax:** A sharp waterfall move lower on a bearish trend, supported by masses of volume. The wholesalers are buying; the retail traders are panic selling. A buying climax is an opportunity for the wholesaler-aligned trader.

**Selling Climax:** Sustained high volume at the top of a bull trend. The wholesalers are selling to retail traders and investors who are buying on the expectation of going "to the moon." A selling climax is a distribution warning.

**Signal:** Both climax types mark a transition between phases of the market cycle.

**Takeaway:** Reverse the public's reading. A "scary" plunge on huge volume is the smart money accumulating. A "thrilling" breakout on huge volume is the smart money distributing.

## The Four Elements of a Price Bar (OHLC)

**What it is:** The components of any bar — open, high, low, close.

**Price action:** Together they describe the journey of price over the period.

**Volume:** Validates the journey.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Each element plays a part in defining the price action within the timeframe, but it is the wicks and the spread that are most revealing of market sentiment when validated with volume.

**Takeaway:** The OHLC is the skeleton; the wicks and spread are the muscle; the volume is the heartbeat.

## Electronic Trading and the Open/Close

**What it is:** How the move to electronic trading has devalued the open and close as signals.

**Price action:** Pre-electronic, gap up or gap down opens were the norm and gave strong signals of breakouts. Today the open of one bar is generally at exactly the same price as the close of the previous bar, which reveals little.

**Volume:** Continuous.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The introduction of Globex in 1992 made virtually every futures contract tradeable 24 hours a day. Cash equities still trade only during RTH (NYSE 9:30am–4:00pm, LSE 8:00am–4:30pm), but the cash-market open is now pre-priced by overnight index futures (ES E-mini from 1997, NQ E-mini from 1999, YM E-mini from 2002). Indices, commodities, and forex all run 24 hours.

**Signal:** A pre-priced open makes the cash open less of a surprise and therefore less informative. This makes volume even more relevant.

**Takeaway:** In the electronic era, the bar-by-bar open is largely redundant. The story is told by what happens inside the bar and by the volume.

## Candlesticks — The Preferred Price Bar

**What it is:** The seven elements of a candle and why they are used.

**Price action:** Open, high, low, close, upper wick, lower wick, spread.

**Volume:** Validates the candle.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The author's apprenticeship was with candlesticks, and the true power of VPA is revealed when using this approach. Bar charts, line charts, and Heikin Ashi are acknowledged alternatives but are not used by the author.

**Takeaway:** Use candlesticks; their anatomy is the natural vocabulary for VPA.

## Price Action as a Sine Wave

**What it is:** The mental model of how a single candle forms.

**Price action:** The market oscillates back and forth as buyers and sellers battle for supremacy during the session. The completed candle captures the full journey — not the path, but the result.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The journey inside the bar is not directly visible; the open, high, low, and close are the only record. Volume shows where the activity clustered and confirms who won.

**Takeaway:** Read each candle as the summary of a battle whose individual blows are hidden in the wicks.

## The Spread — Revealing Session Sentiment

**What it is:** The body of the candle, open-to-close.

**Price action:** A **wide spread** indicates strong market sentiment, bullish or bearish depending on the direction of the close. A **narrow spread** indicates weak sentiment with no strong view either way.

**Volume:** A wide spread on heavy volume is high-conviction; a wide spread on light volume is suspect.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The spread is the session's score-line. The wicks are the turning points within the match.

**Takeaway:** Wide body = conviction; narrow body = indecision.

## The Wicks — Revealing Change in Sentiment

**What it is:** The shadows above and below the body.

**Price action:** If sentiment had remained firm throughout, there would be no wicks at all. A wick is therefore a visible record that sentiment changed during the session.

**Reading (effort vs result):** A wick to the top means buyers tried and were rejected; a wick to the bottom means sellers tried and were absorbed. The wick is the equivalent of a bidding war that hit a ceiling — or a floor.

**Takeaway:** Every wick is a confession of failed effort inside the bar.

## The Lower Wick — Buyers Overwhelm Sellers

**What it is:** A candle whose body has opened, sold off, and closed back at the open (or higher) with a long lower shadow.

**Price action:** The price opened, sellers immediately forced it lower, and buyers were initially overwhelmed. At some point the buyers came back, found the price attractive, and gradually overwhelmed the sellers. The bar closes at or near the open, leaving a long tail below.

**Volume:** To be read with the wick — a lower wick on heavy volume is a far more significant absorption than one on light volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Two key facts: (1) a complete reversal in market sentiment within the session, and (2) the sentiment on the close is bullish, because at the instant of closure the price must have been rising into the close.

**Insiders / psychology:** The selling was absorbed; the smart money used the panic to buy. This is a classic "no demand" reversal in Wyckoff terminology — but as the text stresses, on its own the price action does not signal a trend reversal; volume must complete the picture.

**Signal:** A potential end to a down-move; a possible accumulation signature.

**Takeaway:** A long lower wick is one of the most powerful price actions on any chart once volume validates it.

## The Upper Wick — Sellers Overwhelm Buyers

**What it is:** A candle whose body has opened, rallied, and closed back at the open (or lower) with a long upper shadow.

**Price action:** The price opened, buyers immediately forced it higher and overcame sellers. As the session developed, the buyers began to struggle; the market became resistant to higher prices; sellers gradually regained control. At the high, the buyers ran out of steam and began closing out profits, with waves of selling forcing prices back to the open.

**Volume:** To be read with the wick — an upper wick on heavy volume is far more significant distribution evidence than one on light volume.

**Reading (effort vs result):** Two key facts: (1) a complete reversal in market sentiment, this time from bullish to bearish, and (2) the sentiment on the close is bearish, because at the instant of closure the price must have been falling.

**Insiders / psychology:** The buying was absorbed; the smart money used the rally to sell. This is a classic "no supply" / "upthrust" distribution signature — again, on its own the price action does not signal a trend reversal; volume must complete the picture.

**Signal:** A potential end to an up-move; a possible distribution signature.

**Takeaway:** A long upper wick is an extremely important price pattern that recurs throughout the book.

## Time Frame and the Weight of the Signal

**What it is:** The same wick formation on different timeframes carries different weight.

**Price action:** Identical pattern on a tick chart, 1-min, 5-min, daily, or weekly chart.

**Reading (effort vs result):** The pattern is the same; the weight is not. A lower or upper wick on a daily or weekly chart, accompanied by the correct volume profile, will have a significantly greater effect than the same pattern on a 1- or 5-minute chart.

**Takeaway:** Always qualify the same price pattern with the timeframe and the volume context.

## VPA vs VAP — Two Views of the Same Thing

**What it is:** The distinction between Volume Price Analysis and Volume At Price.

**VPA:** The **linear relationship** between volume and price once the candle has closed. The "big picture" of the volume-price relationship on the outside of the candle.

**VAP:** The **volume profile during the creation of the price bar** — where within the price action the volume was concentrated. The "detail" of the volume profile inside the candle.

**Reading (effort vs result):** VPA is the outside view, VAP is the inside view. Two views of the same event from different perspectives, with one validating the other — a further triangulation of the volume-and-price relationship.

**Takeaway:** Use VPA first, then drill into VAP for confirmation and additional context.

## Price Action Trading (PAT) — Why Volume Completes the Picture

**What it is:** The author's critique of price-action-only methods.

**Reading (effort vs result):** PAT captures all the news, views, and decisions embedded in price, but without volume there is no way to validate that price analysis. Volume gives the bearings; it allows triangulation of the price action and a check on the validity of the analysis.

**Insiders / psychology:** This is what pit traders used to do in person — see a price move, validate it with the order flow in the pit, and act. Most pit traders failed to make the transition to screens because they lost the live volume context. On screen, that context is restored by reading the volume bars.

**Signal:** A price move without volume agreement is an alarm bell. A price move with volume agreement is confirmed.

**Takeaway:** Price tells you **what**. Volume tells you **whether it is real**.

# Deal structuring, pricing, concessions, objections, closing

Once preparation is done (BATNA/ZOPA/interests — see `preparation-batna-zopa.md`), this is the build: how the deal is shaped, how you open, how you give ground, how you handle pushback, and how you close. Plan all of it before the table.

## Deal structuring

A price is one variable; a *structure* is the whole shape. Most value is created in the structure, not the headline number.

- **Issues, not just price.** List every variable: price, payment terms, scope, timeline, exclusivity, renewal, termination, warranties, support, volume, risk allocation. Each is a trade lever.
- **Risk allocation.** Decide who bears which risk (delivery delay, scope creep, non-payment, market change) and price it. Often the cheapest deal moves risk to whoever can bear it most cheaply.
- **Payment structure.** Lump sum, milestones, retainer, deferred, contingent, equity. The *shape* of payment can satisfy a cash-flow interest without touching the total — a free trade.
- **Contingent terms.** When the two sides disagree on a future fact (will volume hit X?), bridge it with a contingency ("price drops if volume exceeds X") instead of arguing the forecast.

## Pricing strategy

- **Anchor from value and comparables, not cost.** Price what it's worth to them, justified by comparables and scope — not your cost-plus.
- **Give a range with a reason, then a point.** "Comparable engagements run $38k–$45k; given the scope we're at $44k." The reason makes the anchor stick.
- **Bundle and unbundle deliberately.** Bundle to hide line-item haggling; unbundle to show value and create tradeable issues.

## Anchoring

- **Anchor first when you have solid information**; the first credible number frames the range and pulls the midpoint your way.
- **Always justify the anchor** — a bare number gets dismissed, a reasoned one drags. Lead with the rationale, then the figure.
- **Re-anchor against an extreme open.** If they open absurdly, don't counter near their number (that legitimizes it). State your own basis and reset the frame, then proceed from there.

## The concession ladder

Plan your concessions before the table; never improvise them.

- **Decreasing increments.** $44k → $42k → $41k → $40.5k. Shrinking steps signal you're approaching the floor; large late jumps invite more pushing.
- **Never free, always traded.** Every concession buys something: "I can move on timeline *if* you move on scope." Free concessions train them to keep asking.
- **Stop visibly at the floor.** Make the last move small and firm so the floor reads as real. Hold your reservation point.
- **Track the exchange.** Keep a running tally of what each side has given; use it to frame fairness ("we've moved three times, you've moved once").

## Multi-issue & multi-party trades

- **Score issues two ways:** how much *you* care (1–5) and how much *they* care (1–5). Trade what's cheap-to-you / dear-to-them for what's dear-to-you / cheap-to-them. That's where joint value comes from.
- **Expand before you split.** Add issues to the table before fighting over price alone; more issues means more room for mutually-good trades.
- **Multi-party:** map each party's BATNA, reservation point, and interest *separately*. Watch for coalitions, side-deals, and parties who don't actually speak for the others. Sequence private conversations before the joint table when it helps.

## Objection handling

- **An objection is a named gap**, not a wall — it tells you where their interest and your offer diverge.
- **Isolate the real one:** "If we solved that, would we have a deal?" surfaces whether it's the true blocker or a tactic.
- **Answer by reframing or trading**, not by reflex discount. "Too expensive" → "compared to what?" often reveals a timing or scope issue solved without dropping the total.
- **Test against their BATNA** to tell a genuine blocker from a bargaining lever.

## Closing

- **Close on convergence, not a new fight.** When open issues are inside the ZOPA, summarize the agreed shape, name the last gap, and propose the bridge.
- **Summary-close:** "So we have X, Y, Z — shall we paper it?" Get the verbal yes on the shape before drafting.
- **Watch the nibble.** A last-minute "and throw in…" gets a reciprocal ask, never a free yes.
- **Then stop.** Hand the agreed shape to the Guild Master for the decision and to `legal-drafting` / `contract-review` for binding language. This skill recommends the close; it does not sign or commit.

# The Magic Doji — Long-Legged, Gravestone, Dragonfly, Tri-Star

## Doji (General Definition)

- **Type:** Indecision / trend-change · single candle line
- **Structure:** Opening and closing prices are the same (perfect doji). The line has no real body. Some flexibility is allowed: if open and close are within a few ticks of each other (e.g., a 1/4 cent in grains, a few thirty-seconds in bonds), the line can still be viewed as a doji.
- **Recognition:** Open ≈ Close. A "near-doji" is a session where open and close are very close but not exact. Whether to treat it as a doji is subjective and depends on context. If recent action has included a series of very small real bodies, a near-doji is not significant; if the market is at an important market junction, at the mature part of a bull or bear move, or other technical signals are sending out alerts, a near-doji is treated as a doji.
- **Psychology:** Represents indecision, uncertainty, or vacillation. An uptrend requires the conviction of buyers to sustain a rally; a doji after an extended rally or in overbought conditions means the scaffolding of buyer support may give way. A doji reflects a balance between buying and selling forces, so a falling market may continue its descent under its own weight even when participants are ambivalent.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A distinct trend-change signal. The likelihood of a reversal increases if subsequent candlesticks confirm the doji's reversal potential. Doji sessions are important only in markets where doji are not common. The doji is such a significant reversal indicator that the entire chapter is devoted to its manifestations.
- **Nison's notes:** A doji can be a significant warning, and it is better to attend to a false warning than to ignore a real one. To ignore a doji, with all its inherent implications, could be dangerous. If many doji appear on a particular chart, a new doji in that market should not be viewed as a meaningful development. Candlestick analysis usually should not use intra-day charts of less than 30 minutes (except for very active markets such as bond and S&P futures), because below 30 minutes many of the candlestick lines become doji or near-doji. The doji has already appeared as a component of the doji star (Chapter 5) and the harami cross (Chapter 6).

## Doji at Tops

- **Type:** Bearish reversal · single candle line (doji)
- **Structure:** A doji following a long white candlestick in an uptrend. The doji can be away from, or within, the prior candle.
- **Recognition:** Doji in an uptrend, especially after a long white candlestick, an extended rally, or in overbought conditions. A long-legged doji (a doji with one or two very long shadows) is a sign of a market top.
- **Psychology:** Indecision, uncertainty, or vacillation by buyers will not maintain an uptrend. The buyer conviction required to sustain a rally is absent. With ambivalent market participants, a falling market can fall due to its own weight.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Doji are valued for their ability to call market tops. The likelihood of reversal increases if subsequent candlesticks confirm. The first sign of peaking can be a doji; a later warning (e.g., another doji) can be confirmed by a hanging man and then a long black candlestick. Confirmation of a doji top can come with the next month's long black candlestick session. There may be less need for confirmation of a top reversal via a doji than for a bottom reversal — doji need more confirmation to signal a bottom than they do a top. A doji that forms in the middle of a trading band is unimportant.
- **Nison's notes:** Based on experience, doji tend to lose reversal potential in downtrends because a balanced market can continue to fall. Once prices break to the downside, doji that develop may not be reversals — only a double bottom of doji can mark a trend reversal (albeit temporary). The more conservative the trading style, the more important it is to wait for verification. Waiting for trend-reversal corroboration reduces risk but can also reduce reward, because by the time the reversal is substantiated, profit potential may be reduced.

## Doji After a Long White Candlestick

- **Type:** Bearish reversal · two-line pattern (long white + doji)
- **Structure:** A long white candlestick (buyers in control) followed by a doji (buyers have lost control). Often the next day produces a black candlestick that completes an evening doji star formation.
- **Recognition:** A doji immediately following a long white candlestick, especially after a prolonged uptrend. A series of three long white candlesticks ending in a doji that also forms a harami cross is a strong stress signal that the prior uptrend might be over.
- **Psychology:** The buyers in control the prior day (as proven by the long white candlestick) lost control the next day. The rally is short-circuited.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Confirmed by the next day's black candlestick, which also completes an evening doji star. An intra-day spike higher the next day can be negated when the market sells off sharply toward the close, confirming the original view that the prior uptrend is about over. A subsequent congestion phase can produce a pattern resembling an evening star (still presaging a top even if the star portion does not gap away from the prior long white real body).
- **Nison's notes:** This is one of the strengths of candlestick charting — a signal not obtainable with Western technical-analysis techniques, where a session with identical open and close carries no forecasting implications. To the Japanese, such a session, especially at the heels of a sharp advance, is a critical reversal sign. Longs should take protective measures (liquidate some longs, move up protective stops, and/or sell calls); the prior strong uptrend negates short selling at the moment of the doji. Provides an "ideal" hammer opportunity on the next session if a price decline begins.

## Long-Legged Doji

- **Type:** Bearish reversal (especially at tops) · single candle line
- **Structure:** A doji with one or two very long shadows. The market pushes strongly higher then sharply lower (or vice versa), then closes at or very near the opening price. Long upper and lower shadows clearly reflect irresolution.
- **Recognition:** Doji line with very long upper and/or lower shadows. Often a sign of a market top. A group of small-range candlestick days (a series of doji or near-doji days) after a rally is an unhealthy sign indicating a tired market.
- **Psychology:** Throughout the session the market vacillated strongly. The market has "lost its sense of direction" — neither buyers nor sellers prevail. The doji (and a series of small-range candles around it) reflects tired markets; in a rally one likes to see the buyers in control.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A confluence of technical factors provides forceful clues that highs are at hand (e.g., long-legged doji + harami + tweezers top). Long upper shadows seen shortly afterward confirm the resistance set by the long-legged doji.
- **Nison's notes:** Especially important doji at tops. A group of small-range candlestick days at a major top can be jokingly called the "falling chandelier formation." Even if the opening and closing are not exactly the same, they can be close enough to be considered a doji day.

## Rickshaw Man

- **Type:** Bearish reversal (variant of long-legged doji) · single candle line
- **Structure:** A long-legged doji in which the opening and closing prices are in the center of the session's range. Small real body (or no real body) with very long upper and lower shadows.
- **Recognition:** Long upper and lower shadows with opening and closing prices in the center of the range. A small-enough real body to consider the line a doji qualifies.
- **Psychology:** Represents a market that has "lost its sense of direction" — extreme indecision. Two rickshaw-man lines in succession are a major danger sign after a rally.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Acts as a resistance area. Two doji in succession (e.g., a rickshaw man followed by a doji a few hours later) can become a significant resistance area; the first signals the uptrend could be reversing, the second provides more proof.
- **Nison's notes:** A specific subtype of long-legged doji defined by the central position of the open/close in the range. If the real body is small enough to consider it a doji, the resistance implication still holds.

## High-Wave Line (referenced)

- **Type:** Reversal · single candle line (non-doji variant of the long-legged/range-indecision family)
- **Structure:** A non-doji session with a very long upper and/or lower shadow and a small real body. A group of high-wave candlesticks forms a reversal formation.
- **Recognition:** Very long upper or lower shadow with a small real body (the line is not a doji, distinguishing it from the long-legged doji and rickshaw man).
- **Psychology:** Represents a candlestick that has "lost its sense of direction." Very long upper or lower shadows reflect irresolution/indecision.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A group of high-wave candlesticks is a reversal formation.
- **Nison's notes:** Concept is parallel to the doji family — the same long-shadow psychology applies whether the line is a doji (long-legged doji / rickshaw man) or a near-doji (high-wave line).

## Gravestone Doji

- **Type:** Bearish reversal · single candle line
- **Structure:** Opening and closing prices are at the low of the day. The market opens on the low, rallies (preferably to a new high for the move), then plummets to the day's lows. Long upper shadow; the long shadow plus a very minor lower shadow still qualifies (a very minor lower shadow does not void it as a gravestone).
- **Recognition:** Open = Close at session low, with a long upper shadow.
- **Psychology:** A new high is touched; it is the bulls' chance to propel prices, but they fail. By the close, prices have pulled back to near the daily low. Represents the "graves of those bulls or bears who have died defending their territory" (a military analogy common to Japanese technical terms). Trouble occurs for the longs as prices plummet to the day's lows.
- **Signal & confirmation:** Confirmed by the breaking of the uptrend support line, after which the bears take over. Confirms heavy supply at a prior resistance level (e.g., one established by a prior stalled pattern). The longer the upper shadow and the higher the price level, the more bearish the implications. The gravestone marks the end of the battle for the bulls.
- **Nison's notes:** Forte is calling tops, but it can sometimes be found at market bottoms. Visually resembles a shooting star; the gravestone doji, at tops, is a specific version of a shooting star. Because the gravestone doji is a doji it has no real body, while the shooting star has a small real body — making the gravestone doji more bearish than a shooting star.

## Doji as Support and Resistance

- **Type:** Support / resistance zone derived from a doji
- **Structure:** A doji at a significant top or bottom, or its shadows/real-body location, marks a level that becomes support or resistance in subsequent price action. The lower shadow of a doji week can become a support area; a doji star top can become a resistance level. Two doji in succession can become a significant resistance area together.
- **Recognition:** A doji at a significant top or bottom, with subsequent price action testing the doji's level.
- **Psychology:** The doji marks a battleground between buyers and sellers; that level retains meaning for future price action.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A doji in one time frame can give a clue that the previous uptrend could be reversing; another doji occurring later gives more proof. A doji's real body being small enough to be considered a doji preserves the resistance implication.
- **Nison's notes:** Even when a real body is only small enough to consider the line a doji (not an exact doji), the support/resistance implication holds.

## Tri-Star

- **Type:** Reversal (top or bottom) · three-line pattern
- **Structure:** Three doji lines. The middle doji is a doji star. The most important aspect is that the lines should be three doji (or near-doji). Tri-star top and tri-star bottom variants exist.
- **Recognition:** Three consecutive doji (or near-doji) lines. The latter two doji can also form a tweezer top. A variation (two doji followed by a third small-real-body candlestick) is recognized as a tri-star variation.
- **Psychology:** Sustained indecision across three sessions — neither bulls nor bears in control. A portentous sign after a large advance.
- **Signal & confirmation:** A very significant reversal pattern. Three doji (or two doji plus a small-real-body third candle) marks a turn, even when not ideal.
- **Nison's notes:** Very rare, but very significant. Nison has yet to see an ideal tri-star as depicted in the canonical example, but the pattern is significant even in its variations. The pattern is discussed in the doji chapter (rather than the stars chapter) because the most important aspect is that the lines should be three doji (or near-doji).

## General Rules and Observations (Non-Pattern Notes)

### Significance of the Doji
- The doji is a distinct trend-change signal, significant enough to warrant its own chapter.
- The doji has already been seen as a component of other patterns: doji star (Chapter 5) and harami cross (Chapter 6).
- Doji sessions are important only in markets where there are not many doji.

### Treatment of Near-Doji
- A perfect doji has the same opening and closing price, with flexibility allowed to within a few ticks.
- Whether to treat a near-doji as a doji is subjective and depends on context:
  - Series of very small real bodies in recent action → near-doji is not significant.
  - Important market junction, mature part of a bull or bear move, or other technical signals sending out alerts → near-doji is treated as a doji.
- Philosophy: a doji can be a significant warning, and it is better to attend to a false warning than to ignore a real one.

### Confirmation and Trading Style
- The likelihood of a reversal increases if subsequent candlesticks confirm the doji's reversal potential.
- Doji need more confirmation to signal a bottom than they do a top — there may be less need for confirmation of a top reversal via a doji than for a bottom reversal.
- The more conservative the trading style, the more important it is to wait for verification of a trend change.
- Waiting for corroboration is a trade-off between risk and reward: less risk, but potentially less reward, because by the time the reversal is substantiated, profit potential may be reduced.

### Tops vs. Bottoms Asymmetry
- Doji are especially valued for calling market tops, particularly after a long white candlestick in an uptrend.
- Doji lose reversal potential in downtrends because a doji reflects a balance between buying and selling forces, and a falling market can fall due to its own weight. An uptrend should reverse under such conditions, but a falling market may continue its descent.
- Once prices break to the downside, subsequent doji are often not reversals; only a double bottom of doji can mark a trend reversal (even if temporary).

### Time Frame and Market Activity
- Candlestick analysis usually should not use intra-day charts of less than 30 minutes, except for very active markets such as bond and S&P futures, because below 30 minutes many of the candlestick lines become doji or near-doji.

### Related Patterns Referenced (mentioned, not defined in the excerpt)
- Doji star (Chapter 5) — doji as a star component.
- Harami cross (Chapter 6) — harami where the second candle is a doji; can accompany a long-legged doji at a top.
- Hanging man — used as bearish confirmation after a doji at a top.
- Evening doji star — a long white candlestick, a doji, and a black candlestick; the doji version of the evening star. Confirms a top after a doji following a long white candlestick.
- Evening star — referenced as a pattern that arose in congestion after a harami-cross doji, presaging a top even when not ideal.
- Tweezer top — formed by two doji (or two candles with matching highs) at a top; the latter two doji of a tri-star variation can also form a tweezer top.
- Bullish hammer — referenced as a stop to a price decline.
- Hammerlike line — a line whose lower shadow is not long enough and whose real body is not small enough to be a true hammer.

### Confluence of Technical Factors
- A single doji is meaningful; a confluence of technical factors is more forceful. Examples in the excerpt: long-legged doji + harami + tweezers top at a peak; gravestone doji confirming heavy supply previously established by a stalled pattern; two doji in succession becoming a significant resistance area.

### Historical / Cultural Context
- Many Japanese technical terms are based on military analogies. The gravestone doji's name reflects this: it represents the graves of those bulls or bears who have died defending their territory.
- To the Japanese, very long upper or lower shadows represent a candlestick that has, as they say, "lost its sense of direction."

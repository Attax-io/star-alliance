# Methodology, Performance Ranking & Glossary

## Caveat: Perfect Trades

Every statistic in the book is computed as a perfect trade: buy at the breakout price, sell at the ultimate high (the highest high before a 20% decline), or short at breakout and cover at the ultimate low. No real trader can match this. Use the figures as comparative tools between patterns, not as expected outcomes.

## Sample and Coverage

Statistics are based on approximately 500 stocks from mid-1991 to mid-1996 plus roughly 500 additional stocks from 1999 to mid-2004 (with others in between), not all covering the full 1999–2004 span.

## Bull and Bear Markets

- Bull market: every date outside the S&P 500 bear market.
- Bear market: the S&P 500 decline from March 24, 2000 to October 10, 2002.

## General Statistics Tracked per Pattern

For each pattern, in each breakout direction, in each market type, the book reports: number of formations, count and performance of reversals versus continuations, average rise or decline, the count of rises or declines over 45%, change after the trend ends, busted-pattern performance, S&P 500 change over the same window, days to ultimate high or low, formation end to breakout, and price-range context (yearly position).

## Breakout: Definition and Per-Pattern Locations

A breakout occurs when price closes outside the trend-line boundary, or above/below the pattern's high or low. Performance measures use the intraday low (upward breakouts) or intraday high (downward breakouts) of the breakout day.

- Broadening patterns (all types), Diamond Bottoms/Tops, Flags, High and Tight Flags, Pennants, Rectangles, Triangles, Wedges, Bump-and-Run Reversal Bottoms, Bump-and-Run Reversal Tops: close outside the trend-line boundary.
- Cup with Handle: close above the right rim or handle trend line.
- Inverted Cup with Handle: close below the right rim.
- Double Bottoms: close above the highest high between the two valleys.
- Double Tops: close below the lowest low between the two peaks.
- Head-and-Shoulders Bottoms (and Complex): close above a down-sloping neckline, or above the high between head and right shoulder when the neckline slopes up.
- Head-and-Shoulders Tops (and Complex): close below an up-sloping neckline, or below the low between head and right shoulder when the neckline slopes down.
- Horn Bottoms: close above the highest high in the pattern. Horn Tops: close below the lowest low.
- Island Reversals and Long Islands: after the second gap.
- Measured Move Up and Down: not applicable.
- Pipe Bottoms: close above the highest high. Pipe Tops: close below the lowest low.
- Rounding Bottoms: close above the right lip. Rounding Tops: close below the right lip (or above the highest high in the pattern).
- Scallops, Ascending: close below the lowest low or above the highest high. Scallops, Ascending and Inverted: close above the highest high. Scallops, Descending: close below the lowest low for down breakouts; close above the right scallop lip for up breakouts. Scallops, Descending and Inverted: close below the lowest low.
- Three Falling Peaks: close below the lowest low. Three Rising Valleys: close above the highest high.
- Triple Bottoms: close above the highest high. Triple Tops: close below the lowest low.

## Reversal vs. Continuation (Consolidation)

- Reversal: price enters and exits the pattern from the same side.
- Continuation (synonym: consolidation): price exits in the same direction it entered; for example, enters from the bottom and exits through the top. Overshoot and undershoot at the start may be ignored when determining entry direction.

## Countertrend Pattern

A pattern with an upward breakout in a bear market, or a downward breakout in a bull market — breakout direction opposes the prevailing market trend.

## Failure Rate and Break-Even Failure

- Failure rate: the share of patterns that fail to rise or decline a chosen amount (5%, 10%, 15%, etc.) after breakout. Patterns that break out in the unexpected direction are not counted as failures.
- Break-even failure rate: the percentage of patterns that fail to rise or decline more than 5% after breakout, on the assumption that 5% covers typical trading costs.
- Some patterns (Gaps, Measured Moves Up and Down, Dead-Cat Bounces) carry N/A values for several statistics because their construction prevents a direct comparison.

## Average Rise or Decline (ARD)

The per-stock rise from the breakout price to the ultimate high, or the decline from breakout to the ultimate low, then averaged across the sample.

## Change After Trend Ends

After price reaches the ultimate high or low, this measures the move to a new ultimate low or high, respectively. It gauges what typically follows the run.

## Busted Pattern Performance

A busted pattern is one that reaches the ultimate high or low within 5% of the breakout price. Busted performance measures the move in the opposite direction from that point.

## Rises or Declines Over 45%

A simple frequency count of how often a pattern's run exceeds 45% from breakout to ultimate high or low.

## S&P 500 Change

The average change in the S&P 500 index from the date of the pattern's breakout to the date of the ultimate high or low, used as a benchmark against the stock's performance.

## Days to Ultimate High or Low

The average number of days from the breakout date to the date of the ultimate high or low. The book's frequency distribution of days uses 7-day buckets (0–7, 8–14, etc.), expressed as a percentage of all patterns in the same breakout direction and market type.

## Formation End to Breakout

The average delay between the end of the pattern and the actual breakout.

## Yearly Price Range Effect (Yearly Position)

Starting the day before breakout, the highest high and lowest low over the prior 12 months define the yearly price range. The range is split into thirds, and the breakout price is classified as Low (L), Center (C), or High (H) within that range. Performance is then reported for patterns whose breakout falls in each third. The book documents that breakout location within the yearly range affects subsequent performance, so position within the yearly range is a usable filter.

## Throwbacks and Pullbacks

- Throwback: after an upward breakout, prices decline to or come very close to the breakout price (or the formation trend line) within 30 days, with visible white space between the hooking action and the breakout price.
- Pullback: the same idea after a downward breakout — prices return to or come very close to the breakout price or trend line within 30 days, with white space separating the pullback from the breakout.

The 30-day window and the white-space rule prevent ordinary near-breakout clustering from being misread as a true throwback or pullback. Throwback/pullback occurrence rates across the patterns commonly run between roughly 40% and 70%, and patterns with throwbacks/pullbacks versus those without are reported separately in average rise/decline.

## Breakout Day Gaps

A breakout day gap (usually a breakaway gap with high volume) is a gap that occurs on the breakout day. The book reports the average rise/decline for patterns with and without a breakout day gap, and the average gap size for those that have one. For trend-line breakouts, performance is computed using the close the day before the gap; for patterns that use the highest high or lowest low as the breakout price, that high or low substitutes for the prior close.

## Volume Trend and Volume Shape

- Volume trend: the slope of a linear regression line fit to volume from pattern start to end. A rising trend means volume generally increases over the formation; a falling trend means it recedes.
- Volume shape: the overall pattern of volume (domed, U-shaped, flat, rising, falling, or random). The last four are grouped as "random" because they occur infrequently. For short patterns, if no shape is visible, look left and right for a larger enclosing shape. For large patterns, use the most recent shape if multiple are present.

## Breakout Volume and Volume Sides

- Breakout volume: the breakout day's volume compared to the average volume over the prior 30 days, excluding the breakout day. Heavy breakout volume and light breakout volume performance are reported separately.
- Heavy left/right volume performance: compares volume around peaks or valleys using the 2 days before through 2 days after each peak or valley.

## Pattern Size: Tall/Short and Narrow/Wide

- Tall vs. short: pattern height is the difference between the highest high and lowest low, divided by the breakout price. Patterns above the median are tall; at or below the median are short.
- Narrow vs. wide: the horizontal time measure from pattern start to end. Patterns shorter than the median length are narrow; longer than the median are wide.
- The book reports combined performance for short-and-narrow, short-and-wide, tall-and-wide, and tall-and-narrow subsets.

## Partial Rise, Partial Decline, and Internal Versions

- Partial decline: after prices touch a top trend line of a valid pattern, prices decline but do not reach the lower trend line, form a distinct minor low, and then usually break out upward immediately.
- Partial rise: the mirror image after a touch of the lower trend line — prices rise but do not reach the upper trend line, form a distinct minor high, and then usually break out downward.
- Internal partial decline and internal partial rise: the same shape, but the breakout does not occur and the pattern continues developing — two consecutive trend-line touches on the same side with a minor low (decline) or minor high (rise) between them, the minor point not coming close to the opposite trend line.
- Partial decline failure and partial rise failure: the partial move forms but the eventual breakout is in the wrong direction.

## Premature Breakouts

A premature breakout is a close outside the formation boundary that is quickly reversed back inside. It cannot be distinguished from a valid breakout in real time. The second edition counts the premature breakout as the actual breakout when computing statistics; the first edition ignored them.

## Overshoot and Undershoot

- Overshoot: prices jump up a few days just before entering the pattern.
- Undershoot: prices drop a few days just before the pattern starts. Sometimes also called overshoot in casual usage. Ignored when determining the trend start.

## Trend Start, Trend High, and Trend Low

- Trend start: starting at the formation start and moving back in time, find the highest high before prices close 20% or more below it (upward-leading case) or the lowest low before prices close 20% or more above it (downward-leading case). Overshoot and undershoot just before the pattern are ignored.
- Trend high and trend low (flags and pennants only): the minor high or low that begins or ends the relevant price trend. For flags and pennants, performance is measured to the trend high or low, not the ultimate high or low — a key exception to the standard measurement rule.

## Ultimate High and Ultimate Low

- Ultimate high: the highest high before prices decline by 20% or more, measured from that high to a close, or a close below the formation low. If the stock has not yet suffered a 20% decline by the data cutoff, the highest high to that point is used.
- Ultimate low: the lowest low before a minimum 20% rise, measured from that low to a close, or a close above the formation high. If the stock has not yet seen a 20% rise by cutoff, the lowest low to that point is used.

## Measure Rule and Price Target

The measure rule varies by pattern but is usually the pattern height added to the breakout price (upward breakouts) or subtracted from it (downward breakouts), producing a predicted price target. Each chapter's Results Snapshot reports the percentage of patterns that meet or exceed the target.

## Lead-in Height, Dual Bumps, Handle Measures

- Lead-in height (bump-and-run reversal): the height from the trend line to the highest high (or lowest low) in the first quarter of the pattern.
- Dual bumps: in a bump-and-run reversal, prices bump up (or down) and return to the trend line two or more times.
- Handle performance: rise or decline to the ultimate high or low from handles shorter or longer than the median handle length in a cup-with-handle. The handle is measured from the right cup lip to the breakout.
- Handle retrace (inverted cup with handle): the rise from the right cup low to the handle high, expressed as a percentage of the cup height from the right cup to the highest high in the pattern.

## Volume and the Linear Regression Method

The volume trend uses a linear regression on volume from the pattern's start to end (not to the breakout). The slope of the fitted line determines whether volume is rising or falling over the course of the formation.

## Performance Ranking

For each market type and breakout direction, the book ranks patterns by summing three component ranks — the rank of the average rise or decline, the rank of the break-even failure rate, and the rank of the change after the trend ends — then sorting the sums and renumbering. A rank of 1 is best. Ties share a number with no gap in the sequence (for example, 1, 2, 2, 3, not 1, 2, 2, 4). Patterns with N/A in any of the three components are listed in the ranking table but receive no overall rank.

### General Principles from the Rankings

- High and Tight Flags lead the up-breakout rankings in both bull and bear markets, with a very low break-even failure rate and a strong average rise.
- In bull markets, the strongest down-breakout performers are the classic reversal patterns: Head-and-Shoulders Tops (and their complex versions), Diamond Bottoms on a downward breakout, and Eve-and-Eve Double Tops.
- In bear markets, the strongest down-breakout performers include the Inverted Cup with Handle, Pipe Tops, and the Bump-and-Run Reversal Tops, with Diamond patterns strong in both directions.
- For up breakouts in bull markets, the top tier includes Pipe Bottoms, Scallops Ascending and Inverted, Three Rising Valleys, Rounding Bottoms, and a band of triangles and rectangle/double-bottom formations, suggesting that established prior trends and clean boundaries are common to leaders.
- Countertrend breakouts (up breakouts in bears, down breakouts in bulls) carry materially smaller average rises or declines than trend-aligned breakouts.
- Patterns with N/A in any of the three ranking components (Gaps, Measured Moves Up and Down, Dead-Cat Bounces) cannot receive a defensible overall rank, so they appear in the table but are excluded from ordinal comparison.

## Trend Duration Terms

- Short-term trend: up to 3 months.
- Intermediate-term trend: 3 to 6 months.
- Long-term trend: over 6 months.

## Trader Style Terms

- Position trader: holds positions for days, weeks, or longer, following a longer-term trend.
- Swing trader: holds positions for days, trying to capture moves from peak to valley or valley to peak.

## Headline Numbers Worth Remembering

- Break-even threshold: 5% move from breakout is assumed to cover trading costs.
- Throwback/pullback window: 30 days from breakout.
- Volume comparison window: prior 30 days, excluding the breakout day.
- Volume peak/valley window: 2 days before through 2 days after each peak or valley.
- Ultimate-high/ultimate-low threshold: 20% reversal from the extreme.
- Days-to-ultimate buckets: 7 days each, expressed as a percentage of patterns in the same breakout direction and market.
- Yearly price range lookback: 12 months ending the day before breakout.
- Trend start threshold: 20% close move in the prior trend.

## Median and Average

- Median: the middle value in a sorted list, with half above and half below. If no middle value exists, the average of the two closest values is used.
- Average: sum of scores divided by the number of scores.

## Qualifying

A pattern qualifies for inclusion in the statistics only if it meets the required breakout direction (up or down) and market type (bull or bear) being analyzed.

## Premature-Breakout Handling

Because a premature breakout cannot be distinguished from a valid one as it happens, the second edition treats it as the actual breakout. The first edition ignored premature breakouts, so earlier-edition statistics are not directly comparable to the second edition's.

## Closing Note on Usage

The book's statistics are best used to compare one pattern against another, not to predict the outcome of any single trade. Trade direction, market regime, pattern height and length, volume trend and shape, breakout location within the yearly price range, the presence of a throwback or pullback, and the existence of a breakout day gap all modify the headline averages and should be weighed before acting on any pattern.
